Framing

Why the US, Why Policy

The Room Without the Floor

Part of the project's country-parity set: each country is read on two axes — the organizations on the ground (Org Study) and the system around them (Policy Study). The United States is the companion to three of this project's largest org studies — Backline, MusiCares, and To Write Love on Her Arms — so the question here is what the jurisdiction around those organizations does, or fails to do.

Where Japan reached for labour law, Germany for social insurance, the UK for a universal NHS floor, and the EU for coordination, the United States has reached for charity and the market. It has the project's thinnest statutory floor and, not coincidentally, its largest and most sophisticated charitable room.

That makes the US the cleanest test of this project's central thesis — a rich room cannot fully replace a missing floor — because here the room is as good as it gets and the floor is as thin as it gets. And in 2026 the floor got thinner: the enhanced ACA subsidies that had widened coverage since 2021 expired on 1 January 2026, raising subsidized premiums by an average of 114%.2

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · The Thin Floor

No Floor, by Design

The United States has no universal health system. Coverage is a patchwork — employer-sponsored insurance, the individual ACA marketplace, and the public Medicare and Medicaid programs. The overall uninsured rate was about 8.0% in 2024 (roughly 11% among working-age adults), but that understates the exposure of music workers: Future of Music Coalition surveys repeatedly found musicians uninsured at far higher rates — about 53% of musician respondents in 2013, 33% in 2010.16

For an independent worker, the route to coverage is the ACA marketplace, where income-based premium tax credits lower the cost. The base credit is permanent, but the enhanced subsidies layered on from 2021 lapsed on 1 January 2026: average subsidized net premiums rose about 114% (~$1,016/yr), and 2026 enrollment fell to 23.1 million — the largest single-year drop since the marketplaces launched.2 Medicaid, covering low-income adults to 138% of the poverty line, has been adopted by 40 states plus DC (about 20.4 million in the expansion group), but the 2025 reconciliation law added work requirements that narrow it.3

The deeper problem is classification. Most working musicians are paid as independent contractors (Form 1099-NEC), not employees — and that single status removes them from the institutions that carry welfare in the US: employer health insurance, unemployment insurance, and workers' compensation all attach to employment, not to contractors, who also owe the full self-employment tax. The IRS decides status by a three-part common-law test — behavioral control, financial control, type of relationship — with "no magic number" of decisive factors.4

Classification law then varies by state. California's AB5 (2019) codified the strict ABC test (Labor Code §2775), but the follow-up AB2257 (2020) carved most musicians back out: §2780 returns recording artists, composers, and musicians and vocalists on sound recordings — and most single-engagement live performances — to the looser Borello test, with exceptions for large venues. Federally, the Department of Labor's contractor rule was rewritten in 2024, partly suspended in 2025, and is being rewritten again under a 2026 proposed rule — so even the federal floor is unstable.5

Section 02 · What the State Funds

Output, Not Welfare

What the state does fund is arts output, not worker welfare. The National Endowment for the Arts (est. 1965) makes grants to nonprofit arts organizations and to state and regional arts agencies; by statute it generally cannot fund individuals beyond honorific fellowships.

01 The NEA survived — barely — and funds output anyway 2025–26

The FY2026 appropriation is $207 million — but only after a turbulent year: the FY2026 budget request proposed eliminating the agency (May 2025), more than 1,600 grants were terminated, and Congress ultimately restored it at flat funding (signed into law January 2026). Survived, disrupted, politically fragile — and an output-funder either way; none of this money is a welfare or mental-health line for workers.7

02 The unions reach a minority 2024–26

The American Federation of Musicians provides no general health insurance to most members — coverage exists only through local funds tied to employer contributions on union gigs, which most freelance work never triggers. Its pension (AFM-EPF) was officially "critical and declining" and survived only through a $1.53 billion federal bailout (the American Rescue Plan's Special Financial Assistance, paid August 2024); an earlier application to cut benefits was withdrawn, never enacted.8

SAG-AFTRA covers recording artists and singers, but its Health Plan requires about $28,090 in covered union earnings (2026) — a threshold most working musicians never reach — and the joint AFM & SAG-AFTRA IP Rights Distribution Fund pays modest royalties, not benefits.9

The net effect: public money flows to arts institutions, union welfare reaches a minority, and the welfare of the individual music worker is left to private charity.

Section 03 · The Charitable Substitute

The Largest Room in the Study

Into that gap steps the largest and most mature charitable room for musician welfare anywhere in this project — the three org studies' subjects plus a long-standing fourth fund.

01 MusiCares — scale and a mental-health track

The Recording Academy's charity (est. 1989) runs preventive, emergency, and recovery programs, with a dedicated mental-health and addiction-recovery track (referrals, treatment funding, free weekly support groups, the backstage Safe Harbor Room). Its scale is real: $35.7 million and 140,000+ services during COVID relief (2020–2022), and $15 million+ after the 2025 Los Angeles wildfires; FY2024 shows about $14M revenue and $32.7M net assets.10

02 Backline — the connector

Founded 2019, Backline is the connector / navigator model — 1:1 case management, a 24/7 mental-health line (B-LINE), and support groups that route music workers to vetted providers; $3.5 million+ invested and 84,000+ people supported, built precisely because the ordinary system leaves a three-plus-month provider waitlist.11

03 TWLOHA — music roots, finite reserves

To Write Love on Her Arms (founded 2006) is a general mental-health nonprofit with music-scene roots — a treatment-and-recovery scholarship and crisis-awareness model rather than a musician-only fund — and it ran three consecutive annual deficits (2022–2024), drawing net assets from $2.79M down to $1.64M.12

04 Sweet Relief — explicitly discretionary

Sweet Relief (1994) gives direct aid that is explicitly discretionary: candidacy "depends on the availability of funds and the number of eligible applicants," and the fund spent net assets down to about $0.88 million in FY2025.13

This room is generous, sophisticated, and nationally significant. It is also, on the evidence of its own filings, discretionary, application-based, criteria-gated, and finite — MusiCares paused intake in May 2020 when demand outran supply; two of the four ran their reserves down. It patches a structural hole; it is not an entitlement.

Section 04 · The Mental-Health Layer

A Burden Measured in Convenience Samples

The burden the room responds to is well documented in convenience samples and thinly documented in anything stronger. The landmark US figure comes from the MIRA Survey of Musicians (2018) — the Music Industry Research Association with Princeton's Survey Research Center and MusiCares — which found about 50% of roughly 1,227 musicians had felt down, depressed, or hopeless (versus under 25% of general adults) and 11.8% had had thoughts of self-harm or being better off dead (versus 3.4%). But it was a non-probability convenience sample, recruited through networks, not a representative survey.14

The peer-reviewed work points the same way. Newman et al. (2021), in the Journal of Psychiatric Research, found among 508 touring professionals 50% at high risk for clinical depression and 39.4% reporting high suicidality (volunteer, international); Berg, King et al. (2022) found among 317 popular musicians who had used mental-health services in Texas over 36% with moderate-to-severe depression — a distinct, smaller, clinic-recruited study, not the MIRA national survey. A 2025 scoping review (Kegelaers & Gibney, 89 studies) confirms a literature dominated by convenience samples.15

What is missing is decisive. A targeted search returned no probability-sample prevalence study of US musicians' mental health; the nearest near-misses are MIRA (largest US sample, non-probability), the THRIV touring study (peer-reviewed but volunteer and international), and the Danish "When Music Speaks" project (most systematically recruited, non-US). A "Van Dyke" study sometimes attached to this topic could not be verified and is not cited here — absence is not non-existence (Rule 4), but a figure with no traceable source is not a figure.15 The burden is real and consistently large across every sample; the US still lacks the representative study that would let anyone state a prevalence with confidence.

Part B — Appraisal

Section 05 · Reading the Model

A Room Cannot Be a Floor

What the US gets right is the room

No country in this project has built a charitable infrastructure for musician welfare as large, as specialized, or as fast-moving. MusiCares can move tens of millions in a disaster; Backline has turned the navigator problem — which provider, how soon — into a 24/7 service; TWLOHA built a national mental-health brand out of the music scene. For a Taiwan NPO this is the most useful catalogue of what a room can be anywhere in the study, and three of these are already this project's org studies.1011

However, the room cannot do what a floor does

Every strength of the US charitable layer is shadowed by the same limit: it is discretionary where a floor is universal, application-based where a floor is automatic, and finite where a floor is funded by law. The structural exclusions are upstream of the charity — 1099 status removes musicians from unemployment insurance and workers' comp before any charity is asked; the ACA is the only health route for most, and in 2026 it became more expensive for millions just as the room was being asked to absorb the difference.42 Charity is, by design, the layer that activates after someone has already fallen; a floor is the layer that stops the fall.

Read the evidence with two cautions

(1) The headline numbers are convenience samples — MIRA and the clinic studies describe the people who answered, not US musicians as a population; the direction is robust across every sample, the magnitude is not a measured prevalence.1415 (2) The policy facts here are fast-moving — the ACA subsidy lapse, the NEA's near-elimination-and-restoration, and the unsettled federal contractor rule are all 2025–2026 events that could move again; this report reads the system as of June 2026.75

Verdict

The United States is the project's clearest room-without-a-floor — the most generous charitable infrastructure for musician welfare in the study, sitting on its thinnest statutory floor, a floor that in 2026 got thinner still. The lesson is not that the room is wrong; the rooms here are excellent. It is that a room, however well built, cannot substitute for the floor it is standing on.

Part C — For This Project

Section 06 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 A rich room is not a floor — complement public coverage, don't replace it

The US proves a well-funded charitable layer can coexist with millions uninsured. Taiwan's advantage is that it already has the floor the US lacks — 全民健康保険 (健保) is near-universal. The strategic implication is the opposite of the US: design the NPO as a complement to public coverage (the music-specific care and navigation 健保 doesn't provide), never a substitute — and defend 健保's universality as the asset that lets a small NPO stay small.

3.2 Classification is the hinge

US musicians' exclusion flows almost entirely from 1099 independent-contractor status. Taiwan's equivalent fight is extending 勞保 / 職災保険 and a stable working status to project-based performers. Track classification as the policy prize, and position the NPO as the evidence-generator and bridge service while that question is unresolved — the gap the US room fills, but on a better foundation.

3.3 Coverage you cannot reach is not coverage

US union health plans exist, but the ~$28,090 earnings gate means most musicians never qualify. When the NPO assesses any Taiwan benefit — public or private — verify reach, not just existence: who actually qualifies, and how many real working musicians clear the bar.

3.4 Build the connector first — highest leverage, lowest capital

Backline's navigator model — a 24/7 line, a triage call, a warm referral to a vetted provider within days rather than months — is the part of the US room most transferable to Taiwan and the cheapest to start. It needs a curated provider network and a phone, not an endowment.

3.5 Generate the evidence — even the US doesn't have it

The world's largest music market still lacks a probability-sample prevalence study of musicians' mental health. A credible Taiwan survey would not be catching up — it would be a genuine contribution, and the citation a Taiwan NPO could use with 文化部 and 衛福部.

Limitations

  1. The headline mental-health figures are convenience samples (MIRA, clinic studies); they establish a consistently large burden, not a representative prevalence.
  2. Several load-bearing facts are 2025–2026 events (ACA subsidy lapse, NEA elimination-and-restoration, the federal contractor rule) and may move again; the report reads the system as of June 2026.
  3. State variation in classification and Medicaid is summarized through the clearest cases (California; expansion status), not surveyed state by state.
  4. Charity scale figures are partly org-reported (annual reports, press) alongside IRS 990 data; where a 990 line was unavailable (e.g., Backline), the figure is labeled org-reported.
  5. The California statutory text was read via a mirror citing the official leginfo URL after the official page returned JS-rendered/empty on direct fetch; the citation is to the official section.
  6. No direct contact with any agency, union, or charity; no primary interviews.
