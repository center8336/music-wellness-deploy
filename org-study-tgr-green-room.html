THE GREEN ROOM

Org Study · Est. 2020

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

Germany's MiM-Verband is an advocacy trade body, and the Künstlersozialkasse is a statutory rights floor. TGR The Green Room is neither — it is the direct-care, founder-built, micro-budget "room." One classical-pianist-turned-psychologist, a roster of part-time artist-practitioners, and a single room in Cologne, delivering donation-based psychological counseling and coaching plus body-based workshops to performing artists, on roughly €80,000 a year. It holds no endowment, runs no helpline, and bills no insurance. For a Taiwan music mental-health NPO it answers a different question from the big funds: not "what does the ceiling look like?" but "what is the smallest credible shape a care service can take — and what breaks when it is built around one person?"

01 — Profile

A care centre built by an artist, for artists5

TGR The Green Room is a non-profit social enterprise founded in October 2020 in Cologne, Germany, by classical-pianist-turned-psychologist Heather O'Donnell, to support the psychosocial and somatic health of performing artists — especially musicians, dancers and theatre-makers. It describes itself as "a space for reflection, support and reorientation — created by artists for artists."16

A note on the name. "The Green Room" is one of the most common names in the performing-arts and wellness worlds, so identification matters. The organization profiled here is TGR The Green Room gUG of Cologne (domain thegreenroomforartists.de, register HRB 103802, founder Heather O'Donnell) — the entity the Orgs Map lists at row #108. It is not Backline's "Green Room" peer-support program (USA; a separate Orgs Map row), nor "The Green Room Coaching", nor the US "Green Room Counseling / Psychology" practices that share the name.5

On the founder. Heather O'Donnell is an American classical pianist who built a substantial concert and new-music career — premieres of works by Berio, Tenney, Finnissy and Rzewski — before transitioning to psychology after career-disrupting injuries. She holds an M.Sc. in (Health & Prevention) Psychology and is an Artistic-Systemic Therapist accredited by the DGSF (the German Society for Systemic Therapy); she is TGR's founding and managing director. The dependence of the whole organization on this single founder-practitioner is the central fact of its risk profile (§6).85

02 — The distinctive model

Practitioner-built, donation-based, scoped to care not crisis

Three structural choices define TGR and separate it from both the advocacy bodies and the larger endowed funds in this series.

CHOICE 01

Practitioner-built, not institution-built

TGR was created by a working psychologist who is also a working artist, and every counselor, mentor and teacher on its roster "combines well-founded psychological knowledge with lived experience in the performing arts." Counseling is "trauma-sensitive, artist-centred", delivered by people who have themselves performed — its explicit differentiator.17

CHOICE 02

Donation-based access, with a means-tested top-up

TGR's core service is explicitly "Donation-Based Psychological Counseling & Coaching for Artists and Cultural Workers" — neither free-at-point-of-use nor a fixed fee nor insurance-billed. On top of it, the FAQ confirms a means-based financial-support application ("Is there financial support…? Yes. Please fill out this form"), reviewed case by case. Access is designed around affordability for a precarious clientele.21

CHOICE 03

Scoped to prevention & transition, not crisis

TGR sits at the preventive and rehabilitative layer. O'Donnell frames the gap it fills as "preventive measures as well as rehabilitative supports" that artists "sorely miss"; the catalogue is built around performance anxiety, burnout, creative blocks, injury recovery, career transitions and resilience — not acute psychiatric crisis. A counseling-and-coaching centre, not an emergency or inpatient service.4

03 — Funding & finances

A micro-budget, grant-and-donation operation6

TGR is, financially, very small — and transparent about being so. There is no endowment, no statutory funding stream, and no audited accounts on the website. Two independent sources triangulate the picture.

What the two sources show

  • The EU mapping: €80k budget, grant-funded

    The CultureForHealth database (an EU-cofunded project) records TGR's budget as €80,000 and its sources of funding as "MRI, BAMF, Goethe-Institut, a.o." — a mix of foundation and institutional grants tied largely to its residency and refugee-artist work, not a single core grant.6

  • The register: a €1,000 non-profit shell, tiny turnover

    The German commercial register (via Northdata) shows a gUG with €1,000 share capital and a revenue figure in the low single-digit thousands of euros for the latest filed year (the register's automated chart plots 2024 revenue at roughly €2k–€14k against a near-zero 2023; an annual report for the year ending 31 Dec 2024 was published 18 May 2026). That is consistent with a micro-entity funded largely through restricted project grants and donations rather than commercial turnover. ⚠️ The register revenue number is an automated approximation, not a stated figure.5

What is solid: this is an €80k-budget-class organization running on grants (BAMF, Goethe-Institut, MRI), donations, and donation-based service income, behind a €1,000-capitalised non-profit shell. The exact composition is ⚠️ not fully public — TGR publishes no audited statements, and the register's headline number is an approximation.56

A concrete mutual-aid event

One sourced funding episode shows TGR also acting as a small hub of practical support: in the final months of jazz pianist Richie Beirach (1947–2026), TGR "helped coordinate support" and ran a GoFundMe campaign that raised €35,264 for his care and living expenses; Beirach died on 26 January 2026. This is distinct from its core counseling services — one-off solidarity fundraising — but it illustrates the organization functioning as a node of community support.2

The method signature — repeatedly described by third parties (DOV Magazine, Pianist Magazine) — is the interface of artistic practice with psychosocial and body / performance-medicine methods, delivered mostly as group classes plus confidential individual counseling and coaching, live in Cologne or online.43

Who delivers it

TGR's assurance is assembled from a founder-psychologist plus a roster of named part-time practitioners, not a salaried clinical department. Counseling and the online transition group are led by Heather O'Donnell (M.Sc. Psychology, Artistic-Systemic Therapist DGSF); coaching by Brigitte Schreiner (systemic coach, flutist); the in-person transition group by Dagmar Regorsek (psychological/career counselor, sociologist). Workshop leaders cover Feldenkrais, Alexander Technique, Pilates, yoga, Qi Gong, Vipassana meditation, PEP/self-worth training, and music physiology / musicians' medicine (including physician Ulrike Kaltenbrunner and violinist-physiologist Christoph Mayer). The unifying hiring rule: every counselor and teacher is an active or former performing artist.37

Reach — organization-reported

Per the CultureForHealth mapping, since founding in 2020 TGR has assisted over 200 artists and held over 60 performances showcasing resident artists for a collective audience of ~2,000. These are TGR's own reported activity counts (not independently audited outcomes), but they are consistent with the scale of a single Cologne centre. The GreenHaven residency strand — under UNESCO-Commission patronage, with named institutional partners — is the most externally validated part of the program. ⚠️ Reach figures are org-reported, as-reported.6

05 — Recognition & networks

Small budget, outsized validation6

For an organization this small, TGR carries an unusual amount of institutional validation — concentrated around the GreenHaven residency / artists-at-risk strand, which is also where most of its grant funding flows.

UNESCO patronage. The GreenHaven residency program holds a patronage of the German Commission for UNESCO; its Secretary-General, Roman Luckscheiter, is quoted that the program "makes a fundamental contribution to the protection of human rights and artistic freedom." This is a credibility marker no marketing budget buys.64

Institutional partners. The residency and refugee-artist work is delivered with named partners — the Goethe-Institut, Artists-at-Risk (Finland), and the German Federal Office for Migration and Refugees (BAMF) — the same bodies that appear in its funding line.6

European networks. TGR is an Associate member of the European Network of Cultural Centres (ENCC) — and, per its own site, serves on the ENCC Board of Directors — and is a member of IETM and On The Move. It has lectured to the Gürzenich Orchestra, the ENCC and the Performing Arts Medical Association on artists' mental and physical health.74

06 — Strengths

What works

A genuinely tiny, credible care service

A real, recognised music-MH counseling centre — UNESCO-patronised, ENCC board member — running on an ~€80k budget and a €1,000-capitalised non-profit shell. Proof that "a room" can be stood up by a practitioner without an endowment, a hotline, or an institutional parent.56

The donation-based + means-tested access model

Pay-what-you-can counseling plus a case-by-case financial-support application is well-matched to a precarious clientele — avoiding both the "free-but-unfunded" and "fee-but-exclusionary" traps.21

Practitioner credibility ("by artists, for artists")

Every counselor and teacher is an active or former performing artist with psychological/somatic training; the founder is herself a concert pianist who became a psychologist after injury. This buys trust and relevance in the exact community served.78

Method breadth from a part-time roster

A wide catalogue (counseling, coaching, performance medicine, Feldenkrais, Alexander Technique, ACT, mindfulness) assembled from named part-time practitioners rather than a salaried clinical department.3

Grant leverage through a sharp niche

The artists-at-risk / refugee-artist residency strand attracted UNESCO patronage and partnerships with the Goethe-Institut, BAMF and Artists-at-Risk — turning a sharply-defined cause into institutional grant support.6

A new institutional home

From autumn 2025 TGR is hosted at Cologne's Alte Feuerwache in a cooperation project — physical anchoring and a partner institution that a one-room operation otherwise lacks.4

07 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

Extreme founder-concentration

TGR is, to a first approximation, Heather O'Donnell plus a part-time roster — sole managing director, lead (and often the named) counselor, and public face. For a clinical-adjacent care service this key-person risk is the dominant one. A documented succession plan was not found. ❓ Unverified.53

Micro-scale & grant-dependence

An ~€80k budget funded substantially by restricted project grants (BAMF, Goethe-Institut, MRI) and donations is fragile: loss of the residency-linked grants would hit hard, and there is no reserve or statutory stream.65

Financial opacity

No audited accounts are published on the website; the only public financials are an automated register approximation. Normal for a gUG this size, but it limits external scrutiny and would flag for institutional funders. ⚠️5

Definitional drift: artists' health, not music-MH

TGR serves performing artists (dancers, theatre, performers) as much as musicians, and blends mental health with somatic / performance-medicine and human-rights residency work. Good for grant-raising, but it is not a pure music-mental-health service — a Taiwan borrower must decide how tightly to scope.16

Scope boundary not foregrounded

TGR is a counseling-and-coaching centre pitched at prevention/rehabilitation/transition — not a crisis line or clinical-treatment provider. Acute-risk users need routing elsewhere, but the public materials do not foreground a crisis-scope disclaimer the way some peers do. ⚠️42

Reach unaudited

The 200+ artists / 60+ performances / ~2,000 audience figures and the €80k budget are organization-reported via a third-party mapping, not independently audited. Directionally credible for a single centre, but not verified outcome measures. ⚠️6

08 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. This is the floor-of-the-floor for "a room": a service one practitioner can start

    Of the organizations in this series, TGR is the smallest viable shape of a care service — a single qualified practitioner, a part-time roster, one room, ~€80k/yr. For a Taiwan founder who is themselves a clinician-musician, it is proof you can begin with counseling + workshops and grow, without a hotline or an endowment.56

  2. Adopt the donation-based + means-tested access model

    Offer counseling/coaching on a pay-what-you-can basis with a simple case-by-case financial-support application on top. It fits precarious music incomes (in Taiwan: freelance gig workers, 接案 musicians) better than either a flat fee or an unfunded "free" promise, and lets income scale with ability to pay.21

  3. Make "by artists, for artists" a hiring rule, not a slogan

    TGR's credibility comes from staffing exclusively with practitioners who are both psychologically/somatically trained and active/former performers. In Taiwan, recruit 諮商心理師 / 臨床心理師 who are also musicians (or pair clinicians with musician co-facilitators) so the service speaks the community's language.71

  4. Assemble breadth from a part-time roster; don't build a department

    Stand up a wide catalogue — counseling, performance-anxiety and resilience workshops, body / performance-medicine practices — by contracting named part-time practitioners, the way TGR amortises Feldenkrais, Alexander Technique, ACT and music-physiology across freelancers.3

  5. Anchor institutional grants to one sharply-defined cause

    TGR turned artists at risk / refugee artists into UNESCO patronage and Goethe-Institut/BAMF partnerships. A Taiwan NPO should identify an analogous fundable niche (e.g. MH support for a specific at-risk music population) to unlock 文化部 / foundation / corporate grants alongside its general service.6

  6. Fix the two things TGR has not: succession and transparency

    Build the service so it is not one person — co-leads, a real (even small) board, documented clinical supervision — and publish reach and (eventually) audited finances early. TGR's founder-concentration and financial opacity are exactly what Taiwanese funders and partners will probe; cheap to design in at the start, expensive to retrofit.35

  7. Name the scope boundary explicitly

    TGR is preventive/rehabilitative, not crisis care. A Taiwan service should state plainly it is not an emergency service, set an intake boundary, and route acute risk to the national line (1925 安心專線) — protecting a light-touch counseling service from being treated as crisis care.24
