THE B-SIDE PROJECT

「B-side」· ソニー・ミュージックエンタテインメント

Org Study · Est. 2021

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

Japan's union floor protects musicians as workers — it sets a baseline of rights for anyone in the trade. B-side does the opposite: it is a room, not a floor. It is a corporate welfare programme built inside Sony Music Entertainment (Japan) — free counselling, a 24/7 medical-chat line, periodic check-ups, even a dystonia clinic — that is genuinely good and genuinely well-run. The catch is on the door: for most of its life you could only enter if you were under an exclusive management contract with a Sony Music Group company, or one of the staff working beside them. This study verifies who runs it, who pays, and who can actually walk in — and tracks the 2024 move to unlock the door for the wider industry through the 音制連 producers' federation, which is real, deliberate, and still only a trial. It also matters because it is one of the very few music-industry-specific mental-health initiatives anywhere in East Asia — which is exactly why a label-gated room leaves so much of the regional need unmet.10

01 — Profile

A label's in-house care programme1

The Orgs Map lists this organisation as "The B-Side Project." Its own name is simply "B-side", and it is not an independent NPO at all — it is a project of Sony Music Entertainment (Japan) Inc. (SMEJ), operated across the wider Sony Music Group (Japan). The project's public site carries the line "© 2026 Sony Music Entertainment (Japan) Inc." and the English handle "the B-side project" only as the address of its website.5

The name carries a deliberate double meaning that doubles as the mission: care for not only the public self that stands on stage (the "A-side") but the everyday self behind it (the "B-side"); and a promise that Sony Music's people will always stand "Be-side" their artists and creators.1 The English tagline on the live site is "Standing by Your Side."5

02 — The model

A good room behind a closed door

The defining fact about B-side is not what it offers — the offer is strong — but who is allowed in. Both the Japanese and English launch releases define eligibility identically: the services are for artists, actors, talents, songwriters and character-business creators who are under exclusive management contracts (専属マネジメント契約) with Sony Music Group (Japan), together with the staff who work directly with them.12 The counselling and dystonia pages on the live site sit behind a login/registration wall, consistent with a closed, members-only service.5

The eligibility gate

What sits inside the room is a genuinely complete care stack, offered free of charge and staffed by professionals — not a hotline-and-T-shirt model. The four founding services, plus a fifth added in 2024, are below.

  • Service 01 · online medical chat

    "first call" — 24/7, anonymous

    Anonymous, round-the-clock access to "first call", an online medical consultation app where a doctor responds by chat to physical or mental concerns. The app is operated by a third party, Mediplat Inc., and provided to eligible creators and their direct staff.16

  • Service 02 · professional counselling

    Sessions with licensed psychologists

    Counselling by certified clinical psychologists (臨床心理士) and nationally certified public psychologists (公認心理師), available to eligible creators and to the staff working directly with them. This is the clinical-grade core — peer support it is not.1

  • Service 03 · periodic check-up

    Voluntary mental & physical check-ups

    An optional mind-and-body check-up, offered within the health-screening cycle to eligible creators who want it — a preventive-maintenance posture rather than a crisis-only one.13

  • Service 04 · workshops

    Internal mental-health workshops

    Regular workshops on mental wellness and self-care for Sony Music Group staff — sometimes with experts, artists or talents speaking about their own experience — to build literacy among the managers who are usually the first line of support.13

  • Service 05 · added Feb 2024

    Dystonia consultation — a musician-specific clinic

    From February 2024 a specialist in dystonia (ジストニア) — the focal movement disorder that can end an instrumentalist's career — joined as one of the counsellors, with online consultation. This is the one piece of B-side that is unmistakably occupational and musician-specific, and it now appears as its own "ジストニア相談" channel on the site.35

Alongside the gated services, B-side runs a deliberately open awareness arm: the casual podcast / YouTube show "B-side Talk ～心の健康ケアしてる？" (running since October 2022), and public World Mental Health Day events — the 2024 edition featured artist Takanori Nishikawa (西川貴教). Anyone can listen; only the contracted can be treated.9

03 — Funding & structure

Who pays, and how it is run

B-side has no donations page, no membership dues, no grant line, and publishes no budget — because it does not need one in the way an NPO does. It is a cost centre inside a profitable record company: SMEJ funds it, and the services reach eligible users at no charge to them.1 That is the structural inverse of the donation-and-merch charities elsewhere in this Org Study — and the reason no fundraising flywheel appears on this page.

Operationally it is run by a cross-company team: members are seconded from the Sony Music Group companies whose creators are covered, each doing B-side work alongside their day jobs (兼務). The originator, artist manager Eri Tokudome, has described it growing from a hand-to-mouth volunteer effort into an organised function once leadership assigned dedicated headcount.3 There is a clear business logic underneath the welfare language: as 音制連 chair Tatsuya Nomura put it, when an artist's mental health fails, "the production company's economic activity simply stops" — prevention protects people and revenue at once.3

04 — The 2024 opening

Unlocking the door — via the trade body3

The most important update to the old "Sony-only" framing is this: in 2024, B-side began extending beyond Sony's own roster. The Federation of Music Producers Japan (日本音楽制作者連盟, "音制連") — a trade body whose ~230 member companies include much of the independent live and management sector — proposed turning B-side into an industry-level scheme. Tokudome joined the 音制連 board in June 2023 to drive it, and a trial rollout to member firms was targeted for around spring 2024.38

Two honest qualifications keep this from being over-read. First, the project's leads stress it was always meant to reach beyond Sony — but because the material is so sensitive, expansion proceeds slowly, with confidentiality safeguards and education sessions for participating firms before they adopt it.4 Second, as of the latest verifiable reporting it is a trial, not a completed national rollout — the ~230 figure is a target population, not a served one.3 The door is being unlocked, deliberately and slowly. It is not yet open.

05 — Appraisal

What works, and what to watch

Clinical-grade, not peer-grade

Licensed psychologists, a doctor-staffed 24/7 line, and a dystonia specialist. This is real care, free at the point of use — well above the awareness-and-referral tier common in the field.

Staff are covered too

Eligibility explicitly includes the managers and crew around artists — the people who otherwise absorb the strain invisibly. Few programmes anywhere extend care to the support layer.

Prevention by design

Voluntary check-ups, workshops and the "dentist before the cavity" framing push toward maintenance, not just crisis — a healthier posture than reactive aid.

A funding model that cannot run dry

Bankrolled by a profitable parent, it carries none of the donation-volatility risk that destabilises independent charities. Stability is its quiet superpower.

Eligibility is the whole story

For most of its life, only Sony-contracted creators and their staff could enter. A Japanese musician on an indie label, self-released, or freelance was simply outside the room.

Not independent, not accountable to the public

As a corporate cost centre it files no public accounts, publishes no budget or outcome data, and answers to a label, not to a membership or a regulator.

The opening is still a trial

The 音制連 extension is promising but unfinished; "~230 firms" is a target, and confidentiality concerns keep the pace slow. Reach beyond Sony should not be assumed.

Tied to one company's priorities

What B-side does, and whether it keeps growing outward, depends on SMEJ's continued will — including through leadership change. A corporate room can be re-scoped from above.

06 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. The "room" is excellent; the locked door is the warning

    B-side proves that a well-run, clinical-grade care room for music workers is buildable and sustainable. But a room only one label's roster can enter leaves the freelance and indie majority — exactly the group a Taiwan NPO would exist to serve — outside. Design for the people without a major-label contract from day one.

  2. Cover the staff, not just the artists

    The single most copyable idea here costs little and is rare: extend support to managers, crew and the people working beside artists. They carry the load and burn out quietly. An NPO can make "the people around the musician" eligible on principle, where a label does it as employee welfare.1

  3. Borrow the occupational angle — including dystonia

    B-side's dystonia clinic is the part that is genuinely music-specific, not generic mental health. A Taiwan org can differentiate from general counselling services by owning the occupational conditions of the trade — performance anxiety, hearing, focal dystonia — that no general clinic addresses well.3

  4. Lead with the trade body, not a single label

    The most strategic move B-side made was letting a federation (音制連) carry it industry-wide. A Taiwan NPO should anchor to a neutral host — a musicians' union, the Recording Academy-equivalent, or a producers' association — so access is defined by being in the trade, not by an employer. That is how a "room" starts to behave like a "floor."3

  5. Pair a casual front door with the clinical back room

    "B-side Talk" lowers the stigma threshold in public while the real care stays private and professional. A Taiwan org can run a light, open, de-stigmatising media channel and a confidential clinical service in parallel — the open part recruits, the closed part treats.9

  6. Watch the accountability gap you can close

    B-side publishes no budget or outcome data because, as a corporate programme, it need not. An NPO's disadvantage — having to fundraise — is also its advantage: publishing usage, outcomes and finances builds the public trust a label never has to earn. Make transparency a feature.
