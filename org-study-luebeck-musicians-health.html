MUSICIANS' HEALTH · LÜBECK

The Musicians' Health Working Group at the Musikhochschule Lübeck & Universität zu Lübeck

Org Study · Est. 2022

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

MusiCares shows an incumbent's ceiling; Music Minds Matter shows how to incubate a charity inside a benevolent fund. Lübeck asks a smaller, sharper question: what does music-specific mental-health care cost and cover when it is not an NPO at all, but a single endowed chair shared by a conservatory and a medical university? The "Musicians' Health Center" on this project's Orgs Map turns out to be the Working Group "Musicians' Health" (AG Musizierendengesundheit) — one professorship, a small counselling office, and a research programme, and the only German musicians'-medicine unit led on mental health. It is the "room" question in its most institutionally embedded form.

01 — Profile

A bridge professorship, not a charity1

The honest first correction is about what this is. There is no independent "Musicians' Health Center" with its own legal personality. The entity is an academic unit — the Professur für Musizierendengesundheit (Professorship for Musicians' Health) and its Arbeitsgruppe (AG) Musizierendengesundheit — created jointly by the Musikhochschule Lübeck (MHL) and the Universität zu Lübeck (UzL), and structurally located inside the University of Lübeck's Institute of Medical Psychology (IMP). The map's "Center" label is a loose rendering of the universities' own phrase, "a centre for medical counselling and treatment" — a described capability, not a registered organisation.14

02 — The model

Two universities, one bridge chair2

The structural distinction of Lübeck is the Brückenprofessur — a single professorship that "bridges" a conservatory (MHL) and a medical university (UzL). At the founding in February 2021 the two universities jointly advertised what they called the "Lübecker Modell zur Musikergesundheit" (Lübeck Model for Musicians' Health), built on a close cooperation of MHL, UzL and the UKSH university hospital and its clinics. Named cultural partners include the Lübeck music schools and the Philharmonic Orchestra of the Hanseatic City of Lübeck.

What makes the model distinctive, in the universities' own framing, is who it reaches: it is explicitly not limited to university members. From the start it was designed to serve, through counselling and continuing education, music-school teachers, freelance music instructors, orchestra musicians, and singing/music-making amateurs — and, on the prevention side, children and young people. A public consultation (Sprechstunde) at UzL, a teaching offer in musicians' health at MHL, and research at both were the founding pillars.2

The funder's own reason, quoted at founding: Max Schön of the Possehl-Stiftung said it funds the chair because the two universities "cooperate for the good of those working in culture in our city," adding that the new services will benefit not only students but also amateur musicians. UzL's president called the joint professorship "unique in the state"; MHL's president framed the goal as helping musicians manage difficulties "and preventing serious disorders from arising in the first place."2

03 — Scope, honestly

Mental health is the differentiator3

This is the finding to be precise about, and it cuts the other way from most musicians'-medicine centres. Germany has, in the institutions' own count, around ten musicians'-medical facilities — the best known being the Institute for Music Physiology and Musicians' Medicine (IMMM) in Hannover, where Scholz spent eleven years. Lübeck is the only one specialised in neurological, psychological and psychotherapeutic topics — i.e. in the mental health of musicians. The others are physiology- and physical-medicine-led; Lübeck's stated focus is "prevention and the psychological health of musicians."37

04 — What it does

Four strands4

  • Since Oct 2022

    Beratung — counselling

    A counselling office (Beratungsstelle) for music students, professionals and amateurs from the North-German region, offering individual consultation on mental and physical health in music-making, at the Tesdorpfhaus.

  • Teaching

    Lehre — for all Lübeck students

    Lectures on the fundamentals of healthy music-making and the neuroscience of music for MHL and UzL students, plus a stage-fright seminar (Lampenfieberseminar) and a "Music & the Brain" seminar.

  • Research

    Forschung — with UzL Neurology

    In close cooperation with UzL Neurology at the CBBM, research on mental load (mentale Belastung) and the neuronal plasticity of music students. Stated priority: prevention and psychological health.3

  • Interdisciplinary

    Hörgesundheit — hearing health

    Work on the psychology and hearing health of musicians, embedded in the HörHanse network with TH Lübeck and UKSH audiology/acoustics.

05 — The visible output

LIMIT — a measurement instrument, free to adopt5

The clearest tangible product to date is a measurement instrument. In early 2026 the working group (Christine Sickert, Dr. Stine Alpheis, Prof. Daniel S. Scholz, with Prof. Dr. Jonas Obleser of UzL's Institute of Psychology) published the "Lübeck Inventory on Musicians' Psychological Stress" (LIMIT) — an open-access, self-administered screening questionnaire designed specifically for music students and professional musicians.6

LIMIT exists because conventional psychological questionnaires capture musicians' lives poorly. It combines, in one instrument, existential worries about a small and fiercely competitive labour market, financial insecurity, performance anxiety, physical complaints from playing, and social strain — neglected friendships and family from freelance/project work, the former hobby become a profession with no compensating leisure. It is explicitly not diagnostic: it is for long-term self-monitoring, so changes are caught early and preventive support sought. MHL plans to introduce LIMIT in 2026, recommended at the start of each semester.5

LIMIT matters for this study because it is the lowest-cost, most transferable thing the unit makes — a validated, open instrument any other institution can adopt at near-zero cost. It is the same lesson MusiCares teaches with its Wellness in Music Survey: the cheapest measurement asset, not the expensive service, is the part that travels.

06 — Funding

Philanthropic seed inside a public university2

The funding model is the heart of the "what it costs" question, and it reads cleanly because this is an endowed professorship, not a fundraising charity.

Start-up: ~€600,000 from the Possehl-Stiftung Lübeck, a large Lübeck civic foundation, covering an initial five years — a Stiftungsprofessur (philanthropically endowed chair), including a scientific-staff post to help build the music-medicine capability at UzL.8 Intended steady state: after a five-year evaluation, the professorship is to be made permanent (verstetigt) and financed onward by MHL and UzL — converting from philanthropic seed to public-university line funding. Whether that conversion has been confirmed is not public (❓).2

Implication: the unit does not run on donations, galas, membership dues, or service fees. Its sustainability rests on a host institution absorbing it — exactly the trade a university-embedded model makes: low fundraising overhead and clinical legitimacy, in exchange for dependence on two institutions' budgets and one foundation's initial bet. For this project's thesis, Lübeck is room (a direct service) built on an institutional foundation rather than a statutory one — closer to a hospital outpatient clinic than to Germany's Künstlersozialkasse (the statutory floor). It shows "room" can be durable without a charity wrapper, if a public institution will own it.

07 — Strengths

What works

Legitimacy by construction

A chair in a medical-psychology institute, tied to neurology and a university hospital, gives the service credibility and a research pipeline a standalone NPO would spend years building.

A real differentiator

Of ~10 German musicians'-medicine centres, the only one led on mental health — a clear, defensible niche, not a me-too clinic.

Low overhead, no fundraising treadmill

No gala, no donor cultivation. One foundation grant seeds it; the intended steady state is institutional line funding.

Reach beyond the campus

By design it serves regional professionals and amateurs, not only enrolled students — unusual for a university unit.

A transferable artefact

LIMIT is an open-access instrument the rest of the field can adopt at near-zero cost.

08 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

Key-person concentration

Essentially one professor plus a two-person counselling/teaching team. Identity, clinical authority and research direction rest on Daniel Scholz — a single point of failure a charity board would diversify.

Funding cliff / unconfirmed permanence

The €600k covers five years; permanent funding is an intention, contingent on evaluation and two universities' budgets. No public confirmation of Verstetigung was found.

Regionally bounded

"North-German region" is the stated catchment — local infrastructure, not a national programme. It does not scale by design.

Opaque service metrics

No public case volumes, waiting times, fees, or intake process — so actual service capacity cannot be assessed from outside.

Embedded, not independent

The institutional home that grants legitimacy also removes autonomy: priorities, survival and growth are decided above the unit, by the two universities.

09 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental-health NPO should take

  1. A university can be the cheapest, most credible host for music-specific care

    The most legitimate way to stand up clinical musicians'-mental-health services is to attach them to an institution that already has clinicians, ethics oversight, and research. For Taiwan, the analogue is a partnership with a music conservatory and/or a university psychology/psychiatry department — buying credibility and a research engine an NPO cannot quickly replicate.1

  2. Pick a defensible specialisation; don't be a me-too clinic

    Lübeck's whole identity is "the mental-health one" among general musicians'-medicine centres. A Taiwan org should choose a niche the existing system does not cover — and for Taiwan, any music-specific mental-health service currently is that niche — and own it explicitly.3

  3. Seed with one anchor funder — but plan the hand-off from day one

    The Possehl model — a single civic foundation funds five years, then the universities take over — is a realistic Taiwan path: a major foundation or the Ministry of Culture seeds a fixed term, with a pre-agreed plan for who absorbs the running cost. Lübeck's open risk (permanence still unconfirmed) is the caution: secure the hand-off in writing before the seed runs out.2

  4. Build a measurement instrument early — it is the cheapest thing that travels

    LIMIT, like MusiCares' survey, is low-cost and high-leverage: a validated, open Taiwan-specific screening tool for music students and professionals would create baseline data where none exists, give the org a reason to be in every conservatory each semester, and be adoptable by others at no marginal cost. The single most copyable element here.5

  5. Design reach beyond the campus from the start

    Lübeck deliberately serves freelancers, orchestra players, teachers and amateurs, not just enrolled students. A Taiwan unit hosted by a conservatory should write external professionals into its remit from day one, or it will only reach the people already inside the building.2

  6. Mind the key-person and capacity risks an NPO would hedge

    The flip side of a lean university chair is fragility: one departure can hollow it out, and service capacity is invisible. A Taiwan org borrowing the model should pair the academic anchor with a succession plan and published service metrics — the governance hygiene a standalone charity brings that a single professorship does not.4
