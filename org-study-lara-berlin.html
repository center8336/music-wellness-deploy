LARA BERLIN

Fachstelle gegen sexualisierte Gewalt an Frauen, trans*, inter* und nicht-binären Personen

Org Study · Est. 1992

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

MusiCares is a music-industry incumbent; the Lübeck clinic treats musicians' bodies; MiM-Verband is a music-MH trade body. LARA is none of these — it is a general, Berlin-Senate-financed specialist crisis and counselling centre for women, trans*, inter* and non-binary people affected by sexualized violence. It is in this project's map for one honest reason: in the absence of a music-specific service, it is one of the general crisis safety-nets a Berlin-based (or, by analogy, a Taiwanese) musician who had survived rape, sexual assault or harassment would actually fall back on. The transferable lessons are structural — a statutory public-funding base, a specialist referral posture, and radically low-barrier access — not anything music-sector. The finding that matters is what LARA is not: it is the floor, not a music service.1

01 — Profile

A specialist sexual-violence crisis centre, not a clinic1

LARA is a Fachstelle (specialist centre) against sexualized violence — for women, girls, trans*, inter* and non-binary people from the age of 14 who have experienced rape, sexual assault or sexual harassment (in partnerships, at the workplace, or by friends, acquaintances or strangers), and for their relatives. It is run by the registered non-profit association LARA – Verein gegen sexuelle Gewalt an Frauen e.V., founded in 1992, recognised as charitable (gemeinnützig und mildtätig) and registered at the Amtsgericht Charlottenburg under VR 12883B.13

The single defining fact: since 1995 the association has been the Träger (operating body) of the project "LARA", financed by the Berlin Senate Department for Labour, Social Affairs, Equality, Integration, Diversity and Anti-Discrimination (Senatsverwaltung für Arbeit, Soziales, Gleichstellung, Integration, Vielfalt und Antidiskriminierung). LARA is therefore a publicly commissioned crisis service embedded in Berlin's statutory anti-violence system — not a grassroots line scraping by on donations. That sets it sharply apart from the donation-only model of Samaritans of Thailand.15

LARA belongs to two recognised structures: the national bff – Bundesverband Frauenberatungsstellen und Frauennotrufe (the umbrella "Frauen gegen Gewalt"), and, since 2010, the Deutscher Paritätischer Wohlfahrtsverband — one of Germany's six statutory welfare-umbrella associations. This is the German Wohlfahrt architecture in action: an independent non-profit delivers a specialised public service under a Senate contract, inside a national peer network that sets standards.14

02 — The model

Specialist counselling, with a referral hinge2

LARA's distinctiveness is not breadth of programmes but depth on one harm and a clear referral posture. Its services, stated on its own site and in the German federal good-practice database:

  • Core · counselling

    Free, anonymous, multilingual counselling

    Telephone and in-person counselling is the core; the hotline (030 216 88 88, Mon–Fri 9–18) takes both appointment-booking and direct crisis calls, and walk-ins are possible during opening hours. Email counselling answers within ~3 working days; counselling materials are offered in nine languages (German, English, Turkish, Polish, French, Spanish, Russian, Arabic, Farsi), with language mediation and sign-language interpreting arranged on request. The service is free and can be used anonymously.23

  • Specialist · depth

    Crisis intervention, short-term therapy, groups, legal & court support

    Beyond counselling: crisis intervention, short-term therapy (Kurzzeittherapie), guided and self-help groups, free legal counselling by experienced lawyers every Tuesday 17:30–19:00 (including advice on filing charges and on the Nebenklage, joining a trial as co-plaintiff), and psychosocial court-process accompaniment (Psychosoziale Prozessbegleitung). It also runs a mobile counselling team for refugee women and provides training and supervision for professionals in schools, the police and public agencies.36

  • Referral · knows its edges

    Routes acute risk & long-term therapy outward

    Unusually instructive: LARA refers acute risk outward rather than holding everything in-house. Its own "acute support" page tells people in acute crisis to also contact the Berliner Krisendienst (Berlin's 24/7 general psychiatric crisis service), the police, or a hospital emergency room; for long-term therapy it hands out a vetted trauma-therapist list and points to the Traumanetz Berlin rather than providing open-ended therapy itself. LARA knows exactly what it is — a specialist daytime counselling and court-support service — and routes everything outside that scope to the right partner. That referral discipline is the single most transferable thing on this page.2

03 — Funding & sustainability

A statutory floor, with donations at the margin1

This is where LARA most usefully contrasts with the other general crisis services in this series. LARA's core "Fachstelle" work is financed by the Berlin Senate (Senatsverwaltung für Arbeit, Soziales, Gleichstellung, Integration, Vielfalt und Antidiskriminierung) — public, recurring funding under a service mandate the association has held since 1995. On top of that statutory base, the e.V. relies on membership fees and donations specifically to develop new projects and ideas "that receive no public financing." Donations fund the margin and the experiments, not the core service.1

That is the German model worth studying: a specialist NPO delivering a public service on a Senate contract, supplemented by donations for the parts the state will not fund, inside the Paritätischer/bff welfare-association architecture. It is structurally far more secure than a donation-only helpline — there is a statutory floor under it.4

Honest limitation (❓). The fact of Senate financing is sourced (LARA's own site and the Senate's pages); the amount is not public, and no audited LARA accounts surfaced. So on the Orgs Map, row #54's funding Basis can move from "❓ No source" to ✅ Sourced for the model — public (Berlin Senate) + membership/donations — while any figure remains ❓. (This mirrors the Samaritans-of-Thailand caution in reverse: there the model was donation and the figures were stale; here the model is statutory and the figures are simply not published.)1

04 — Reach & operating profile

Single-site, daytime, capacity-bound — and largely unquantified2

LARA is a single-site, daytime, Berlin-wide service: one centre in Schöneberg, hotline and opening hours Monday–Friday 9–18, plus the mobile refugee-women outreach. Its own counselling page notes the hotline is frequently busy — a candid signal of demand outstripping a fixed-capacity, publicly funded team, and a reminder that even a statutorily funded service is capacity-bound.

No current public statistics (annual case numbers, staffing, budget) surfaced in this research. Per Rule 4, that is an absence of published figures, not evidence of a small service: LARA is a long-established, Senate-funded Fachstelle inside the national bff network, which implies a professional standing team — but the specific operating numbers are ❓ No source. The federal Gesundheitliche Chancengleichheit record is dated (Stand 2011) and is used here only for the service taxonomy, which LARA's current site fully corroborates, not for any current figure.3

05 — What it offers musicians

Nothing music-specific — said plainly1

This must be said without hedging: LARA offers nothing designed for the music sector. There is no musician track, no industry partnership, no occupational-health or touring/gig framing, no music-industry-aware counsellors. It is a general specialist service for one category of harm — sexualized violence — open to anyone in its target groups. A musician who had been raped or sexually harassed would reach exactly the same counsellors, the same Tuesday legal clinic, the same daytime hotline, as any other person in Berlin.3

That is precisely why LARA sits in this project's map and in this study. Sexual violence and harassment are documented occupational risks in the music industry — backstage, on tour, in power-asymmetric professional relationships — yet there is no music-specific service that addresses them. In that gap, a survivor in the music world falls back on the general specialist centre. LARA's value to the music sector is therefore real but incidental — it is the floor a musician lands on, not a music-sector service. This is the same honest-scoping verdict as Samaritans of Thailand: the general crisis service is the nearest-available resource, and the music-specific layer is missing.

06 — Strengths & weaknesses

What works, what to watch (as a musician's fallback)

Statutory funding floor

A Berlin-Senate-financed mandate since 1995 — structurally secure in a way donation-only crisis lines are not. The public floor is exactly what fragile peers lack.

Specialist depth + referral hinge

Deep expertise on one harm, paired with the discipline to route acute risk and long-term therapy to the Berliner Krisendienst, police, ERs and a vetted therapist list rather than over-reaching.

Radically low-barrier

Free, anonymous, no referral, walk-in possible, nine-language counselling with interpreting and sign-language on request — every friction removed.

Embedded in a recognised network

Member of the bff and the Paritätischer Wohlfahrtsverband — shared standards, legitimacy and a national peer structure.

General, not sector-specialised

Serves everyone in its target groups, so serves no occupational group — including musicians — with anything tailored.

Narrow harm scope

Only sexualized violence; a musician's burnout, depression, addiction or financial precarity is out of scope (LARA would refer those out too).

Daytime, single-site, capacity-bound

Mon–Fri 9–18, one centre, a "frequently busy" hotline — not 24/7 and not city-saturating.

Opaque operating figures

No current public case numbers or budget — the public evidence base on scale is thin (❓).

07 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. Anchor to a statutory funder first — LARA's real lesson

    LARA's security comes from a public mandate (the Berlin Senate), with donations funding only the margin. A Taiwan music-MH NPO should likewise anchor to a public base — the NT$5.63B national MH plan, Ministry of Culture, or local social-affairs budgets — and treat donations as the experiment-and-margin layer, not the foundation. This is the opposite of the donation-only posture that left Samaritans of Thailand fragile.1

  2. Be a specialist with a referral hinge, not a do-everything service

    LARA's most copyable design is knowing its edges: it goes deep on one harm and refers acute risk and long-term therapy to named partners (the Berliner Krisendienst, ERs, a vetted therapist list). A music-MH NPO should define a tight scope — the music-sector layer — and build referral relationships to general crisis lines and clinical services, adding the music-aware layer on top rather than rebuilding crisis infrastructure.2

  3. Engineer low-barrier access deliberately

    Free, anonymous, walk-in-possible, multilingual with interpreting — LARA removes every friction that keeps precarious people away. For a gig-economy, often-uninsured music workforce, the same design (no paperwork, no referral, language access) is what makes a service actually reachable.2

  4. Join — or seed — a welfare / peer network

    LARA's standing rests partly on the bff and Paritätischer architecture: shared standards, legitimacy, statutory recognition. A Taiwan music-MH NPO benefits from being inside a recognised umbrella (an arts-welfare body, a mental-health association) rather than standing alone — for credibility with funders and for referral pathways.4

  5. Name the sector-specific gap honestly — the floor is not the service

    LARA shows that even a well-funded, well-run general specialist service is, for a musician, only a fallback. Sexual harassment and assault are real occupational risks in music, yet no music-specific service addresses them. A Taiwan NPO should be explicit that the general safety-net (crisis lines, sexual-violence centres, the national MH plan) is the floor, and that the music-specific room — occupational framing, industry-aware staff, sector trust — is the distinct thing still missing. Don't assume an existing general or welfare body already covers it.
