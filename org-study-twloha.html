TO WRITE LOVE ON HER ARMS

Org Study · Est. 2006

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

MusiCares and the national funds treat reach as a means to deliver service. TWLOHA inverts that: the reach is the product. It is a US general mental-health movement — not a clinic, not a music charity — that builds an audience through story and "wearable hope," then refers people to treatment others provide and funds a slice of it directly. On roughly $3M a year3 it reaches, by its own count, over a hundred million people online.2 The Orgs Map lists it as a multinational "in 27 countries incl. India, Japan, Thailand." This study asks the only honest question that label invites — what is actually there? — and finds the answer is a directory of other people's helplines and a T-shirt, not infrastructure.

01 — Profile

A movement built from one essay16

To Write Love On Her Arms began in 2006 as a short essay by Jamie Tworkowski about five days he spent helping a 19-year-old named Renee Yohe before she entered rehab. He printed T-shirts to pay for her treatment and posted the story on MySpace; bands wore the shirts on stage, and a movement formed around them. It is, by origin, a music-scene story — but it has never been scoped to musicians. TWLOHA is a general mental-health organization for anyone struggling with depression, addiction, self-injury, or thoughts of suicide.5

One sentence from TWLOHA's own About page contains the entire model: it exists "to encourage, inform, inspire, and invest directly into treatment and recovery."2 It does not deliver treatment — it invests in and refers to treatment that licensed providers deliver. Its Find Help disclaimer says so directly: "you are responsible for selecting the provider… TWLOHA cannot accept responsibility for any of the services provided."4

02 — The model

Awareness → referral → fund — with no clinic in the loop

The clearest way to read TWLOHA is as a four-move loop. What matters as much as the four moves is what is deliberately absent from them: TWLOHA never employs a therapist, never runs its own crisis line, never operates a facility. That is precisely why it can claim global reach on a small budget — and precisely why reach and service must not be confused.

The awareness → referral → fund loop
MOVE 01

Awareness — the front door

Storytelling (blog, podcast, social) reaching, by the org's own figure, 103.9M people online a year, plus "wearable hope" merch that makes a T-shirt a conversation. Awareness is the product TWLOHA actually manufactures.2

MOVE 02

Referral — the hand-off

The FIND HELP Tool is a US treatment directory — "only currently available in the U.S." Everyone else gets the International Resources list of third-party national helplines. TWLOHA connects; others treat.4

MOVE 03

Fund — the one tangible service

The Treatment & Recovery Scholarship pays licensed providers directly. Cumulatively the org reports $4.7M invested and 35,000 therapy hours funded — the single place money lands on the ground, and shown CLOSED at the time of writing.24

MOVE 04

Support — the supplements

Free virtual Peer Support Groups, a Mental Health Toolkit, a high-school curriculum, and The Hopeful app — all low-cost, self-serve, scalable, none clinical.2

It even outsources crisis: the "text TWLOHA to 741741" line routes to Crisis Text Line, a separate organization.4 The whole design is built to be cheap to scale and impossible to be liable for — the strength and the limit in one decision.

Where the money comes from — donations plus a real merch line3

In FY2024, contributions were $2.54M (81.9%) and net merchandise sales $570K (18.4%). The merch share is not incidental — it is how the movement was built (bands in T-shirts) and a meaningful, mission-aligned revenue stream most charities don't have. There is no endowment, no large institutional backer, and no government funding in the mix. Expenses of $3.58M run roughly half on people (executive compensation $379,754; other salaries $1.33M), normal for an awareness- and communications-led organization. Every board member is unpaid; senior staff each earned about $102K–$112K — all disclosed in the filing, with no hidden-pay structure.

04 — The footprint question

What "27 countries incl. India, Japan, Thailand" really is4

This is the heart of the study. The project's Orgs Map lists TWLOHA as a multinational "in 27 countries incl. India, Japan, Thailand." Against TWLOHA's own primary sources, that "presence" resolves into two things, neither of which is on-the-ground service.

(a) A merchandise shipping footprint

TWLOHA reports its "missional merchandise reaches more than 52 countries," ~20,000 pieces a year.6 That is a shipping footprint, not a programmatic one — and note the org's own number is 52, not 27, so the map's specific figure isn't even TWLOHA's claim. It is carried ⚠️.

(b) A directory of other organizations' helplines

The International Resources page is a table that, for each country, links to an independent, locally-run helpline TWLOHA neither owns nor funds.4 For the project's jurisdictions:

And critically: the FIND HELP Tool — the one piece of genuine TWLOHA infrastructure — states it is "only currently available in the U.S."4 Outside the US, TWLOHA is a signpost to local services, plus a T-shirt. Its reach (awareness, merch, social) is genuinely global; its service is US-only; and its "presence" in Thailand, Japan, or Taiwan is a hyperlink to an organization that existed independently and would exist without it. For a country with little local music-MH infrastructure, that is the crux: a borderless awareness movement imports no infrastructure — at best it points you to whatever already exists.

05 — Strengths

What works

An original funding engine

Merch as ~18% of revenue is a real, mission-aligned, non-grant income stream born from the music scene. Most MH charities cannot turn their brand into a product people want to wear.3

Liability-light by design

By referring rather than treating, TWLOHA scales awareness globally without the cost or risk of clinical operations — the reason a small org can claim nine-figure reach.2

One concrete, honest service

Paying licensed providers directly through the Therapy Scholarship is where the model converts brand into care, and it is auditable in spirit.2

Cultural fluency & durability

Two decades meeting young people at concerts, on campuses, in social feeds, and via an app — and it survived its charismatic founder's complete 2021 exit under professional co-EDs.6

06 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

Three straight deficit years

A 41% reserve drawdown FY2022→FY2024 is the defining current risk; the model must re-find surplus or shrink.3

Reach ≠ service

"103.9M reached" is impressions; "$4.7M invested / 35,000 hours" over ~18 years is the actual care. Both are real; conflating them is the trap.2

The one service is intermittent

The Therapy Scholarship — the single tangible offer — was shown CLOSED at the time of writing, gated by funding. A structural fragility, not a one-off.4

General, not music-specific + a thin footprint

Nothing in the program is scoped to musicians (the musical heritage is brand, not service), and the international "presence" is a directory of other people's helplines — the weakest claim to a country footprint.4

07 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. Build awareness cheaply — but name it honestly

    TWLOHA proves a small org can earn enormous reach through story + merch + social with no clinical overhead. A Taiwan music-MH NPO can do the same on a small budget. But label it as awareness and referral, never as treatment — TWLOHA's own disclaimers model the honesty that keeps an awareness brand credible.4

  2. Treat merch as mission revenue, not a gift-shop afterthought

    ~18% of TWLOHA's income is "wearable hope." In a market with no music endowment and limited grant culture, a well-designed product line tied to the cause is one of the few non-grant revenue options a Taiwan startup actually controls.3

  3. Referral alone is not enough — in Taiwan there is little music-specific service to refer to

    TWLOHA's whole international model is "point people to the local helpline." That works where one already exists. Taiwan has general lines (1995, 1925, 1980) but essentially no musician-specific MH service — so a Taiwan org cannot just be a signpost; it has to build the thing it would otherwise refer to. The footprint analysis is the lesson: a borderless awareness movement leaves an under-served country as under-served as it found it.4

  4. Fund treatment directly before delivering it

    The Therapy Scholarship — paying licensed local providers so clients can afford care — is the most transferable, lowest-risk piece of the model. A Taiwan NPO could fund sessions with vetted Mandarin-speaking therapists for musicians long before it could responsibly run its own clinic.2

  5. Plan for surplus from day one

    TWLOHA's three-year drawdown is the cautionary half: an awareness-led, donation-heavy org with thin reserves is one bad fundraising year from cuts. Build the boring discipline — a reserve target, a surplus in normal years — that the famous part of the model tends to neglect.3

  6. Institutionalize early so the movement outlives its founder

    TWLOHA professionalized into a staffed org with co-EDs and an unpaid board, which is why it survived Tworkowski's 2021 exit. A Taiwan founder should design for succession from the start, not build a cult of personality.6
