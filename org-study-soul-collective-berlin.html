SOUL COLLECTIVEA Berlin wellness platform on the map — but not what the label says

Org Study · Est. 2019

↓ Scroll
Scope flag · boundary case

The Orgs Map lists Soul Collective Berlin under the profile "Mental Health Support." Primary sources show the entity behind that name is a small for-profit wellness-events business — a curated guide to yoga, meditation, sound-healing and retreats — not a nonprofit, not a mental-health service, and not music-specific. This study verifies what it actually is and corrects the record. Per the project's evidence discipline, a short honest page is the right output here.

One model, in one line

The big U.S. funds answer "what does the ceiling look like?"; Germany's TGR The Green Room answers "what is the smallest credible shape a care service can take?" Soul Collective Berlin answers neither — because it is not a care service at all. It is a two-founder commercial platform that curates and resells access to wellness experiences hosted by independent Berlin practitioners. It earns its place in this series only as a calibration point: proof of what a directory's loosest "wellness" inclusion criteria sweep in, and a reminder that a Taiwan org's own resource map will need a definition of "mental-health support" tight enough to keep lifestyle-wellness businesses out of it.

01 — Identity

What it actually is2

Soul Collective (operating as Soul Collective Berlin; domains soulcollective.berlin → soulcollective.eu) describes itself, in its own words, as "an online guide to mindfulness, connection and well-being experiences," sharing "workshops, ceremonies, events, retreats, trainings and courses that promote personal growth and transformation." It was founded in 2019 by Marianne and Natalie, who "hand-select offerings from teachers and practitioners that inspire them."2

The legal entity, per the site's own Impressum (the German §5 TMG legal notice), is Marianne Abbott & Natalie Herb GbR — a Gesellschaft bürgerlichen Rechts, the German civil-law business partnership form. A GbR is a for-profit partnership: it is not an eingetragener Verein (e.V.), not a gemeinnützige (charitable) entity, and carries no nonprofit or public-benefit status.3

Disambiguation — "Soul Collective" is a crowded name

"Soul Collective" is common across wellness, music and dance — searches surface a "Soul Collective Agency," music/label uses, a "Soul Care Circle Berlin" Meetup, and "soulspace Berlin" (a youth-crisis service), among others. The entity profiled here is positively pinned by a consistent fingerprint: domains soulcollective.berlin / soulcollective.eu, founders Marianne & Natalie, the GbR imprint (Abbott & Herb), the Squarespace storefront, and the "online guide to mindfulness… experiences" tagline. That is the same name, profile and country the project's captured Backline directory snapshot records at row #55, so it is the entity the Orgs Map means. No separate Berlin "Soul Collective" providing music-industry mental-health support could be found.56

02 — The scope finding

The map says "Mental Health Support." The evidence says "wellness business."

Three gaps separate the label from the entity, each established from the organization's own pages.

Not a mental-health service

No counseling, no therapy, no peer-support groups, no crisis line, no clinical referral. The catalogue is yoga, meditation, sound healing, breathwork, cacao ceremonies, ecstatic dance, tantra, permaculture and retreats — consumer wellness experiences it curates and resells, not mental-health care it delivers.1

Not music-specific

Nothing frames musicians, the music industry, performing artists, or the night-life/electronic scene as a target group. The audience is the general Berlin wellness consumer; corporate clients are tech and lifestyle brands (Ironhack, Veuve Clicquot, Liganova). Music appears only incidentally — a "somatic singing circle" among hundreds of events.2

Not a nonprofit

A GbR — a commercial partnership with a shopping cart, paid listings and gift certificates — not a charity, foundation or association. The "floor vs. room" frame does not apply, because it sits outside the rights-and-services field entirely.3

How it got the "MH Support" tag

Backline's global directory casts a deliberately wide, multilingual net over "mental health and wellness" resources, with coarse taxonomy buckets. A Berlin platform that programs meditation and sound healing is plausibly swept in and surfaced under a broad "Mental Health Support" label. The project's mid-2026 pass had already flagged this row ❓ "No public funding information located" — an entry not yet verified to entity level.76

Recommended record action (a documentation fix for the Orgs Map maintainer — not changed by this study): keep the row, since it documents a real directory entry, but relabel its service profile to "Wellness / mindfulness platform (general; for-profit)" and note that it is not music-specific and not a nonprofit. The funding Basis stays ❓ in the nonprofit-funding sense — there is no public nonprofit funding because it is not a nonprofit. The honest one-liner: commercial wellness business — out of scope as a music-MH NPO.

03 — Model

A wellness marketplace, briefly1

Because Soul Collective is commercial, the usual NPO funding question — grants? donations? a statutory floor? — does not apply. Its revenue model, read from its own site, is a wellness-marketplace / media stack:

  • Listings

    Paid event & retreat advertising

    "If you have an upcoming event, retreat, training or course you'd like to share with our audience, please send us an email" — a paid-placement / curation model, with sponsored third-party features marked "Sponsored Post."4

  • Sales

    Retreats, teacher trainings, gift certificates

    Sells access to its own and partner retreats and yoga/sound/breathwork teacher trainings across Europe, plus gift certificates — an e-commerce storefront, not a donation page.1

  • B2B

    Corporate & festival wellness

    "Soul Collective creates mindfulness and well-being offerings for companies, festivals and conferences" — paid programming for brands and events.2

No charitable donations, no membership dues, no public/statutory funding, no foundation grants — none of the mechanisms that define the nonprofits elsewhere in this series. No public financial figures exist, and none are expected: a small GbR files nothing comparable to an IRS 990 or a Vereinsregister account, and it publishes no budget. Basis: ⚠️ model described from the site's own commerce surfaces; no figures (none public).

04 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a boundary case teaches

This page earns its keep precisely because the org is out of scope. Three transferable takeaways:

  1. Define "mental-health support" before you map it

    The most useful lesson is methodological. A Taiwan org building its own resource directory will face the temptation that put Soul Collective on the map: "wellness" is elastic, and yoga/retreat/mindfulness businesses cluster at its edge. Decide up front whether the directory lists clinical and peer mental-health services (counseling, therapy, support groups, crisis lines, financial-aid funds) or the broader wellness-and-lifestyle field — and label the two differently. Conflating them inflates the apparent size of the support ecosystem and risks sending a musician in distress toward a cacao ceremony.1

  2. A wide net is a feature for discovery, a bug for trust

    Backline's inclusiveness is a strength for a global concierge whose job is to surface anything that might help. But a Taiwan directory that wants to be a trusted referral source needs a verification gate — entity type, service type, music-relevance — so users can rely on what they find. The cost of the wide net is exactly cases like this one; the project's own ❓ funding flag shows that gate working in slow motion.76

  3. Commercial wellness is the comparison class, not the competitor

    For a Taiwan music-MH NPO, profitable, well-marketed wellness platforms are useful context: there is consumer demand and willingness to pay for "well-being" experiences — but that market does not serve precarious or in-crisis music workers, and is not trying to. The NPO's distinct value proposition (free or subsidized, music-literate, care-grade, not lifestyle-grade) is clearer once the wellness-marketplace space next door is named and set aside.2

What this study deliberately does not claim: any music-MH impact, any reach figures, any nonprofit status, any founders beyond the two named, any funding numbers. None exist in the sources; inventing them would be the exact failure this page exists to avoid.
