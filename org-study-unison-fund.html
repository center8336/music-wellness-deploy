THE UNISON FUND

Org Study · Est. 2011

↓ Scroll

Why this case matters

Backline was a 2019 cold start; MusiCares was built inside the Recording Academy; Music Minds Matter was incubated inside a century-old benevolent fund. Unison is a fourth pattern — and arguably the closest structural analogue to what a Taiwan NPO would have to build from scratch: a national, industry-created safety-net charity that pairs an outsourced 24/7 counselling EAP with emergency cash relief paid straight to creditors, funded by a deliberate three-way blend of major-label money, foundation philanthropy, and government grants — and without a large endowment. It is the "national music-charity" model (alongside MusiCares in the US, Support Act in Australia, MusicHelps in New Zealand, Help Musicians in the UK) executed in a mid-sized market that, like Taiwan, has no domestic music endowment to draw on.1

01 — Profile

The heartbeat of the Canadian music community1

Unison is an independent national Canadian charity, created by the music industry for the industry, to ensure that "the people who make up the Canadian music business never face times of crisis alone." It is the brainchild of Jodie Ferneyhough and Catharine Saxberg, who set out to convert ad-hoc benefit-concert charity into a permanent, professionalized fund. A board was assembled in 2010, the fund was formed in 2011, and two service lines followed in sequence — counselling in 2012 and a financial-assistance program in 2015.2

02 — The distinctive model

Industry-created, outsourced, crisis-gated

Four structural choices define Unison and set it apart from the other organizations in this series.

CHOICE 01

Created by the industry, as a standing institution

Unlike a label-internal program (MusiCares inside the Recording Academy) or a spin-out from an old benevolent fund (Music Minds Matter from Help Musicians), Unison was purpose-built in 2010–11 as an independent national charity the whole Canadian industry funds collectively. Its founding capital came from a trade body (Music Canada) and a label/foundation (Slaight) — not from one company's marketing budget.2

CHOICE 02

Counselling is outsourced wholesale to a commercial EAP

Unison does not employ a clinical workforce. It buys a seat at TELUS Health (the former LifeWorks / Morneau Shepell EAP) and extends that corporate Employee Assistance Program — 24/7, multilingual, phone/in-person/virtual — to every registered member as if Unison were their employer. A small charity delivers enterprise-grade clinical access by renting it.3

CHOICE 03

Emergency cash is crisis-gated and paid to creditors

The Emergency Financial Support Program is deliberately narrow: crisis only, medical/rent/basic-living priorities, ≥5 consecutive years in the industry, and — critically — "all approved support will be paid directly to a creditor/third party … Financial support will NOT be sent directly to applicants." A fraud- and dignity-control design choice a startup can copy verbatim.4

CHOICE 04

A blended, endowment-free funding base

Unison runs on three legs — major-label/industry money, foundation philanthropy, and government grants — rather than on investment income from a reserve (the Help Musicians model) or artist-led galas alone (the MusiCares model). It has no large endowment to spend down; it must re-raise its operating base each year.5

03 — Financial profile

Cumulative impact, with the annual filing flagged pending2

Unison publishes cumulative impact figures rather than a posted annual report. On its own history page it states that over $24 million has been distributed since 2015 (the majority between 2020 and 2023), and that it has financially supported over 10,000 music professionals. During COVID-19 it recorded a 3,000% increase in applications in 2020 and disbursed more than $3.5 million, helping more than 3,500 Canadians through 2021. These are organization-stated cumulative totals — ⚠️ org-stated, useful for scale but not a substitute for audited annual statements.2

04 — Programs & impact

Rented clinic, creditor-paid relief, and built-in evidence

  • The 24/7 EAP

    Counselling & Health Solutions

    Registered members get free access to TELUS Health (formerly LifeWorks): 24/7 support for mental, financial, physical and emotional wellbeing — by phone, in person, or online — with crisis callers reaching a counsellor immediately on 1-855-9UNISON. Service is in English and French plus 250 additional languages via LanguageLine, with TTY, an online portal, and the TELUS Health One app; eligible dependent family members can be invited on. Registration is free, with no dues or fees. Supported by RBC Foundation and The Azrieli Foundation.3

  • Crisis relief

    Emergency Financial Support Program

    For applicants with ≥5 consecutive years in the industry facing an emergency; prioritises medical support, basic living expenses, and rent. Crisis only — explicitly not for unemployment, ongoing debt, recurring/long-term situations, or business expenses. All approved support is paid directly to a creditor/third party (landlord, medical supplier), never to the applicant. Multiple applications are allowed; annual funding limits apply. A baseline test requires ≥55% of income from music plus Canadian status, residency, banking and tax.4

  • Veterans

    The Slaight Family Foundation Legacy Program

    For veterans contributing ≥10 years who are over 60 and/or coping with unexpected or chronic illness — targeted at those struggling to maintain an adequate standard of living, needing medical intervention, or living with mental-health issues. A separate Grocery Gift Card Support Program addresses food insecurity for music workers in unemployment or underemployment.9

  • Sept 2024

    Soundcheck — the field-evidence partnership

    Unison, SOCAN and Revelios launched Soundcheck: Mental Health in the Canadian Music Industry, described as the first national study of its kind. Its widely-reported finding — that over half of respondents (52%) had felt life wasn't worth living — came from the study's 2025 preliminary release; the final national report, with 1,250+ participants, followed in early 2026. For this project, Soundcheck matters because it is an industry-funded charity generating the demand evidence that justifies its own services — a feedback loop a Taiwan NPO should design in from the start.12

05 — The outsourced-EAP model

Clinical reach without a clinical payroll

Unison's most replicable idea is that it does not build a clinical service; it rents one. By extending TELUS Health's corporate EAP to all registered members, a small charity offers genuine 24/7, multilingual, family-inclusive counselling and crisis support across an entire country — coverage a charity its size could never staff directly. The trade-off is real (no proprietary clinical model, dependence on a commercial vendor's quality and pricing), but the capital efficiency is decisive for a launch-stage organization: clinical access becomes a procurement decision, not a hiring one.3

The same logic runs through the cash program. By paying approved relief directly to creditors and gating it to genuine crises with documentary proof, Unison keeps administrative discretion tight and fraud exposure low without a large caseworker team. The model is "thin charity, rented capacity" — the opposite of an institution-heavy welfare body.4

07 — Strengths

What works

Capital-efficient clinical reach

Renting TELUS Health's 24/7 multilingual EAP gives a small charity national, enterprise-grade counselling and crisis access without a clinical payroll — the single most copyable element of the model.3

Genuinely blended, government-validated base

Three active legs — major labels, foundations, and federal + provincial government — mean no single funder failure is fatal, and the government grants are external validation that the need is real.6

Fraud- and dignity-controlled relief

Paying approved aid directly to creditors, gating to documented crises, and capping annual funding keeps administration lean and misuse low — a design a startup can adopt on day one.4

Industry-embedded governance

A board chaired from the live sector and seating RBC and Slaight ties the fund's leadership to its money and its constituency, easing fundraising and legitimacy.13

Evidence generation built in

The Soundcheck partnership turns the charity into a producer of national mental-health data, strengthening both its case for funding and the field's understanding of need.12

Scale relative to market

Over $24M distributed and 10,000+ workers supported since 2015 is meaningful penetration of a mid-sized national music workforce — proof the model works at country scale outside the US.2

08 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

No endowment — perpetual re-raising

Unison must rebuild its operating base every year; it has no investment reserve to smooth income shocks the way Help Musicians can. This is the price of the blended model.2

Government dependence is cyclical

A large share of the 2020–2023 surge was pandemic-era government money. As emergency programs wind down, the structural question is whether industry and philanthropy can sustain the elevated demand the pandemic exposed.6

Vendor dependence for the core service

Outsourcing counselling to TELUS Health means service quality, pricing, and continuity sit with a commercial vendor Unison does not control — efficient, but a single point of dependency for the program members value most.3

Thin public financial transparency

Unison publishes a periodic Impact Report (cumulative figures) but no audited annual financial statements, and the authoritative T3010 could not be retrieved this pass (CRA maintenance window; mirror JS-gated). Self-reported totals are not a substitute for posted audited accounts.7

Narrow eligibility excludes the most precarious

The ≥5-years-in-industry / ≥55%-income-from-music gates, and the explicit exclusion of unemployment and "recurring" hardship, mean the newest and most casual workers — often the most precarious — may fall outside the emergency program.4

Name-collision and brand confusion

Sharing "Unison" with an Alberta seniors' charity, a UK union, and a US fintech created a real fabrication hazard in this very study and dilutes searchability — a cautionary tale for any new org choosing a name.7

No Asia-Pacific footprint

Like MusiCares, Help Musicians and Music Minds Matter, Unison is single-country (Canada) — the field-wide gap this project tracks.1

09 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. Rent the clinic; don't build it

    This is the headline lesson. Unison delivers national 24/7, multilingual counselling by extending a commercial EAP (TELUS Health) to its members. A Taiwan NPO should likewise procure clinical capacity — contract an established EAP or counselling provider and extend it to registered music workers — rather than hire a clinical team. It converts the hardest, most expensive part of the service into a vendor contract a small org can manage.3

  2. Build the funding base on three legs from day one

    Unison runs on industry money + foundation philanthropy + government grants, with no endowment — precisely the situation a Taiwan startup faces. The replicable sequence is Unison's own: seed with trade-body + patron commitments (Music Canada + Slaight, 2011), add foundation program-sponsors (RBC, Azrieli), and pursue government cultural-sector grants (the Taiwan Ministry of Culture is the analogue to Canadian Heritage). Seat each funder's representative on the board to lock in the relationship.5

  3. Copy the crisis-gated, pay-the-creditor relief design verbatim

    Unison's emergency program — documented crisis only, prioritised to medical/rent/basics, capped annually, and paid directly to landlords and suppliers rather than to applicants — is a ready-made template that controls fraud and administrative load without a large team. A Taiwan NPO can adopt this rulebook on launch.4

  4. Distinguish "register" from "apply," and make registration free

    Unison's two-tier access — free registration unlocks counselling and the mailing list; financial aid is a separate, evidenced application — lowers the barrier to the help most people actually need (someone to talk to) while keeping cash disbursement disciplined.3

  5. Generate your own demand evidence early

    Unison co-produced the first national mental-health study of Canada's music industry, which both justifies its services and advances the field. A Taiwan NPO should partner with a local university or research firm to run an equivalent baseline survey — it is the strongest possible grant-application, and the field has none yet for Taiwan.12

  6. Don't rely on a pandemic-style government windfall as the base case

    Much of Unison's 2020–2023 scale was emergency government money. A Taiwan org should treat one-off government relief as a bonus, not the foundation, and plan a sustainable mix it can hold when emergency programs end.6

  7. Choose a distinctive, unambiguous name

    Unison's brand collides with a seniors' charity, a union, and a fintech — a hazard that nearly caused a financial-figure error in this study. A Taiwan NPO should pick a name with no domestic collision so funders, applicants, and researchers can find it cleanly.7

  8. Publish audited annual accounts even when not strictly required

    Unison's reliance on cumulative self-reported totals, with no posted annual report, is a transparency gap. A new org that posts audited statements from year one builds the funder trust that a blended, re-raised-annually model depends on.7
