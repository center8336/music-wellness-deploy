MUSICIANS' UNION OF JAPAN日本音楽家ユニオン

Org Study · Est. 1983

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

MusiCares and the national funds are charities that raise money to deliver service. The Musicians' Union of Japan is neither a charity nor a mental-health body — it is a labour union whose welfare arm is a self-financed mutual-aid fund (自主共済). It pays small, fixed lump sums when a member is hospitalised, dies, marries, has a child, or loses a home to fire or flood, subsidises health check-ups, brokers entertainers' National Health Insurance, and gives free legal and tax counsel. That is a textbook floor: rights and welfare for everyone who pays in. What it pointedly does not run is a counselling line, a clinic, or any mental-health programme. This study asks the question a "floor" invites — what does a disciplined music-worker safety net look like, and where does it stop? The answer is: it stops exactly where the room (the service) would begin.

01 — Profile

A union, not a charity1

The Musicians' Union of Japan was formed in October 1983 through the merger of two predecessor bodies — the Japan Performers' Association (日本演奏家協会) and the Japan Musicians' Labour Union (日本音楽家労働組合). Counting from those 1970s predecessors, MUJ describes about 50 years of work on behalf of Japanese musicians. It holds the legal personality of a labour union, and musicians join it as individuals, not through an employer.19

That single fact separates MUJ from every U.S. organisation in this series: it is a union, and its welfare programmes are an extension of collective labour representation, not philanthropy. MUJ negotiates performance fees with NHK, commercial broadcasters, and the recording industry; works on orchestra working conditions; and pursues secondary-use royalties. The welfare benefits sit on top of that bargaining identity.2

02 — Floor vs room

A floor that stops where the room begins

The structural distinction of MUJ is not any single benefit. It is that the union builds a floor — a self-financed welfare base of rights and lump-sum benefits every dues-paying member stands on — and deliberately goes no further. There is no counselling service, no clinic, no mental-health programme above the floor. The diagram makes the shape literal: a solid welfare base, and an empty "room" where a service would sit.

The welfare floor — and the empty service room above it

Everything that follows is one of the four bricks in that floor — and the conspicuous absence above them is the subject of §4 and the lesson of §7.

03 — The mutual-aid fund

自主共済 — the self-financed core3

The core of the floor is MUJ's self-run mutual-aid fund (自主共済) — a member-financed pool that pays fixed lump sums, not health services, when a defined life event occurs. Benefits apply to members with at least six months' continuous membership, and a claim must be filed within one year. The union's English summary puts the mutual-aid (plus a financial-assist system) at a budget of about ¥40 million a year.2

The death, marriage, and childbirth benefits extend to lifetime full members (終身正会員); the income-replacement-style benefits are for active general members. The amounts are deliberately modest — this is a solidarity top-up, not insurance. Its purpose is to make membership tangibly worth paying for and to soften the sharpest financial shocks of a freelance career.3

04 — Health (and what is missing)

A checkup subsidy and brokered insurance — but no clinic, and no mental health

MUJ's health offer has two real parts, both welfare-broker functions rather than care delivery.

Health-checkup subsidy (健康診断)4

When a regional branch organises a health check-up, MUJ subsidises the member's and family's cost on a tiered scale.

Brokered entertainers' National Health Insurance (国民健康保険)5

MUJ is the route into two arts-sector National Health Insurance societies — the Tokyo Entertainers' National Health Insurance Society (東京芸能人国民健康保険組合, established 1952) and the Osaka Culture & Arts National Health Insurance Society (大阪文化芸能国民健康保険組合). These give the same statutory 70% coverage as municipal NHI but with more favourable childbirth, childcare, funeral, and human-dock (comprehensive checkup) benefits, plus free general and adult-disease screenings; members pay premiums through the union. Alongside these, the member-services menu lists labour-bank loans, cooperative insurance, instrument insurance, and free legal and tax consultation.6

No MH service — verified, not assumed

A complete read of MUJ's member-services menu and activity pages finds welfare, insurance-broking, and rights/contract help — and nothing branded as mental health, counselling (カウンセリング), or psychological support. The only "consultation (相談)" offers are labour consultation (労働相談) — for sudden dismissal, unpaid performance fees, and contract breaches, handled by a desk plus a network of advisory lawyers, confidentiality assured7 — and the free legal/tax consultation above. There is no clinical or emotional-support arm.

Accuracy flag — the “24-hour MH helpline” is refuted

A claim circulating on secondary blogs that MUJ operates a 24-hour mental-health / こころ helpline is not supported by any MUJ primary source. No such line appears anywhere on muj.or.jp, and no MH / こころ / メンタル page exists in the site's archived URL inventory. The 24-hour こころ / 電話健康相談 lines that exist in Japan belong to unrelated health-insurance societies (健康保険組合) and public mutual-aid funds — not to MUJ. MUJ should not be cited as running a mental-health helpline.6

05 — International standing

FIM's Japanese affiliate8

MUJ is the Japanese affiliate of the International Federation of Musicians (FIM). The union's own about-page describes joining FIM "as the sole organisation representing Asia (アジアを代表する唯一の団体)" and serving as an executive-committee member country (執行委員国).1 FIM's own membership directory independently lists MUJ under "Asia and Pacific → Japan → MUJ," and one of MUJ's listed advisers is John Smith, then FIM President — underscoring the depth of the tie.2

One claim phrased carefully

"Sole organisation representing Asia" is the union's own framing and reflects its historical position. FIM's current directory groups several unions under "Asia and Pacific" — Australia (MEAA), India (MFI), and Iran (IMU) alongside Japan (MUJ). So the accurate statement is that MUJ is FIM's Japanese affiliate and a longstanding Asian member sitting as an executive-committee member country — how the union itself describes its role — not, in the present tense, the only Asian union in FIM. The bare membership and Asia-Pacific grouping are confirmed by FIM directly.8

06 — Appraisal

What works, and where it stops

A genuine self-financed floor

The mutual-aid fund is funded by members for members — no celebrity gala, no wealthy parent body, no grant cycle. A union dues base is about as durable a funding engine as music-worker welfare gets.

Welfare bundled with bargaining power

Because MUJ is a union, benefits ride on real leverage: fee agreements with broadcasters and the record industry, orchestra working-condition work, neighbouring-rights royalties. Members get a safety net and a negotiator.

Breadth of practical help

Checkup subsidy, brokered NHI, loans, instrument insurance, free legal/tax counsel — an unglamorous but coherent stack of things a freelance musician actually needs.

Institutional embeddedness

Affiliation with GEIDANKYO and MIC, and an executive role in FIM, give MUJ cultural-policy standing well beyond its ~5,200 members.

No “room” — no MH service at all

The benefits are financial and procedural. A member in psychological distress finds labour consultation and a lawyer network, not a counsellor or a clinic. This is the central gap for a project about mental health.

Lump sums are small, and gated

¥10,000 for a hospitalisation, ¥50,000 for a death — solidarity gestures on a ~¥40M/year budget — and benefits require 6 months' (some 10 years') continuous membership, protecting dues-payers rather than the broader precariat.

07 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. A membership mutual-aid fund is a credible, self-renewing floor — copy the structure, not the amounts

    MUJ shows that even modest, fixed lump-sum benefits, financed by members, create durable value and a concrete reason to join. A Taiwan music body could run a comparable solidarity fund (hospitalisation, bereavement, disaster) on a transparent published schedule — funding that does not hinge on a gala or a grant cycle.3

  2. But a floor is not a room — the MH service is exactly what MUJ does not provide, so it is the open lane

    MUJ proves a well-run welfare/rights body can exist for decades with no MH offer. A Taiwan NPO should not assume the union/welfare layer will grow a counselling arm on its own; the "room" — counselling, referral, peer support, crisis help — is a distinct build, and the unmet need is visible precisely because the floor stops short of it.6

  3. Bundle welfare with rights — or partner with a body that already does

    MUJ's benefits work because the union also bargains fees and fights unpaid-wage and dismissal cases. In Taiwan — where the nearest analogue is the broad-arts 藝創工會 rather than a music-specific union — a music-MH NPO might partner with a labour/rights body so members get both representation and care, rather than trying to be both at once.7

  4. Broker, don't build, where a statutory system already exists

    MUJ does not run hospitals; it brokers entry into arts-sector National Health Insurance and subsidises check-ups. A lean NPO adds the most value by connecting musicians to Taiwan's National Health Insurance and existing mental-health resources (and subsidising access) rather than duplicating clinical infrastructure.5

  5. Name the eligibility floor deliberately

    MUJ's 6-month / 10-year continuity gates suit a stable dues-paying union but exclude the newest and most precarious. A Taiwan MH NPO that wants to reach emerging and part-time musicians should set inclusion criteria around recent music-related activity, not tenure.3

  6. Use international standing as leverage

    MUJ's FIM executive role gives a small union outsized policy voice. A Taiwan music-MH body should seek analogous network membership — regional or international music-welfare and mental-health coalitions — early; credibility and comparative evidence travel through those ties.8
