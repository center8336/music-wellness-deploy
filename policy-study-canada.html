Framing

Why Canada, Why Policy

A Floor With a Gap

Part of the project's country-parity set: each country is read on two axes — the organizations on the ground (Org Study) and the system around them (Policy Study). Canada is the companion to one org study, Unison Fund — and the analogue this whole project has been circling toward. The Unison study flagged Canada as the closest structural match to Taiwan in the set: a mid-sized market, no domestic music endowment, and — unlike the United States — a universal public health floor already in place.

The United States showed a room substituting for an absent floor. Canada is the mirror case. It has the floor the US lacks — provincial Medicare, universal and tax-funded — and still runs an industry charity, Unison, whose 2024 budget took exactly zero dollars from government.11

So Canada is not a story about a missing floor. It is a story about a floor with a mental-health-shaped gap cut into it — and the room built to fill that gap. Reading the gap precisely is the whole point, because Taiwan's floor has the same shape.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · The Floor

A Floor, and Two Gaps

Canada has the floor the United States never built. The Canada Health Act (1984) ties full federal transfers to five criteria a provincial plan must meet — public administration, comprehensiveness, universality, portability, accessibility — and names its objective as protecting the "physical and mental well-being of residents… without financial or other barriers."1 On paper, the mandate names mental health.

Gap one — it covers the psychiatrist, not the psychotherapist

The binding definition undercuts the mandate. The Act insures "medically necessary" hospital and physician services — and that phrasing, not the preamble, is what provinces are paid to deliver.1 In mental health it draws a sharp line: a psychiatrist is a physician, so psychiatric care is covered; a psychologist or psychotherapist in private practice is not. The Canadian Medical Association puts it bluntly — "Canada doesn't have universal mental health care" — listing private-practice therapy among the services patients pay for out of pocket, "$125 to $225" a visit, with most prescription drugs outside hospital also uncovered.2 The hour with a therapist — most of what this population needs — sits outside the floor.

The trend is not toward closing it. The national strategy has called since 2012 for raising mental health's share of spending from 7% to 9%;3 a dedicated Canada Mental Health Transfer promised in 2021 was never stood up, and the 2017 federal–provincial mental-health agreements were, as of Budget 2025, set to expire with no announced replacement.5

Gap two — the income floor is cut to the shape of an employee

Employment Insurance protects insurable employment — employees who lose a job. Most working musicians are self-employed, and regular EI does not reach them at all. Since 2010 they can opt in to EI special benefits — pay premiums, wait 12 months, then claim six life-event benefits (sickness up to 26 weeks, parental, caregiving) at up to 55% of earnings, a 2026 maximum near $729 a week.4 But the opt-in covers illness and caregiving, not a cancelled tour, and must be arranged in advance. Occupational coverage has the same opt-in character: Québec's CNESST states plainly that self-employed workers are not automatically covered for a work accident or occupational disease — they must apply and pay for optional "protection personnelle."10

Section 02 · What the State Builds

Rights and Output, Rarely Welfare

What the Canadian state does build for artists falls into two piles, and welfare is in neither: a large pile of output funding and a smaller pile of bargaining rights.

01 Arts money funds the work, not the worker 2024

The Canada Council for the Arts exists, by statute, "to foster and promote the study and enjoyment of, and the production of works in, the arts"; its FY2023–24 appropriation was about $369 million, roughly 88% (about $326M) disbursed as grants — and a full-text scan of its Act finds no mention of artist health, welfare, or mental health anywhere.6 Provincial councils add a second tier (Québec's CALQ, the Ontario and BC arts councils), and the federal Canada Music Fund (via FACTOR and Musicaction) invests in recording, touring, and marketing.7 14 This money funds the work. It does not fund the worker.

02 Federal Status of the Artist Act — rights, not benefits 1992

The federal Status of the Artist Act (1992) recognizes self-employed artists' freedom of association and the right of their associations to bargain "scale agreements," administered by the Canada Industrial Relations Board. But its reach is narrow: it binds only federal-jurisdiction producers — the CBC, the National Film Board, federal museums — and it confers bargaining rights, not health, welfare, or pensions.8

03 Québec's Bill 35 — the exception that proves the rule 2022

Québec's Bill 35 (2022) did something no other instrument in this project does: it wrote a workplace-health protection directly into artist-status law. A new chapter gives every artist the right to a work environment "exempt de harcèlement psychologique," obliges producers to adopt a prevention policy, and — the striking part — lets the Tribunal administratif du travail order a producer to "financer le soutien psychologique requis par l'artiste": to fund the psychological support the artist needs.9 It is channelled through harassment law, not social insurance, and it is one province only — but it is the closest thing in Canada to a state-built artist well-being protection.

Section 03 · The Charitable Room

Privately Built, on Top of the Floor

Into the gap steps Unison (formerly the Unison Benevolent Fund), Canada's music-industry safety-net charity — and the org study this report companions. Formed in 2011 "by the industry, for the industry," it runs the two programs that map exactly onto the two holes in the floor.

01 Unison — the EAP gig workers don't have, plus the therapy the floor omits

Its Counselling & Health Assistance program — a free, confidential, 24/7 line (with TELUS Health), open to any registered Canadian music-industry worker and their family — is the employee-assistance plan a self-employed musician otherwise lacks. A separate Extended Therapy Support Program goes further and pays a member's own therapist directly for longer-term psychotherapy or psychiatry — buying the talk therapy Medicare omits. The second program, Emergency Financial Support, pays rent or medical costs straight to creditors during a crisis — the income shock EI's employee-shaped door misses.11

02 A layered room beneath it

The AFC (incorporated 1958) is the entertainment-wide safety net, with a mental-health-and-wellness track across the whole sector;12 Over the Bridge (2017) is a smaller music-specific non-profit focused on addiction and mental health;13 and the Canadian Mental Health Association, the national general-MH charity (BounceBack, peer support, the 988 line), is the public backbone the industry bodies refer into.2

Section 04 · The Mental-Health Layer

Better Organized Than Measured

The burden these services respond to is, in Canada, better organized than it is measured. The honest starting point: there is no peer-reviewed, probability-sample prevalence study of Canadian musicians' or performing-arts workers' mental health. Stating that plainly matters (Rule 4) — the absence is a real gap, not evidence of low burden.

What Canada has is two things, each useful and each limited. The largest is the Soundcheck national report (Revelios, with Unison and SOCAN, 2026) — a bilingual survey of 1,216 participants in which 86% reported personally experiencing mental-health challenges and 84% reported financial precarity. But it is grey literature: a self-selected convenience sample, its banded "50–86%" mixing several items rather than one instrumented rate.15 The second is the only peer-reviewed Canadian dataset: Barbeau et al. (2024), a UQAM survey of 1,618 Canadian musicians using validated instruments, which found professional and post-secondary musicians scoring lower on well-being than amateurs — real and peer-reviewed, but a well-being study, not a prevalence one.16

For an actual prevalence magnitude the field still borrows from abroad, and it should be flagged as borrowed: the most defensible are Norwegian (Vaag et al., 2016 — musicians' higher anxiety and depression than the general workforce) and Australian (Kenny et al., 2014 — roughly a third of an orchestral near-census screening positive for depression and social phobia), with UK and Danish surveys pointing the same way.17 The signal is consistent across every country that has looked: the burden is large. The measurement, in Canada specifically, is not yet there.

Part B — Appraisal

Section 05 · Reading the Model

A Floor Worth Having, With a Gap Worth Naming

What Canada gets right is that it has a floor at all

This is not small, and the contrast with the United States makes it concrete: a universal, tax-funded health system means no Canadian musician is one diagnosis away from ruin for hospital or physician care — the single biggest welfare asset in this study. Québec goes furthest of any jurisdiction in the project, writing a fundable psychological-support protection into artist-status law.9 Canada proves the floor and the room can coexist — public coverage does not make a charity redundant, because the two cover different things.

However, the floor has a gap exactly where this population lives

Medicare covers the psychiatrist and not the psychotherapist, and talk therapy is most of what the evidence says musicians need — so the universal system is, for practical purposes, not universal for the relevant service.2 The income floor is built for employees and the workforce is self-employed; the occupational floor must be opted into; the arts money funds output and names no welfare line.4 6 That is why Unison exists in a country that already has Medicare — and why its $0 government funding is less a triumph of private generosity than a measure of what the floor leaves uncovered.11

Read the evidence with one caution

The Canadian numbers are a convenience-sample industry survey (Soundcheck) and a peer-reviewed well-being study (Barbeau) — directionally consistent with a large burden, but neither a probability-sample prevalence estimate; the magnitudes the field quotes are imported from Norway, Australia, the UK, and Denmark.15 17 The burden is real and consistent; the precise Canadian rate is, honestly, unmeasured.

Verdict

Canada is the project's clearest floor-with-a-gap — a genuine universal health system with a mental-health-shaped hole, an income system cut to the shape of an employee, and a privately funded room built precisely around those edges. It is the optimistic half of the thesis the US stated darkly: proof that a floor is worth having, and a precise map of which gaps a room must still cover even after the floor is built.

Part C — For This Project

Section 06 · Translation — the Closest Analogue

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

Canada is the closest structural analogue to Taiwan in the whole set — mid-sized market, no music endowment, a universal health floor already in place. These lessons carry extra weight: less an adaptation than a near-direct read.

3.1 You already have the floor — copy Canada, not the US

Taiwan's 全民健康保險 (健保) is universal, like Canadian Medicare and unlike the American patchwork. So the US "room-without-a-floor" model is the wrong template and Canada is the right one: the NPO's job is not to substitute for public coverage but to patch the specific gaps the floor leaves. Build to complement 健保, and defend its universality as the asset that lets a small NPO stay small.

3.2 The gap to target is the same as Canada's — talk therapy

Canadian Medicare covers the psychiatrist but not the private psychotherapist, and 健保 has the same shape — psychiatric care covered, 心理試商 / 心理治療 largely out-of-pocket. Unison's highest-value services — the 24/7 line and the program that pays a member's own therapist directly — exist to buy exactly the care the floor omits. The NPO's flagship should be funding and navigating the therapy the floor does not cover, not duplicating the hospital care it already does.

3.3 Be the employee-assistance plan gig workers don't have

Unison's counselling line is, functionally, the workplace EAP a self-employed musician lacks because they have no employer. Taiwan's project-based performers sit outside employment-tied 勞保 / 職災保險 the same way. A free, register-and-you're-in 24/7 line is low-capital, high-reach, and fills a gap that exists by definition for the self-employed — the cheapest high-value piece to launch first.

3.4 Don't wait for arts money to fund welfare — it won't

Canada Council and FACTOR fund creation and production and name no welfare line; the welfare room is funded separately, by industry. Taiwan's 文化部 and 文化內容策進院 are structured the same way — output funders. Build the welfare room beside the output funders, not inside them.

3.5 A mid-sized market with no endowment can still fund the room — from industry

Unison runs a multi-million-dollar program on $0 government money, funded by SOCAN, labels, and live-music companies. For Taiwan, which likewise has no music endowment, the model is fundable from industry contributions — the collecting society (MÜST), labels, streaming platforms, live promoters — without waiting for either an endowment or a government line.

3.6 There is a policy ask worth importing — Québec's funded-support clause

Most of Canada's floor leaves welfare to charity, but Québec's Bill 35 shows a state can write funded psychological support into artist-status law. As Taiwan develops its own artist-status and arts-promotion framework (文化蛇術夢助及促進條例), the NPO has a concrete, in-force clause to point legislators toward — not a hypothetical.

3.7 Generate the evidence — Canada hasn't either

Even Canada — universal floor, mature charity, a 1,200-person industry survey — has no probability-sample prevalence study of its musicians' mental health, and borrows its magnitudes from Norway and Australia. A credible Taiwan survey would not be catching up; it would do something Canada itself has not done, and give the NPO a citation to take to 文化部 and 衛福部.

Limitations

  1. The Canadian MH figures are a convenience-sample industry survey (Soundcheck) and a peer-reviewed well-being study (Barbeau); they establish a large burden, not a representative prevalence. The magnitudes the field quotes are imported from Norway, Australia, the UK, and Denmark.
  2. Several facts are moving — EI parameters are 2026 figures; the Canada Mental Health Transfer / 2017 bilateral cliff was unresolved as of Budget 2025; the report reads the system as of June 2026.
  3. Canada is federated: health coverage, workers' comp (CNESST), and artist-status law (Québec's Bill 35) vary by province. This study reads the federal floor plus Québec as the most-developed provincial case, not all ten provinces and three territories.
  4. The non-existence of a standalone Canada Mental Health Transfer and of a federal music-worker MH program are negative claims (Rule 4); absence is not proof of non-existence.
  5. Unison / AFC / Over the Bridge scale figures are partly org-reported (annual/impact reports, press) alongside the audited revenue lines.
  6. No direct contact with any agency, council, union, tribunal, or charity; no primary interviews. French-language Québec sources (LégisQuébec, Éditeur officiel, CNESST, TAT) were read directly for Section 02 and the CNESST item.
