POLICY STUDY · Six-Region Scan

Policy Study

One question, asked of one jurisdiction at a time: how does a country actually protect the mental health and welfare of its music workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies? Each scan reads a different lever. Japan reached for labour law; Thailand for industrial policy; the EU for supranational coordination. The point of comparison is a working blueprint for a Taiwan music mental-health NPO.

The scans

Region · lever · takeaway
  1. 01 Japan Labour law Rights over charity. Japan extends workers' accident insurance to performers (2021) and enacts a Freelance Protection Act (2024, now enforced) — a natural experiment in whether labour law can do what a charitable safety net does elsewhere. Read the scan →
  2. 02 Thailand Industrial policy Industry before safety net. Thailand builds a KOCCA-style soft-power export engine while the workers powering it remain overwhelmingly informal and outside full social security — a test of whether growing the industry protects the people inside it. Read the scan →
  3. 03 European Union Coordination Coordination without competence. Reports, a Parliament resolution, a €1.23bn mental-health communication, and a proposed Artists' Charter — a thick layer of problem-definition and soft law, but no treaty power to mandate the fix in any member state. Read the scan →

The levers, compared

What each buys · what it misses

Labour law Japan

BuysEnforceable rights — workers' accident insurance extended to performers (2021) and a Freelance Act (2024) now backed by real enforcement (first JFTC action, 2025).

MissesNo mental-health programme of its own; a right on paper still needs a worker able to invoke it, and the prevalence evidence stays thin.

Industrial policy Thailand

BuysScale and momentum — a KOCCA-style soft-power apparatus growing the creative economy as a national export engine.

MissesLeaves the workforce informal — largely outside the Social Security Fund's full protection, with no dedicated mental-health provision.

Coordination EU

BuysProblem-definition and money — expert reports, a Parliament resolution, a €1.23bn mental-health communication, a proposed Artists' Charter.

MissesNo competence to mandate the fix — culture and health stay national, so Brussels can name a problem it cannot compel a member state to solve.

For Taiwan, the open question: which lever — or which blend — fits a market that already has a creators' union (藝創工會) but no music-specific mental-health layer?

Each scan is a self-contained reading of one jurisdiction's system — what it does, what it leaves uncovered, and what a Taiwan music mental-health NPO could borrow. The throughline is the lever: law, charity, industrial policy, or coordination are different answers to the same question, and Taiwan will have to choose its own.
