MUSICHELPS

Org Study · Est. 2012

The Minimum Viable National Charity — New Zealand

↓ Scroll

Why this case matters

MusicHelps is the second of the only two full-scale, independent, music-specific charities this project's scans have found in Asia-Pacific — the other is Support Act, studied 2026-07-06. Where Support Act shows the model at A$4.7M with state grants and ~16 staff, MusicHelps shows the minimum viable version: a national 24/7 wellbeing service, a benevolent fund, and a grants programme run on ~NZ$300–400k a year with one full-time person. For a Taiwan NPO that will start small, this is the more proximate blueprint — including its failure modes, which the register data makes unusually visible.16

The rename that states the theory of change2

The 2018 rebrand from "NZ Music Foundation" to "MusicHelps" was framed by the org as making it "more direct, more impactful" — and the new name reads two ways at once: help for music people and music as help for everyone. That ambiguity is not cosmetic; it runs through the whole organization (see §5) and is the single most instructive tension in this case.

02 — The model

One Desk, Three Levers345

MusicHelps delivers a national footprint with almost no organization behind it, by pulling three levers a small charity can actually hold:

LEVER 01

An outsourced 24/7 clinical layer

The MusicHelps Wellbeing Service (0508 MUSICHELPS) offers free, confidential, round-the-clock support to NZ music people in crisis. The clinical delivery is not in-house: the org's own privacy policy states the majority of the service is managed by Southern Cross Wellness Partners, a commercial workplace-health provider. NZ Musician reported the 2016 launch as a "world-first" wellbeing service for musicians (⚠️ outlet's framing). The charity buys a national counselling capability it could never staff — the same rent-the-EAP pattern as Unison Fund (TELUS Health). (Support Act's helpline delivery contract is not public; its own study leaves that ❓.)3

LEVER 02

A gated benevolent fund

Crisis financial assistance for music workers with 10+ years of industry service, committee-approved, 3–6 week turnaround. Org-claimed past-year scale: close to NZ$40,000 across 8 workers (⚠️ as-reported). The 10-year gate is strict — it rations a small pool toward career members, at the cost of excluding emerging workers.4

LEVER 03

Regranting

Two grant rounds a year (March and September) fund other charities that use music to improve wellbeing — the March 2026 round lists 19 named recipients, from the Auckland Philharmonia Trust and Chamber Music New Zealand to hospice, brain-injury, and children's-health trusts. Org-claimed cumulative reach: "over 90,000 people"; April 2026 milestone: NZ$1M in cumulative funding since 2012.5

QUIETER LEVER

MusicHelps Legacy

A fourth, quieter lever — free wills through partner Footprint — builds a bequest pipeline at near-zero cost.3

Three structural reads:

  • This is a regranting charity with a service attached, not the reverse. The org's own filing notes put programme spend at: grants NZ$137,258 + wellbeing service NZ$45,000 (counselling) + NZ$27,362 (emergency services) in FY2025; grants NZ$134,008 + wellbeing NZ$25,012 in FY2026. Grant-making outweighs the flagship helpline — ≈1.9:1 in FY2025 (counting counselling plus emergency services) and ≈5:1 in FY2026 — and the wellbeing line fell by half in FY2026.8
  • Income is one bad year from crisis. Donations halved from FY2023 (NZ$385,712) to FY2024 (NZ$176,833) and have not recovered to trend; FY2024 ran a deficit and FY2025 broke even to the dollar (−$334). Industry reporting has the org saying it "doesn't qualify for a lot of funding" and must "hustle" (⚠️ as-reported). The FY2026 surplus (+$112,562) restores the cushion — cash NZ$531,441, ≈1.85× annual expenditure — but the swing illustrates how thin the base is.67
  • The donor-facing "100%" claim does not map onto the filings. The About page states: "All administration costs are covered by the music industry itself. 100% of what you contribute will be applied directly to the music charities we support." The register shows fundraising expenses and NZ$83k–200k/yr of "all other expenditure" paid from general income, with no ring-fenced industry-funded admin pool visible in the Tier-3 filing. The claim may reflect an internal earmarking convention, but nothing in the public accounts substantiates it — a transparency gap for an org whose finances are otherwise exemplary.12
04 — COVID

Small-Org Speed13

MusicHelps' pandemic response shows the upside of having no bureaucracy. MusicHelpsLive launched 30 March 2020 — days into NZ's first lockdown — with a NZ$2M target for music workers whose livelihoods had collapsed (final tally not found in public sources, ❓). Emergency assistance grants went out at NZ$500 (2020), raised to NZ$1,000 during the 2021 Delta outbreak. The register shows the spike: FY2021 income NZ$1,039,793 — roughly triple a normal year — tapering through FY2022 (NZ$778,590) back to the NZ$300–400k band: the same windfall-and-cliff shape documented at Support Act, in miniature.613

05 — Dual mission

The Dual Mission Problem5

The grants list is the tell: of the 19 March 2026 recipients, most are not music-industry organizations — they are hospices, schools, disability trusts, and health charities running music-based programmes. MusicHelps operates two different theories of change under one brand:

  • "Help music people" — the Wellbeing Service and Benevolent Fund, serving industry workers (the Support Act/MusiCares theory).
  • "Music helps people" — the grants programme, funding music as an intervention for general wellbeing (a music-therapy funder's theory).

By spend, the second theory dominates (§3). Both are legitimate; but the headline impact number ("over 90,000 people") belongs almost entirely to the second, while the brand story and patron wattage suggest the first. A donor giving after a musician's suicide story funds, mostly, community music therapy. This is not misconduct — it is mission drift made visible by good disclosure, and the single most important design warning this case offers.58

06 — Governance

Governance11

Nine current trustees per the register — six shown on the org's About page: Anthony Healey (Chair; APRA AMCOS NZ), Cath Anderson (CEO, NZ Music Commission), Jo Oliver (CEO, Recorded Music NZ), Myra Hemara (Universal Music), plus artists Don McGlashan and Joel Little. Three trustees added 23 June 2026 (Davis, Ellis, Lock) do not yet appear on the site — a staleness gap. The board is the NZ music industry's plumbing: the royalty society, the government music office, the labels body, and a major label sit on it by office-holder. There is no CEO named anywhere (❓); filings are certified by an outsourced entertainment-industry accountant (⚠️ inferred from matching contact details). Founding chair Campbell Smith retired in 2021; the founders' generation has fully handed over.11

07 — Strengths

What works

The minimum viable national charity, demonstrated

One FTE + an outsourced clinical provider + a regranting programme = 24/7 national coverage, a crisis fund, and a field-funder role, on ~NZ$350k/yr. No other org in this series does so much with so little.68

Transparency by regulation, exploited well

Fourteen years of line-item filings, machine-readable, cross-checkable via two public endpoints. The org files promptly (FY2026 filed 8 July 2026, 99 days after year-end) and its narrative notes voluntarily disclose programme-level spend.68

Industry-embedded board as a fundraising machine

NZ Music T-Shirt Day (May, inside NZ Music Month, 100% net proceeds) runs on infrastructure the trustees control in their day jobs — APRA AMCOS, Recorded Music NZ, and the Music Commission all promote it. Patrons Neil Finn and Lorde price the brand far above its budget class.1114

Reserves discipline

Cash ≈ 1.1–1.85× annual expenditure across the period — a small org that has never bet the trust.9

Crisis speed

MusicHelpsLive shipped within days of lockdown; grant sizes flexed with variant waves.13

08 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

Key-person concentration

One paid role, engaged as a contractor for two of the four years examined. Illness, departure, or a contract dispute stops the organization.810

Revenue fragility

Donations halved in one year; grants received swing NZ$95k–148k; the whole income statement is smaller than Support Act's helpline budget.67

The "100%" claim

Unsubstantiated by the public accounts (§3). For a charity whose registry hygiene is otherwise excellent, this is an avoidable credibility exposure.12

Mission dilution by regranting

The flagship musician-facing wellbeing spend (NZ$45,000 → NZ$25,012) is small and shrinking while grants to general-population music programmes hold at ~NZ$135–165k (§5).8

No audit, no annual report

Tier-3 filing is legal and adequate, but the audit field is blank and no narrative annual report exists — at exactly the moment the org claims a NZ$1M cumulative milestone, there is no independent assurance behind any impact number.65

Governance-page staleness

Minor, but visible within weeks to any donor who checks the register.11

09 — Lessons for Taiwan

Lessons for a Taiwan-Based Music Mental Health NPO

  1. Budget realism: the entry ticket is ~NT$6–8M/yr, not NT$80M

    (NZ$300–400k at ≈19 TWD/NZD — computed, not quoted.) MusicHelps runs a country of 5.2M people on NZ$300–400k. Taiwan's music workforce is larger but the same architecture — one coordinator, an outsourced counselling contract, a small committee-gated fund — is a credible year-one shape. Support Act is the ten-year aspiration; MusicHelps is the day-one blueprint.68

  2. Rent the clinical layer; own the trust layer

    Southern Cross delivers the counselling; MusicHelps owns the brand, the 0508 number, and the industry relationships. A Taiwan NPO should contract an EAP/counselling network (張老師基金會-adjacent providers, or a commercial EAP) rather than hire clinicians — and keep the intake identity music-native.3

  3. Pick one theory of change and name the org for it

    MusicHelps' dual mission (§5) shows how "help musicians" quietly becomes "music helps everyone" — regranting is easier than service delivery, and general-wellbeing recipients are more numerous than musicians. A Taiwan founder must decide before branding: 幫助音樂人, or 用音樂幫助人. This project's thesis (the service room for music workers) is the first; drift toward the second should be a governance-level alarm, not an accident.5

  4. Put the industry's plumbing on the board, keep stars as patrons

    MÜST/ACMA-analog officeholders, a Ministry of Culture music-office representative, and a label-association seat replicate the Healey/Anderson/Oliver structure; celebrity belongs in the patron slot (Neil Finn/Lorde), where turnover doesn't destabilize governance.11

  5. One branded annual moment beats year-round asking

    T-Shirt Day inside NZ Music Month concentrates the entire year's public fundraising into one industry-amplified week. The Taiwan analog is obvious: a branded day inside 金曲獎 week or 貴人散步/LUCfest, with labels and streaming platforms as amplifiers.14

  6. File like it's marketing

    MusicHelps' register discipline — prompt, granular, narrative notes included — is what made this study possible and is itself a trust asset. A Taiwan NPO should publish 衛福部/內政部 filings and a simple annual accounting voluntarily from year one — and never print a "100% to the cause" claim its own filings can't support.612

  7. The floor-vs-room thesis holds at the last APAC data point

    New Zealand has universal public healthcare, yet a musician-specific charity still found a service room to fill — as in Australia, the universal floor did not make the music-specific room redundant. The gap this project tracks is now confirmed to end, in both directions, at the Tasman Sea.15
