Framing

Why Thailand, Why Policy

A Third Lever

Second of a six-region policy cycle. The aim is not to profile an organization but to read how a jurisdiction protects the mental health and welfare of music and performing-arts workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies.

If Japan reached for labor law and the U.S. for charity, Thailand has reached for a third lever: industrial policy. It is building the creative economy as a national export engine — a "soft power" super-agency modeled on South Korea's KOCCA — while the workers who power that economy remain overwhelmingly informal, outside the Social Security Fund's full protections, and with no dedicated mental-health provision.

Thailand is a natural experiment in whether growing the industry protects the people inside it.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · The Bet

The Soft-Power Bet

Since 2023 the Thai state has made the creative economy a headline national priority. The vehicle is THACCA — the Thailand Creative Culture Agency — informally stood up in early 2024 and explicitly modeled on South Korea's Korea Creative Content Agency (KOCCA), the body credited with engineering the "Korean Wave."2

01 A soft-power "super agency" 2024–2026

THACCA is mandated to develop 11 soft-power industries — food, sports, festivals, tourism, music, books, film, games, art, design, and fashion — and is intended to become a statutory super agency. As of June 2026 it is not yet legally established: the first Creative Culture Promotion Act has completed drafting at the Council of State and awaits Cabinet consideration, and on 25 March 2026 ten party-sponsored bills — including a THACCA Bill — were submitted to Parliament. While THACCA awaits its statute, the programs run through the body that already has legal standing, the Creative Economy Agency (CEA). The program is framed in jobs: a target of roughly 20 million creative-industry jobs via training, hiring subsidies, and tax incentives.2

02 Music Exchange 2024–

Music Exchange, the flagship for musicians, part-funds Thai artists to perform abroad. In its inaugural 2024 year — run by CEA with the music subcommittee — it part-funded 48 Thai artists and bands across 44 festivals in 12 international markets and brought ~78 international professionals to five Thai festivals, exceeding its ~30-act target. An export-and-exposure subsidy, not a welfare program.5

03 Fiscal support for creative livelihoods 2025

The 2025 budget approved roughly 200 million baht for screen content, and a more durable subsidy followed: under Royal Decree No. 803, gazetted 6 February 2026, artists earning income under Section 40(6) of the Revenue Code may deduct 60% as expenses (up from 30%), permanently from the 2025 tax year — a direct subsidy to creative incomes.6 Real money — but investment in output and export, not in welfare or mental health.

Section 01 · The Gap

Protection Lags the Ambition

Underneath the ambition, the workforce that produces the content is largely unprotected. Most Thai creative workers are freelance or informal — outside the employee protections of Sections 33/39 of the Social Security Act, unable to use the labour-protection complaint machinery, left to pursue unpaid fees through civil court themselves.4 A 2024 survey by the Creative Workers Union Thailand (CUT) — a young union founded to make creative labor legible as labor — put numbers on it.3

The state's answer is opt-in and thin. Self-employed people can enroll under Section 40 for 70, 100, or 300 baht a month, buying benefits well short of an employee's; roughly 10.9 million are currently insured against a Labour Ministry target of 20 million independent workers. A structural fix — the draft Independent Labour Promotion and Protection Act, Cabinet-endorsed in principle and under Council of State review, guaranteeing basic occupational rights, social-security access, and the right to form unions, and covering actors and production staff — is not yet law.4 A legal obstacle compounds the gap: Thai law requires union members to come from the same company or sector, blocking the cross-sector organizing a fragmented freelance workforce needs.3

On mental health there is nothing targeted. The peer-reviewed evidence is structural: a matched case-control study of Thai informal workers found occupation-linked health disparities, with unskilled informal workers at elevated risk, and called for government budget for informal-worker health.1 Thailand's general mental-health burden is rising, concentrated among 20–29-year-olds — the same cohort that fills the creative workforce.3 The drivers are documented; the services are not.

Part B — Appraisal

Section 02 · Reading the Model

Industry First, Worker Later

What Thailand gets right is the bet on leverage

Treating the creative sector as a strategic growth engine, not a charity case, is not naïve: it builds political constituency, public budget, and institutional infrastructure — THACCA, a statute, sub-committees with sector representatives — that a purely welfare framing rarely commands. The 60% tax deduction — now locked in by royal decree — and Music Exchange grants put money directly into creative livelihoods.6 And the formalization drive — Section 40 plus the independent-worker bill — is, in principle, the mechanism that could eventually pull freelance musicians into a protective net.

However, it builds the stage before the safety net

The soft-power spending flows to projects, exports, festivals, and reskilling — to the industry's output, not to the welfare or mental health of the people producing it. The protections that would matter are opt-in (Section 40, which a worker earning under 3,000 baht a month is unlikely to prioritize, and which has reached only ~10.9M of a 20M target), thin in benefit, and — for the structural bill — still draft.4 The union route that could force the issue is itself hobbled by a law that forbids cross-sector membership. KOCCA's model, which THACCA copies, was an export engine, not a worker-welfare body; importing it imports that silence. For mental health specifically there is nothing: no service, no line item, no agency mandate.

Read the evidence with care

Three caveats. (1) The headline precarity figures come from a single CUT survey of 380 illustrators — credible and directionally consistent with the informal-economy literature, but one union, one occupation, self-selected, with percentages carried from prior reporting and not re-extracted this run; ⚠ as-reported.3 (2) THACCA is still pre-statute — announced and budgeted but not yet enacted, so this reads an intended architecture as much as an operating one.2 (3) The peer-reviewed anchor concerns informal workers broadly and non-communicable disease, not musicians' mental health — a problem-size citation, not a what-works one.1

Verdict

Thailand is the clearest current example of the industrial-policy route to the creative sector — and the clearest demonstration that growing the industry does not, by itself, protect or heal the workers inside it.

Part C — For This Project

Section 03 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 Don't mistake creative-economy investment for worker welfare

Taiwan already has its own KOCCA-style body — 文化內容策進院 (TAICCA) — pursuing the same content-and-export logic THACCA copies. Thailand warns that this apparatus, however well-funded, leaves the worker-welfare and mental-health gap untouched. Position the NPO against that gap, not in the assumption the creative-economy agency will close it.

3.2 The union is the engine of worker protection — back it

The most concrete pressure for creative-worker rights in Thailand comes not from the state but from CUT, a young union that made precarity legible by surveying its own members. Taiwan's analog is 藝創工會; partnering with it (data, a counseling pilot, joint advocacy) is a faster route to legitimacy and reach than building from zero.

3.3 Opt-in social security won't reach freelance musicians alone

Section 40 shows a voluntary, low-benefit scheme barely touches workers below the poverty line. Taiwan's 職業災害保険 and labor-insurance debates face the same adverse-selection problem. The NPO's role is to bridge — enrollment help, subsidized premiums, or a complementary benefit — not to assume the state scheme suffices.

3.4 Mental health is the open lane

Across Thailand's entire soft-power and welfare apparatus, mental health for creative workers appears nowhere. That absence is the strategic opening: an NPO that owns the care layer — counseling, peer support, crisis referral built for music workers — complements rather than duplicates whatever industrial policy and labor protection the state eventually provides. As in Japan, supply what policy doesn't.

Limitations

  1. The creative-worker precarity figures rest on union sources and one CUT survey of illustrators, not a representative sample of music/performing-arts workers; the qualitative crew account was read in full this session, but the illustrator percentages were not re-extracted this run and are carried from prior reporting — treated as ⚠ directional.
  2. THACCA's statutory form is not yet enacted — the report reads an intended architecture as much as a settled one, with CEA as the current delivery body.
  3. The peer-reviewed anchor concerns informal workers and NCDs generally, not musicians' mental health; no Thailand-specific peer-reviewed study of musician mental health was located via PubMed (absence ≠ non-existence — Rule 4).
  4. Thai-language primary sources (the draft Act; Ministry of Labour notices) were read via English government summaries, national press, and policy-analysis secondary sources, not the full statutes.
  5. No direct contact with THACCA, CEA, CUT, or the Social Security Office.
