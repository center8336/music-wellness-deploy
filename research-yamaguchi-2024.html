Lower-Limb Musician's Dystonia —
Two Case Studies

Frontiers in Neurology, 15

DOI: 10.3389/fneur.2024.1489143 · 1398476

Reviewed by 陳棉 · 2026-07-15

↓ Scroll
Part A — Framing

Section · 01

Why Read This, and Why It Belongs

Read Frontiers Paper →

Musician's dystonia sits in both legs of this project's remit at once. It is an occupational-neurological injury — years of high-intensity fine-motor work rewiring sensorimotor control until the body fails at the one task it was trained for — and it carries a mental-health load: losing command of one's instrument is a documented blow to livelihood and identity. Treating it as one or the other misses the point. It is a clean test case for the claim that occupational health and mental health are co-equal, not competing, concerns.

The entry point is Satoshi Yamaguchi (山口智史), a former RADWIMPS drummer whose lower-limb (right-foot) focal dystonia interrupted his band career, who then moved into research on the condition at Keio University and returned to performing through an assistive instrument. That arc is genuinely compelling. But the brief for this reading is critical, not celebratory — so the first job is to separate what the research establishes from what the story suggests. They are not the same size.12

Section · 02

What These Papers Are (and Are Not)

Both papers are single-subject (n = 1) case studies of the same participant — an anonymised "36-year-old male professional rock drummer" with lower-limb dystonia (symptoms from age 24, diagnosed at 29, ~6,760 hours of practice at onset).12 They come from the Keio NeuroMusicLab; the corresponding author on both is Shinya Fujii, and the first authors are Shizuka Sata and Kazuaki Honda. Yamaguchi is a co-author (third author on one paper, fourth on the other) — not the lead investigator.

This matters for how the work is described elsewhere. It is inaccurate to call these "Yamaguchi's studies of his own dystonia." He is a contributor within someone else's lab, and the papers do not name their subject.

One unresolved point deserves flagging rather than smoothing over. The anonymised subject's profile — professional rock drummer, lower-limb/right-foot dystonia, onset in the mid-twenties — matches Yamaguchi's own public story closely, and he is a co-author. Yet nothing in either paper identifies the subject, and no external source confirms it. Whether this is effectively an n-of-1 self-study by a uniquely motivated patient-researcher, or a study of a separate but similar drummer, is left genuinely unclear. That ambiguity is not idle: if the co-author is also the subject, it bears on data access, motivation, and how we weigh objectivity. A careful reader should note the gap, not fill it.

Section · 03

Design and Methods

The two papers share one participant and one motor task (an eight-beat rock pattern at 80 BPM), recorded with and without dystonic symptoms, and analyse it two ways:

Paper 1: Muscle Synergies

Decomposes lower-limb EMG into spatial and temporal "muscle synergy" modules and compares the dystonic and non-dystonic states.1

Paper 2: Performance and EMG

Relates the acoustic output (bass-drum strike timing and amplitude, via piezoelectric sensors) to the underlying muscle activity.2

Both are careful, well-instrumented biomechanics. Neither has a control group — no healthy drummers, and (Paper 2 notes) not even a systematic healthy-versus-affected-side comparison.2 This is a within-subject, descriptive design: rich on mechanism, silent on generalisation.

Section · 04

What They Genuinely Add

A Precise Phenotype of a Rare Subtype

The musician's-dystonia literature is dominated by the hand (pianists, guitarists, strings) and the embouchure (brass, woodwind). Lower-limb / pedal dystonia is comparatively undescribed. Documenting exactly how it corrupts a drummer's foot control is a real, if narrow, contribution.

A Concrete Mechanism

The papers converge on a specific signature: with symptoms, the ankle dorsiflexors (tibialis anterior, extensor digitorum longus) activate earlier and harder. In Paper 1 (muscle synergies), the affected temporal synergy module peaks earlier — ≈318 ms vs ≈219 ms before the beat — with a spatial-module cosine similarity of r = 0.984, and its activation amplitude runs roughly 2.9× higher (0.58 vs 0.20 a.u.).1 Paper 2 traces the downstream cost in sound: bass-drum peak amplitude falls to about 60% of baseline (a ~40% loss) and the strike lands early — synchronisation error −26.2 ± 20.8 ms with symptoms vs −4.1 ± 14.5 ms without (Z = 5.00, p < 0.01).2 The dystonia isn't "weakness"; it's mistimed over-activation. That framing is useful and well-evidenced for this subject.

Objective, Low-Cost Measurement

The acoustic + EMG method is the kind of quantification that could, if scaled, support diagnosis and progress-tracking — the papers' implicit promise.

Part B — Critique

Section · 05

Weaknesses and Limits (Load-Bearing)

5.1 – n = 1, No Controls (Critical)

This is the ceiling on everything. The authors say so plainly: "our data is based on a single case, and the muscle synergy patterns … may vary considerably in other drummers," calling for larger samples and healthy-drummer comparisons.1 Nothing here establishes that the 318–219 ms peak shift or the dorsiflexor signature is characteristic of lower-limb MD rather than of this person. The correct reading is existence proof and hypothesis generator, not evidence about the condition in general. Severity: High — and appropriately owned by the authors.

5.2 – Mechanistically Rich, Epidemiologically Weightless

These papers can tell you how one drummer's foot fails. They can say nothing about how common lower-limb MD is, who gets it, what triggers it, or what treats it. Citing them for anything beyond mechanism would over-reach.

5.3 – Subject-Identity Ambiguity

Unresolved, and it touches the study's independence. (See §2.)

5.4 – Bespoke Task, Bounded Generalisation

One tempo, one pattern, one kit configuration; Paper 2 notes sensors were only on the bass drum, so cross-limb effects are unmeasured.2 Fine for a first description; a reason not to extrapolate to "drumming" writ large.

None of this makes the papers bad. It makes them small — and the honest failure mode is letting a moving personal story inflate how much two n-of-1 reports can carry.

Section · 06

Situating in the Evidence Base

Prevalence

MD affects roughly 1% of professional musicians3 — rare, but concentrated in high-intensity professionals and frequently career-ending.

Neurological, Not Psychological

MD is a disorder of maladaptive sensorimotor plasticity, historically misdiagnosed as psychogenic. Performance anxiety is routinely named as a "trigger," but the causal story is weaker than that word implies. A 2024 Hannover neuroimaging study (Alpheis et al.) found that stress-related limbic activation did not differ between musicians with and without focal dystonia; the group differences lay in childhood-trauma history and precuneus activity, not in limbic stress reactivity.4 The defensible reading is that anxiety is a modulator, comorbidity, and consequence, not a clean cause. For a mental-health-framed NPO this is not a footnote: calling dystonia "stress-caused" risks re-stigmatising a neurological injury as a failure of nerve. The accurate frame is two layers — an occupational-neurological injury, and a real downstream mental-health and identity blow.

Treatment Limits

Botulinum toxin helps some; sensorimotor retraining helps some, over weeks to years; there is no reliable cure. The highest-leverage point is upstream — prevention and early detection — which is exactly where Détári & Altenmüller (2025) aim their guidance for music educators.5

Section · 07

The Return, Read Carefully

Yamaguchi's return to the stage runs through the Yamaha VXD, a voice-controlled drum system (Yamaha × Keio) that triggers a bass drum from a spoken "don" via a throat sensor; first public performance 21 December 2024.6 It is a genuinely moving piece of assistive engineering and a vivid proof that a career need not simply end. But read for what it is: a bespoke, single-artist device, not a clinical treatment and not scalable to the next drummer without its own R&D. The story to carry forward is the bridge — musician → patient → researcher → performer — not "technology fixes dystonia." For most musicians, the realistic path remains prevention, early referral, and adaptation, not a custom instrument.

Section · 08

The Policy Layer: Floor vs. Room

The most transferable finding sits outside the papers, in how societies classify the condition — and it maps cleanly onto this project's floor (rights) vs room (services) thesis.

Germany: Recognition as Occupational Disease

Germany treats musician's focal dystonia as a compensable occupational disease. It is Berufskrankheit (BK) 2115, added to Appendix 1 of the Berufskrankheiten-Verordnung by the 4th amendment, effective 1 August 2017, under the title "focal dystonia as a disease of the central nervous system in instrumental musicians through fine-motor activity of high intensity."7 That is a rights floor — and it did not appear from nowhere; it sits atop decades of German musicians'-medicine infrastructure (Hannover's IMMM, Dresden, the Charité musicians' medicine centre).

Taiwan: Silence on Both Fronts

Taiwan recognises no such thing. The 勞動部 list of human-factor occupational diseases contains ten ergonomic conditions — carpal tunnel, rotator cuff, tendinitis, disc herniation and the like — all mechanical/compression injuries. A neurological movement disorder does not appear, and no Taiwan MD clinic or case paper could be found.8 The gap is on both axes: no services (room) and no recognition (floor).

Most other jurisdictions this project covers (Japan, UK, US, Canada, EU, Argentina) also do not list it. Germany is the exception, not the rule — which is precisely why it is the useful comparator. The lesson is directional: Germany's floor and its room grew together. Recognition followed the research and clinical base, not the other way around.

Section · 09

So What — For a Taiwan Music NPO

1. Name It Correctly, Loudly

The cheapest, highest-leverage intervention is language: musician's dystonia is a neurological occupational injury, not "nerves," not weakness, not a discipline problem. Correcting that frame among musicians, teachers, and doctors is free and it is where stigma lives.

2. Build the Room Before Lobbying for the Floor

Taiwan will not legislate a BK 2115 on request. But an NPO can seed the services layer that recognition eventually rests on: awareness, a referral pathway to neurologists who know the condition, and prevention/early-detection education for conservatoire students and teachers (the Détári–Altenmüller model).5

3. Center Lived Experience Without Importing the Hero Narrative

Yamaguchi's bridge — patient to researcher to performer — is a real template for musician-led destigmatisation. But for every documented return (Fleisher, Boyd, Leisner, Yamaguchi) many careers end quietly. The NPO's value is in the unglamorous work — prevention, early referral, rights advocacy — not in inspirational endings.

4. Use Germany as the Concrete "Possible"

BK 2115 is the evidence that a state can treat this as an occupational disease. It is a specific, citable target for a long-horizon Taiwan advocacy argument — and a reason to start the research/clinical base now, because that is what it grew from.

Section · 10

Bottom Line

Two careful n-of-1 papers that precisely describe how lower-limb dystonia wrecks one drummer's timing — real, honest, and small. Their scientific reach ends at "mechanism in one person"; the authors say so. The larger value for this project is not in the biomechanics but in what surrounds them: a correction to the lazy "stress causes dystonia" story, a clear-eyed read of assistive tech as bridge-not-cure, and a clean floor-vs-room contrast — Germany's BK 2115 against Taiwan's silence — that turns a moving personal arc into a concrete argument for building the services, and the recognition, that Taiwan's musicians do not yet have.
