TONIC RIDER

Org Study · Est. 2012

↓ Scroll

Why this case is different

Backline (USA) was a cold-start crisis directory; MusiCares (USA) is a large endowed fund inside the Recording Academy; Music Minds Matter (UK) is a helpline-led charity spun out of a £48M benevolent fund. Tonic Rider is the fourth pattern, and the smallest: a lean, clinically-facilitated peer-support and psychoeducation programme run on roughly half a million pounds a year by a team of ten. It does not operate a 24/7 helpline, hold an endowment, or employ a large clinical workforce. Instead it puts a mandatory consultation/triage gate in front of a catalogue of short, repeatable, theme-based groups, workshops and accredited courses, delivered by qualified practitioners and paid for strand-by-strand by named referral partners. For a Taiwan startup that must launch on a modest budget, this is the most directly copyable service design in the series — a programme you could stand up without a fortune, a hotline, or a hundred-year-old parent.

01 — Profile

From a Portsmouth gig charity to a national music-industry programme1

Tonic Rider is the music-industry programme of Tonic Music for Mental Health Ltd, a small Portsmouth charity founded in 2012 on the belief that participation in music is good for mental health. Of the four organisations in this series it is the smallest and the leanest — and, for that reason, the most directly copyable.

Tonic Music for Mental Health was founded in 2012 in Portsmouth; in its early years it raised funds and awareness through gigs and ran music/art workshops for people living with mental illness, before "developing into a registered charity operating across the UK" and formally registering with the Charity Commission on 12 June 2020.12

Tonic Rider — the strand aimed at people professionally engaged in music — launched on 16 February 2021 as a digital support programme, a direct response to the pandemic's devastation of the live sector: free virtual peer groups (originally delivered by Babyshambles' Dr Adam Ficek, a psychotherapist and researcher) plus digital workshops on performance anxiety, acceptance and commitment therapy, emotional coping skills, mental-health first aid and suicide prevention. Founding patron Terry Hall of The Specials and patron Barry Ashworth of Dub Pistols fronted the launch; CEO Steph Langan framed it as a way "to reach the people who need our support the most."3 Tonic now runs three parallel programmes — Tonic Rider (music industry), Never Mind The Stigma (music fans) and Tonic Futures (18–25s) — but Tonic Rider is the one this study examines, because it is the music-occupational-health service.2

02 — The distinctive model

Gate, catalogue, partner-fund

Three structural choices define Tonic Rider and separate it from the helpline-and-endowment pattern of the other UK case.

CHOICE 01

A mandatory consultation gate

No one drops into a group cold. Every participant first completes a registration form (contact, health and wellbeing, demographics, emergency contact and GP details) and then attends an up-to-one-hour online consultation whose explicit purpose is "to identify any risks / concerns and address additional / unmet needs … a safe and positive experience." A deliberate clinical-safety and triage device — and cheap, because it is a single structured conversation, not an assessment clinic.4

CHOICE 02

A catalogue of short, repeatable units

Rather than open-ended therapy, Tonic Rider offers a modular menu on a rolling calendar: 6-week peer-support groups, 12-week group therapy, 2-hour workshops, and accredited day/half-day courses (§4). The same workshop — "Music Performance Anxiety," "Emotional Resilience," "Substance Use Awareness" — runs again and again for different sub-audiences. Content built once and amortised across many cohorts.56

CHOICE 03

Funding stitched strand-by-strand

Tonic Rider does not rely on one income source; specific strands are underwritten by specific funders. On the 2026 schedule, peer-support groups and accredited courses are largely "Supported by The Christopher Meredith Foundation," the workshop series by "PPL Giving," and 12-week Group Therapy by "The Royal Society of Musicians." A small charity expands by signing a partner to fund a named strand, not by winning one large unrestricted grant.6

03 — Financial profile

A small, roughly break-even, fast-growing operation7

Tonic Rider is the opposite of Music Minds Matter's planned-deficit, reserve-funded model — it runs on money raised and spent in-year, with no endowment and no investment income. The performance-anxiety and resilience strands the project flagged as the borrowable core are present at two depths: a 2-hour preventative workshop and a 6-week peer-support group dedicated to Music Performance Anxiety — a useful "light-touch then deeper" progression.6

Clinical governance

Tonic Rider's clinical assurance is built three ways, none of which requires a large in-house clinical department:

  • Qualified-practitioner delivery

    Tonic states its team "includes qualified mental health practitioners." Named on-site: Mental Health Lead Jade Hughes, psychotherapist Dr Claire Bloxsom, Programmes Lead Jeordie Shenton, and adviser/facilitator Dr Adam Ficek; group therapy is run by two psychotherapists and 1:1 counselling by an MBACP-registered counsellor.26

  • A dedicated governance function

    The team includes a Governance, Risk & Compliance Lead (Stanley Iampietro) — unusual and notable for a ten-person charity — and the Charity Commission record lists a full policy suite: safeguarding, safeguarding vulnerable beneficiaries, serious-incident reporting, risk management, and internal financial controls.21

  • The consultation gate as front-line screen

    The §2 gate is the front-line safety screen, plus explicit, repeated framing that "Tonic Music is not a crisis service" with sign-posting to NHS 111 — a clean scope boundary that keeps a light-touch service from being treated as emergency care.4

Impact metrics — found in the filed annual report

Tonic Rider does not publish headline impact metrics on its public website, but the FY2025 Trustees' Annual Report filed with the Charity Commission does — closing the gap the prior pass left open. For the year to 30 June 2025 the charity reports (across its programmes): 317 newly registered participants (+17% year on year), 225 consultation appointments (+32%), 469 hours of 1-to-1 therapy (+36%), 312 hours of peer-support groups (+70%), 28 hours of skills workshops (+56%) and 52 hours of accredited training (+30%); on outreach, 414 posters to 79 venues, 188 mental-health lounges/stands at events (+86%) delivering 5,000+ interventions, and 22 Tonic Talks. Cumulative active participants are reported as 926 for Tonic Rider and 308 for Never Mind The Stigma. These are the charity's own reported activity counts (✅ sourced to the filed annual report; org-reported, not independently audited outcome measures). The remaining genuine gap is website-level transparency — the numbers exist in the filed report but are not surfaced where most readers or funders would look.8

05 — Reach

Through partners and patrons, not payroll6

Tonic Rider's reach multiplies through two near-zero-cost channels rather than headcount.

Referral & funder partners. The published schedule names a wide partner network — Help Musicians, the Night Time Industries Association (NTIA), Music Venue Trust, the Musicians' Union, Music Managers Forum, Featured Artists Coalition, BAPAM, Music Support, the Royal Society of Musicians, PPL, the Christopher Meredith Foundation, the Baring Foundation and many venues and festivals. Some are funders of named strands; others are referral routes that channel their members and audiences into the programme. The same organisation often does both — e.g. a foundation that funds the peer-support groups while a union refers members to them.6

Patrons and the live circuit. Tonic's identity is woven through the UK alternative-music scene: founding patron Terry Hall (The Specials), patron Barry Ashworth (Dub Pistols), and a long roster of musician ambassadors, plus a touring "Tonic on Tour" presence (the Libertines, the Specials, festivals such as Mucky Weekender) that carries information stands front-of-house and backstage. This buys credibility and access in the exact community the programme serves — at the cost of a stall and some volunteer time, not a marketing budget.23

A thin, very new board1

Tonic Music for Mental Health is governed by a board of three registered trustees, all appointed recently.

No trustee receives remuneration or benefits, and no employee has total benefits over £60k. Founder and CEO Steph Langan runs the charity as staff, not as a registered trustee — a clean executive/board separation, accurate on both the register and the org's own site.12

Two governance observations follow honestly from the record. First, a three-person board, all appointed in 2024–2025, is a thin and very new board for a charity with ten employees and half a million pounds of turnover — the kind of structure funders and a future merger/expansion would want to see deepened. Second, against that, the charity has invested in a dedicated Governance, Risk & Compliance function and a complete written policy suite (§4), which is more governance infrastructure than many charities this size carry. The picture is of an operationally careful organisation whose board has not yet scaled to match its operations.1

07 — Strengths

What works

A genuinely lean, copyable service

The whole programme runs on ~£0.5M a year with ten staff, 93% of spend on charitable activity, and low fundraising cost — proof that a credible music-industry mental-health service does not require an endowment or a 24/7 helpline.7

The consultation gate

A single, up-to-one-hour screening conversation in front of all provision delivers risk triage, scope-setting and routing cheaply — a safety mechanism a small org can actually staff.4

A modular, amortised catalogue

Short, theme-based groups and 2-hour workshops, built once and re-run for many cohorts and sub-audiences, give wide coverage (performance anxiety, resilience, substance use, neurodiversity, identity, bereavement) without bespoke design each time.6

Clinical rigour without a clinical payroll

Qualified practitioners, two-psychotherapist group therapy, MBACP-registered counselling, MHFA/RSPH-accredited courses, a Governance/Risk/Compliance lead and a full policy suite — assurance assembled from named people and accreditations rather than a large salaried clinical department.21

Strand-by-strand partner funding

Expansion by signing a funder to a named strand (CMF → peer groups, PPL → workshops, RSM → group therapy) diversifies income and lets the programme grow one fundable unit at a time.6

Fast, organic growth

Income tripled in four years (£165k → £557k) while staying near break-even — growth funded by demand and partnerships, not by drawing down capital.7

08 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

No reserve cushion

Tonic spends close to what it raises and holds no endowment or investment income; a single lost major funder (e.g. the Christopher Meredith Foundation, which underwrites much of the peer-support and training calendar) would hit the programme directly. Small surpluses are accumulating but slowly.7

Funder concentration in the calendar

The 2026 schedule leans heavily on a small number of named funders for whole strands. Diversified in principle, but in practice the loss of one would visibly thin the menu.6

A thin, very new board

Three trustees, all seated in 2024–2025, is light governance for the scale and risk profile of clinical mental-health delivery, and a likely flag for institutional funders.1

Impact data filed but not surfaced

Reach/activity metrics exist in the FY2025 annual report filed with the Charity Commission (§4) but are absent from the public website, where funders and participants are most likely to look — a communications gap rather than a measurement one. Publishing them on-site would strengthen the fundraising case.8

Capacity, not crisis

By design Tonic Rider is "not a crisis service," 18+, and UK-only; participants in acute risk must be routed elsewhere (NHS 111). That is the correct and safe scope, but it means the model covers prevention and peer support, not the acute end.4

Key-person and founder dependence

A ten-person charity built around a founder-CEO and a handful of named facilitators carries real key-person risk; succession and bench depth are not visible in the public record. ❓ Unverified.

09 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. This is the model to copy first — it fits a small budget

    Of the organisations in this series, Tonic Rider is the one a Taiwan startup could most realistically build in year one: a half-million-pound (NT$ low-double-digit-million) programme, ten people, near break-even, no endowment, no hotline. The lesson is that a credible service can start small and grow with demand.7

  2. Put a consultation/triage gate in front of everything

    Adopt Tonic's mandatory registration + up-to-one-hour online consultation before anyone joins a group or workshop. It is the single most transferable safety device here: it screens risk, captures GP/emergency-contact details, routes people correctly, and is cheap because it is one structured conversation — not a clinic.4

  3. Build a modular catalogue, not bespoke therapy

    Design a fixed menu of 6-week peer groups, 12-week group therapy, and 2-hour workshops (performance anxiety, emotional resilience, substance use, identity, neurodiversity) and re-run them for different sub-audiences (artists, crew, 18–25s, managers, venue staff). Build the content once; amortise it across cohorts.6

  4. Fund strand-by-strand from named partners

    Rather than chasing one large grant, sign specific funders to specific strands — a foundation to underwrite the peer-support groups, a collecting society or rights body (the PPL analogue in Taiwan would be a body like a local music-rights or broadcasting fund) to underwrite the workshop series, a musicians' benevolent body to underwrite group therapy. Each fundable unit can be added as a partner is secured.6

  5. Borrow clinical assurance; don't build a clinical department

    Staff with qualified, accredited practitioners (in Taiwan: 諮商心理師 / 臨床心理師-registered counsellors and psychotherapists), use internationally recognised accredited courses (MHFA-equivalent, suicide-first-aid), and appoint an explicit governance/risk/safeguarding lead even at ten people. Assurance comes from named, registered people and a written policy suite — not headcount.21

  6. Name a crisp scope boundary and sign-post out

    State plainly "we are not a crisis service," set an age floor (18+, with a youth strand if resourced), and route acute risk to the national emergency/mental-health line (the 1925 安心專線 in Taiwan, as NHS 111 is for Tonic). This protects a light-touch service from being mistaken for emergency care.4

  7. Use patrons and the live circuit for reach

    Tonic's credibility comes from being embedded in its own music scene via patrons, ambassadors and a touring information-stand presence — near-zero-cost reach. A Taiwan NPO should recruit respected local artists as patrons and take a backstage/front-of-house presence to festivals and venues rather than buying advertising.23

  8. Seat a real board and publish impact from day one

    Learn from Tonic's two visible gaps: build a board deeper than three people and publish reach and outcome metrics early. Both are cheap to do at the start and expensive to retrofit, and both are exactly what Taiwanese funders (文化部, corporate sponsors) will ask for.18
