Framing

Why Japan, Why Policy

A Natural Experiment

This is the first of a six-region policy cycle. The aim is not to profile an organization but to read how a jurisdiction protects the mental health and welfare of music and performing-arts workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies.

Japan is a useful first case because it took a route opposite to the U.S.: instead of building a charitable safety net (MusiCares), Japan has reached for labor and transaction law — extending workers' accident insurance to performers (2021) and enacting a Freelance Protection Act (2024).

It is a natural experiment in whether rights can do the work that charity does elsewhere.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · Protections

Three Recent Moves

Japan's music and performing-arts workforce is large and overwhelmingly freelance — roughly 218,250 performing-arts workers, within a 2019 government count of about 4.6 million freelancers overall, 6.3% of them artists.1 For most of the postwar era this workforce sat outside the protections employees take for granted. Three recent moves have begun to change that — none of them a mental-health program.

01 Accident insurance reaches performers 2021

In 2021, "performing arts workers" were, for the first time, named eligible for special enrollment in Japan's workers' accident compensation insurance (労災保険) — a structural milestone driven by Arts Workers Japan, whose president also chairs a nationwide centre for performers' accident-compensation enrollment. Before this, the editorial states plainly, "no legal protection is in place for performers and staff working in the arts and entertainment industry."1

02 The Freelance Protection Act (フリーランス新法) in force 2024-11-01

Japan's "Act on Ensuring Proper Transactions Involving Specified Entrusted Business Operators" took effect November 1, 2024. It covers solo business operators with no employees — explicitly including creative freelancers — and applies to all B2B commissioning regardless of the client's size, closing a gap in the older Subcontract Act. Its duties: written contracts, on-time payment, 30 days' notice before terminating or non-renewing a continuing contract, accommodation of caregiving/parenting, and — critically for wellbeing — a duty to prevent and respond to harassment (sexual, power, and other). It is enforced by the Japan Fair Trade Commission, with fines up to ¥500,000.2

03 Pandemic economic support (文化庁) 2020–2022

During COVID-19 the Agency for Cultural Affairs ran a sequence of economic-continuity programs — 文化芸術活動の継続支援事業 and the two "ARTS for the future!" rounds — funding professional arts groups and individual performers to keep working through the crisis.3 This was income protection, not care, and it was time-limited.

Section 01 · The Gap

Protection Without Care

Against these protections sits the mental-health picture, which is severe. The same editorial reports survey findings that 83% of arts and entertainment workers had experienced psychological attacks and 42.7% reported suicidal thoughts, with harassment widespread (93% abuse-of-position, 73% sexual).1

Overwork is structural: a sector survey conducted October–December 2023 found over 40% of technical staff and stage directors working 60+ hours a week, and 60% of directors and producers taking one day off a week or fewer.4

Yet there is no dedicated public mental-health service for this workforce. The most targeted provision is a mental-care consultation service with a clinical psychologist, run by Arts Workers Japan itself — a worker body, not the state.1

Part B — Appraisal

Section 02 · Reading the Model

Rights, Not Rooms

What Japan gets right is the diagnosis

Its reforms treat the roots of poor mental health in this workforce — precarity, non-payment, abrupt contract termination, and harassment — as enforceable legal wrongs rather than private misfortunes. The harassment-prevention duty in the Freelance Act is the clearest example: it converts a leading driver of distress (the editorial's 73% sexual-harassment figure) into a compliance obligation backed by a regulator.2 Extending accident insurance to performers does the same for physical risk. This is, in principle, more durable than charity: it does not depend on annual fundraising, and it binds the counterparties who create the harm.

However, it is protection without care

Nothing in this architecture funds or delivers mental-health support. The Freelance Act governs only B2B transactions — it does not reach platform or audience income, employment-status disputes, or the isolation and identity pressures the wider literature ties to musicians' distress. The COVID money was economic, group-oriented, and has ended.3 Mental health itself is left to Japan's general systems — and to a single clinical-psychologist consultation desk run by a volunteer association. For a workforce reporting 42.7% suicidal ideation, that is a safety net with one thread. Japan has built the floor (rights) but not the room (services).

Read the evidence with care

The headline prevalence figures are the weakest link. They come from surveys reported in a two-page editorial whose author leads the advocacy body campaigning for these reforms — credible and PubMed-indexed, and consistent with national reporting on overwork, but not a peer-reviewed probability sample with published methodology. They should be cited as ⚠ as-reported and treated as directional. The legal and policy facts (the Act's provisions and dates, the 2021 insurance change) are firm and government-sourced.2

Verdict

Japan is the strongest current example of the labor-law route to music-worker wellbeing — and the clearest demonstration that the labor-law route, on its own, leaves the mental-health gap open.

Part C — For This Project

Section 03 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 Borrow the labor-law lever, not just the charity model

Japan shows that a Freelance-Act-style instrument — enforceable rules on contracts, payment, termination notice, and especially harassment — attacks mental-health drivers at the root. Taiwan's 藝創工會 and 勞動部 already debate freelance-creator protections; Japan's Act (and its JFTC enforcement) is a concrete, recent template to point to.

3.2 Extend occupational insurance to freelance performers

Japan's 2021 move to let performing-arts workers into accident-compensation insurance is directly translatable: Taiwan could add a performing-arts category to 職業災害保険, giving freelance musicians a floor that currently doesn't exist.

3.3 Position the NPO to fill the gap Japan left open

The strategic takeaway. Even a jurisdiction that does the labor-law work well still lacks a dedicated mental-health service for music workers. A Taiwan organization should aim to be exactly that — the care layer that complements, rather than duplicates, whatever labor protections the state provides. Don't compete with policy; supply what policy doesn't.

3.4 Union-run counseling is a viable, low-cost first service

Arts Workers Japan stood up a clinical-psychologist consultation desk without state funding. A Taiwanese equivalent (e.g., via 藝創工會) is a realistic early MH offering — small, partnership-based, and credible because it sits inside a worker body the community already trusts.

Limitations

  1. The mental-health prevalence figures rest on advocacy-linked surveys reported in an editorial, not a peer-reviewed methods paper; treated as ⚠ directional.
  2. Japanese-language primary sources (the Freelance Act text; 文化庁 program reports) were read via government summary pages and secondary legal briefings, not the full statute.
  3. This is a policy scan, not an exhaustive review — Japan's general suicide-prevention framework (自殺対策基本法) and prefecture-level arts support were noted but not deep-read.
  4. No direct contact with Arts Workers Japan or 文化庁.
