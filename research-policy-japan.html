Framing

Why Japan, Why Policy

A Natural Experiment

This is the first of a six-region policy cycle. The aim is not to profile an organization but to read how a jurisdiction protects the mental health and welfare of music and performing-arts workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies.

Japan is a useful first case because it took a route opposite to the U.S.: instead of building a charitable safety net (MusiCares), Japan has reached for labor and transaction law — extending workers' accident insurance to performers (2021) and enacting a Freelance Protection Act (2024).

It is a natural experiment in whether rights can do the work that charity does elsewhere.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · Protections

Three Recent Moves

Japan's music and performing-arts workforce is large and overwhelmingly freelance — roughly 218,250 performing-arts workers, and 94.6% of arts and entertainment workers are freelancers.1 The editorial situates them within about 4.6 million freelancers nationally — an estimate that traces to a 2020 Cabinet Secretariat survey (fielded early 2020), which put the population near 4.62 million.8 For most of the postwar era this workforce sat outside the protections employees take for granted. Recent moves have begun to change that — none of them a mental-health program.

01 Accident insurance reaches performers 1 Apr 2021

On 1 April 2021, "arts and entertainment workers" (芸能関係作業従事者) were, for the first time, made eligible for special enrollment (特別加入) in Japan's workers' accident compensation insurance (労災保険) — a structural milestone driven by Arts Workers Japan, whose president also chairs a nationwide centre for performers' accident-compensation enrollment. Before this, the editorial states plainly, "no legal protection is in place for performers and staff working in the arts and entertainment industry."1 A broader change followed on 1 November 2024, opening special enrollment to essentially all commissioned freelancers (~2.7 million estimated eligible) — and Arts Workers Japan now runs one of the MHLW-approved enrollment bodies, an institutional role, not just advocacy.7

02 The Freelance Protection Act (フリーランス新法) in force 2024-11-01

Japan's "Act on Ensuring Proper Transactions Involving Specified Entrusted Business Operators" took effect November 1, 2024. It covers solo business operators with no employees — explicitly including creative freelancers — and applies to all B2B commissioning regardless of the client's size, closing a gap in the older Subcontract Act. Its duties: written disclosure of terms, on-time payment (within 60 days), 30 days' notice before terminating or non-renewing a continuing contract, accommodation of caregiving/parenting, and — critically for wellbeing — a duty to prevent and respond to harassment (sexual, power, and other). Several duties are tiered by contract length (the 30-day notice and caregiving accommodation attach to continuing engagements, not one-off gigs). Enforcement is split: the Japan Fair Trade Commission handles transaction fairness, while the Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare owns the work-environment duties; penalties run to a fine of up to ¥500,000.2

And the Act is no longer untested. On 17 June 2025 the JFTC issued its first-ever recommendation under it — against the publisher Shogakukan, which commissioned 191 freelancers (writers, photographers, illustrators, hair-and-makeup) without written terms and paid late — and by December 2025 had issued guidance to 128 broadcasting and advertising businesses, the sectors closest to performing-arts work. The Act has teeth, and they are biting in the creative economy.5

03 Pandemic economic support (文化庁) 2020–2022

During COVID-19 the Agency for Cultural Affairs ran a sequence of economic-continuity programs — the 文化芸術活動の継続支援事業 (FY2020, ≈¥50.9bn; individuals up to ¥200,000–¥1.5M) and the two "ARTS for the future!" rounds (up to ¥25M per organization) — funding professional arts groups and individual performers to keep working through the crisis.3 This was income protection, not care, and it was time-limited. Post-pandemic, 文化庁's FY2026 budget request proposes two relevant new lines (a ¥200M arts-harassment program and a ¥76M legal-consultation desk), but these are requests, not yet enacted.10

Section 01 · The Gap

Protection Without Care

Against these protections sits the mental-health picture, which is severe. The same editorial reports survey findings that 83% of arts and entertainment workers had experienced psychological attacks and 42.7% reported suicidal thoughts, with harassment widespread (93% abuse-of-position, 73% sexual).1

Overwork is structural: a survey conducted October–December 2023 for the MHLW's FY2024 White Paper on Measures to Prevent Karoshi (run by JNIOSH; n=488 arts/entertainment workers) found 46.2% of technical staff and 40.7% of stage-direction/production staff working 60+ hours a week, and ≤1 day off a week reported by between 38.5% and 60.4% of roles (the ≈60% figure is the highest-burden role, not "directors and producers" specifically).4

Yet there is no dedicated public mental-health service for this workforce. The most targeted provision is a clinical-psychologist consultation service — "芸能従事者こころの119," launched in 2022 and run by Arts Workers Japan itself — a worker body, not the state.1 Nor is the evidence base deep: a targeted search located no pan-Japan probability-sample study of this workforce's mental-health prevalence; the nearest peer-reviewed empirical work is a small, recent music-performance-anxiety literature (e.g., a 2026 study of 390 Japanese musicians) measuring anxiety in convenience samples, not clinical prevalence.9

Part B — Appraisal

Section 02 · Reading the Model

Rights, Not Rooms

What Japan gets right is the diagnosis

Its reforms treat the roots of poor mental health in this workforce — precarity, non-payment, abrupt contract termination, and harassment — as enforceable legal wrongs rather than private misfortunes. The harassment-prevention duty in the Freelance Act is the clearest example: it converts a leading driver of distress (the editorial's 73% sexual-harassment figure) into a compliance obligation. It is best read not as a wholly novel instrument but as a gap-closing extension of Japan's employee-side Power Harassment Prevention Act (パワハラ防止法; mandatory for large employers in 2020 and SMEs in 2022) to a non-employee population the employee regime never reached.6 Extending accident insurance to performers does the same for physical risk. And the 2025 enforcement record shows the floor is real, not merely declarative: the JFTC has named offenders in exactly the creative sectors where freelance work concentrates.5 This is, in principle, more durable than charity: it does not depend on annual fundraising, and it binds the counterparties who create the harm.

However, it is protection without care

Nothing in this architecture funds or delivers mental-health support. The Freelance Act governs only B2B transactions — it does not reach platform or audience income, employment-status disputes, or the isolation and identity pressures the wider literature ties to musicians' distress. The COVID money was economic, group-oriented, and has ended; the FY2026 successor lines are still only requests.10 Mental health itself is left to Japan's general systems — and to a single clinical-psychologist consultation desk run by a volunteer association. For a workforce reporting 42.7% suicidal ideation, that is a safety net with one thread. Japan has built the floor (rights) but not the room (services).

Read the evidence with care

The headline prevalence figures are the weakest link. They come from a survey reported in a two-page editorial whose author leads the advocacy body campaigning for these reforms — credible and PubMed-indexed, and consistent with national reporting on overwork, but with no published sample size or methodology, so not a peer-reviewed probability sample. They should be cited as ⚠ as-reported and treated as directional. The wider Japanese evidence base is genuinely thin: no probability-sample prevalence study of this workforce exists, only a small, recent performance-anxiety literature.9 The legal and policy facts (the Act's provisions, dates, enforcement record, and the 2021 insurance change) are firm and government-sourced.2

Verdict

Japan is the strongest current example of the labor-law route to music-worker wellbeing — and the clearest demonstration that the labor-law route, on its own, leaves the mental-health gap open.

Part C — For This Project

Section 03 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 Borrow the labor-law lever, not just the charity model

Japan shows that a Freelance-Act-style instrument — enforceable rules on contracts, payment, termination notice, and especially harassment — attacks mental-health drivers at the root, and (as the 2025 JFTC recommendations show) can be enforced rather than left on paper. Taiwan's 藝創工會 and 勞動部 already debate freelance-creator protections; Japan's Act, its JFTC/MHLW split enforcement, and its first creative-sector cases are a concrete, recent template to point to.

3.2 Extend occupational insurance to freelance performers

Japan's 2021 move to let arts and entertainment workers into accident-compensation insurance — and its 2024 expansion to all freelancers — is directly translatable: Taiwan could add a performing-arts category to 職業災害保険, giving freelance musicians a floor that currently doesn't exist.

3.3 Position the NPO to fill the gap Japan left open

The strategic takeaway. Even a jurisdiction that does the labor-law work well still lacks a dedicated mental-health service for music workers. A Taiwan organization should aim to be exactly that — the care layer that complements, rather than duplicates, whatever labor protections the state provides. Don't compete with policy; supply what policy doesn't.

3.4 Union-run counseling is a viable, low-cost first service — and can grow into an institutional role

Arts Workers Japan stood up a clinical-psychologist consultation desk ("こころの119") without state funding, and has since become an MHLW-approved labor-insurance enrollment body. A Taiwanese equivalent (e.g., via 藝創工會) is a realistic early MH offering — small, partnership-based, credible because it sits inside a worker body the community already trusts — with a plausible path from service provider to recognized institutional intermediary.

Limitations

  1. The mental-health prevalence figures rest on an advocacy-body survey relayed in an editorial with no published sample size or method; treated as ⚠ directional.
  2. Japanese-language primary sources (the Freelance Act text; 文化庁 program reports) were read via government summary pages, statute explainers, and secondary legal briefings, not the full statute in every case.
  3. This is a policy scan, not an exhaustive review — Japan's general suicide-prevention framework (自殺対策基本法, amended 2025, child/school-focused) and prefecture-level arts support were noted but not deep-read.
  4. The harassment duty is framed here as an extension of the employee-side Power Harassment Prevention Act; the precise boundary between the two regimes for freelancers was not litigated in primary case law.
  5. The "6.3% artists" share and the 218,250 headcount are editorial/census figures inherited via the editorial, not independently reconciled to the underlying government tables.
  6. The 文化庁 FY2026 lines are budget requests, not enacted.
  7. No direct contact with Arts Workers Japan, the JFTC, or 文化庁.
