SAMARITANS OF THAILAND

สมาคมสะมาริตันส์แห่งประเทศไทย

Org Study · Est. 1978

↓ Scroll

One model, in one line

MusiCares is a music-industry incumbent; SADEM is a musicians' union that owns a health fund. Samaritans of Thailand is neither music-specific nor a service this project would normally profile — it is a general, volunteer-run, donation-funded suicide-prevention helpline. It is here for one reason: in the absence of any Thai music-specific mental-health organization, it is the nearest resource a Thai musician in distress would actually reach. The transferable lesson is the cheap, trainable befriending-helpline method — and its funding fragility — not anything music-sector. The finding that matters is what is missing around it.1

01 — Profile

A befriending helpline, not a clinic1

Samaritans of Thailand is a registered association (สมาคม) that runs a telephone listening service. Its model is befriending: trained lay volunteers — not psychiatrists or psychologists — listen with what the organization calls ฟังด้วยใจ ("listening with the heart"), without giving advice, diagnosis, or solutions, and keep every call confidential. It was founded in 1978 by the Thai physician Prof. Dr. Udomsilp Srisangnam, after the British Samaritans model, with about 40 Thai and foreign volunteers. It is explicitly non-religious, non-political, and non-philosophical; its volunteers are unpaid and drawn from many professions.13

Critically for this project, the service is general: anyone in emotional distress can call. The verified Find A Helpline record lists its focus topics as anxiety, depression, loneliness, stress and suicide, and "also helps with" a long list including relationships, family, abuse, grief and substance use — no occupational or sector-specific track of any kind, and nothing for musicians.3

02 — The model

Volunteer befriending, in the Befrienders lineage4

The distinctive thing about Samaritans of Thailand is not any program — it runs essentially one service, a phone line — but the method and the lineage.

  • Method · befriending

    Listen and ask — never advise

    Volunteers are trained to listen and ask, never to suggest. Advice is prohibited (it "increases the dependence of callers," and "right and wrong do not depend on our standards"); volunteers ask directly whether a caller has suicidal thoughts and an actual plan, and in an active crisis ask the caller to stop. Becoming a volunteer is deliberately hard: a multi-day workshop, ~3 weeks of general training, then 4–6 months (up to 12) of one-on-one mentoring before handling real calls.2

  • Lineage · Befrienders Worldwide

    Recognized method, on its own financially

    The Bangkok and Chiang Mai centres are listed members of Befrienders Worldwide (formerly Befrienders International; UK Samaritans founded 1953 by Rev. Chad Varah). Befrienders supplies shared befriending standards and a global "find a centre" directory — but it does not fund local centres. So Samaritans of Thailand carries an internationally recognized method and brand while being financially on its own.14

  • Languages & reach

    Thai + English, two cities, not 24/7

    The line runs in Thai and English. The Thai line operates daily 12:00–22:00; the English service was added in 2007 and still runs as a call-back line (historically ~100 calls/year), not a live 24-hour line. A Thai caller and an English-speaking expatriate both have somewhere to call — but neither has the 24/7 coverage of the UK or Singapore Samaritans.2

03 — Funding & sustainability

Donation-dependent, thinly resourced2

This is the part most relevant to a startup — and the part where the sources are weakest. Thai associations of this size do not publish audited accounts, and none were found; every monetary figure here is a dated journalist estimate or a single grant, never a filing (❓).

  • Base

    Donation-funded

    Unlike its UK counterpart — which receives British-government support — Samaritans of Thailand "depends solely on donations." Its current site solicits donations to a Bangkok Bank (Siam Paragon branch) account in the association's name.21

  • Cost

    ~500,000 baht/year to run

    The then-director estimated about 500,000 baht/year to run operations (electricity, phone, rent). ⚠️ as-of-2015.2

  • Grants

    Episodic, not a base

    Rotary International gave a one-million-baht goal-specific grant (recruitment / outreach) around 2014, which boosted volunteer numbers. The last government grant was a 100,000-baht grant from the Department of Mental Health in 2011 — the director said the DMH "saw it as something abstract rather than concrete," unable to measure a listening service's success rate. Public money has been occasional and small, never a sustaining base.2

  • Peers

    Less equipped than regional neighbours

    The Samaritans of Singapore and Befrienders Malaysia both run 24-hour lines with larger volunteer corps and national-government support; Samaritans of Thailand, by contrast, is "less equipped in terms of manpower," and was constrained from growing its lines from four to ten by volunteers and funding.2

Honest limitation. Every figure above is a decade-old estimate or a one-off grant. On the Orgs Map, this org's funding Basis can move from "❓ No source" to ⚠️ Inferred (donation-funded) — the donation model is stated on the org's own current site and corroborated by the Bangkok Post — but it should not be ✅, because there is no published filing or audited account to verify any amount.1

05 — What it offers musicians

Nothing music-specific — said plainly3

This must be said without hedging: Samaritans of Thailand offers nothing designed for the music sector. There is no musician track, no industry partnership, no occupational-health framing, no touring or gig-economy content, no music-industry-aware volunteers. It is a general emotional-support and suicide-prevention line. A Thai musician in crisis would reach exactly the same volunteers, the same noon–10pm Thai line or the call-back English line, as any other caller.

That is precisely why it appears in this project's map and in this study: in the absence of any Thai music-specific mental-health organization (§6), the general crisis line is the nearest-available resource a musician would actually use. Its value to the music sector is real but incidental — it is the floor, not a music-sector service.

06 — The gap finding

A policy layer and a general helpline — but no music-specific mental-health organization5

This is the point of the Thailand parity study. Before concluding the music-MH org layer is empty, I ran a genuine Thai-language search (Rule 4: absence found ≠ non-existence) using the prescribed terms — มูลนิธิ (foundation), นักดนตรี (musician), สุขภาพจิตนักดนตรี (musician mental health), อุตสาหกรรมดนตรี (music industry), สหภาพนักดนตรี / สมาคมนักดนตรี (musicians' union / association). What it surfaced, and why none of it closes the gap:

Found · not music-MH

LOVEiS Music Foundation

A registered foundation (มูลนิธิ เลิฟ อิส มิวสิค) founded to carry out the vision of the CEO of the LOVEiS Entertainment label. Music-specific — but its purpose is music education and industry development: free classes, conservatory scholarships, instrument donation. No mental-health service, no welfare for working musicians.6

Found · welfare, not MH

Music Association of Thailand (Royal Patronage)

The closest music-worker welfare body. It runs ad-hoc charity concerts ("ด้วยใจ") raising relief funds for ill and deceased musician members, and acts as a government-recognized certifying body for the COVID-era 5,000-baht relief payment to musicians. Episodic welfare — not a standing mental-health service (the same welfare-not-MH pattern as SADEM in Argentina).7

Exists · general / government

The policy layer

Thailand has a national Mental Health Hotline 1323 (DMH), the multi-stakeholder HOPE Taskforce (est. 2019, with the Royal Thai Police and social-media influencers), and a national suicide-prevention plan to 2030 aligned to WHO's LIVE LIFE package. The policy layer exists — but it is general-population, not music-sector.8

Confirms the gap

Backline's directory: zero Thailand orgs

Backline — the US hub that aggregates music-industry mental-health resources worldwide (now with Spotify) — lists organizations for other countries (e.g. Japan, New Zealand) but nothing for Thailand. A Thai music worker searching the field's main directory finds no Thai music-MH org to reach.9

The comparison case the Taiwan opportunity rests on. Thailand has (a) a policy layer for suicide prevention, (b) a general emotional-support helpline a musician would actually reach (Samaritans of Thailand), and (c) an ad-hoc music-welfare body — but no music-specific mental-health organization. That is exactly the structure behind the project's "floor (rights) vs room (services)" thesis: Thailand has some floor and some general room, but the music-specific room is empty. It mirrors Taiwan, where 藝創工會 supplies a broad-arts floor and the national plan supplies general counseling, but no music-specific MH body exists.5

07 — Strengths & weaknesses

What works, what to watch

Proven, recognized method

40+ years of befriending in the Befrienders Worldwide lineage; rigorous volunteer screening and training. The model travels well and is cheap to run relative to clinical care.

Bilingual, multi-city

Thai + English, Bangkok + Chiang Mai — broader reach than a single-language, single-city line.

Low barrier to access

Free, anonymous, confidential, no diagnosis or paperwork — the opposite of the eligibility gates that exclude precarious workers from grant-based bodies.

Trusted, neutral brand

Explicitly non-religious / non-political; a name a distressed person can call without fear of judgment.

Donation-dependent, fragile

~500k baht/year on donations, only episodic grants, no government base — structurally fragile, as the COVID closure showed.

Not 24/7

Thai line noon–10pm; English line call-back only — coverage gaps exactly when crises peak overnight, unlike the Singapore/Malaysia peers.

Capacity-bound & stale reporting

Service scale is capped by volunteer supply (busy signals were a stated problem), and there is no recent annual report — the public evidence base is a decade old.

General, not specialized

It serves everyone, so it serves no occupational group — including musicians — with anything tailored.

08 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. Borrow the befriending method, not the funding

    A confidential, non-judgmental listening line staffed by trained lay volunteers (not clinicians) is the most cost-effective crisis-support primitive in this whole series. A Taiwan music-MH NPO could stand up a Mandarin befriending line for music workers far more cheaply than a clinical service. The method is the asset.2

  2. Do not rely on donations alone — it is the failure mode

    Samaritans of Thailand's donation-only base left it thinly resourced, not 24/7, and unable to stay open through COVID. Taiwan's NT$5.63B national MH plan and Ministry of Culture funding are a base a startup should anchor to first, with donations as a supplement — the opposite of the Thai funding posture.2

  3. Specialize where the general system is general

    Samaritans is valuable precisely because it is the floor — but it serves no occupational group with anything tailored. The Taiwan opportunity is the music-specific room that Thailand (and Taiwan) lacks: industry-aware volunteers, occupational-health framing, gig/touring content. Build the thing the general line cannot be.

  4. Partner with the general line; don't rebuild it

    The right relationship to a body like Samaritans (or Taiwan's general counseling lines) is referral, not duplication — a music-MH NPO refers acute crises to the trained crisis line and adds the music-sector layer on top. Befrienders' model shows the value of a shared standard plus a referral directory.4

  5. A welfare body is not a mental-health body

    Thailand's Music Association does music welfare (illness/death relief), not MH; Argentina's SADEM does health insurance, not standing MH. Across the region, music-specific welfare exists more often than music-specific MH. A Taiwan NPO should be explicit that mental-health support is the distinct, missing layer — not assume an existing welfare or union body already covers it.7
