Demands & Resources Associated with Mental Health Among Norwegian Professional Musicians

Work, 63(1), 39–47 · Paywalled (IOS Press)

DOI: 10.3233/WOR-192906

Reviewed by 陳棉 · 2026-07-05

↓ Scroll
Part A — Summary

Summary

What the study set out to do, and what it found

This cross-sectional study asks which personal and work-related factors best explain psychological distress in professional musicians, organised around the Job Demands–Resources (JD-R) model — strain arises when job demands outrun the resources, personal or organisational, a worker can draw on. The sample is large: 1,607 professional Norwegian musicians drawn from the Norwegian Musician's Union. The authors fit a hierarchical multiple regression, entering blocks of predictors (personality, then work factors) so the added contribution of each layer can be read against distress as the outcome.1

The headline result is that personal factors dominate. Level of neuroticism and sense of mastery had the strongest associations with psychological distress — neuroticism raising it, mastery buffering it. Extraversion, openness to experience, conscientiousness, job demands and social support also contributed to the final model, but to a lesser degree. The finding the authors flag as surprising is negative: work–family conflict, effort–reward imbalance and job control were NOT associated with distress once the other predictors were in the model.

From this the authors draw a training-and-industry recommendation rather than a workplace-redesign one: emphasise the early development of sense of mastery and social support in music education and across the industry. They are explicit that a cross-sectional design cannot establish direction, and call for prospective work.

Comment

What this adds, and what it can't show

The real strength is the sample and its lineage. 1,607 union members is a large occupational cohort for this field, and — a fact the abstract alone does not reveal — it is the same dataset behind Détári et al. (2020), the companion paper already reviewed in this project, which compared these musicians against 8,517 general-workforce employees.2 Détári showed that musicians, relative to the general workforce, report more work–family conflict and perceive their work as more demanding. Aalberg then shows that within musicians those same work variables largely drop out as predictors of distress once personality is entered. The group is distinctive on the work stressors, yet the work stressors are not what best tracks who is distressed.

The tension worth keeping

On the same 1,607 people, musicians differ from the general workforce on work–family conflict, demands and control — yet inside the musician sample those work variables don't predict who is distressed once personality is entered. "Fix conditions" and "who suffers" are not the same lever.

However, the JD-R framing invites a reading the data cannot bear, and the design compounds it. Everything is self-reported at one time point, so neuroticism and distress are related across instruments that share method variance and arguably share a construct — trait negative affectivity inflates both how one answers a personality inventory and a distress scale. A large part of the "personality dominates" story could be this artefact rather than a causal ordering, and a cross-sectional regression cannot separate them. The recommendation to develop mastery early is humane, but the study provides association, not evidence that mastery-building interventions reduce distress — as the authors acknowledge.

Three cautions bear on transfer to a Taiwan NPO. First, generalisability: Norwegian musicians are unionised and operate inside a strong welfare-state floor — the "floor vs room" distinction this project tracks. Findings about which personal resources matter may travel; the muted role of effort–reward imbalance and job control may be partly a Nordic artefact. Second, the null findings are model-dependent — "not associated in the final model" means after personality is entered, not that job control is irrelevant to musicians' lives; Détári, on the same people, shows it is not. Third, the elevation is not confined to professionals: Vaag et al. (2021) found Norwegian music and arts students already report higher anxiety (OR 1.60) and depression (OR 1.41) than the general student body,3 which supports Aalberg's intervene-early conclusion better than Aalberg's own cross-sectional data do.

A large, well-sampled cohort whose value lies less in its own cross-sectional model than in what it adds to the same-dataset companion paper — read the two as a pair, and treat "personality dominates" as a hypothesis about measurement as much as about people.
