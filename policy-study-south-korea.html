Policy Study 004 · 2026.6

Framing

Why South Korea, Why Policy

Both the Floor and the Room

This is an added comparator beyond the project's original six-region set — the expansion past six was authorized. The aim is unchanged: read how a jurisdiction protects the mental health and welfare of music and performing-arts workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies. South Korea earns its place because it does something no region in the original cycle did. Japan built the floor of rights (labor law) but left the care layer to a volunteer worker body. The EU diagnosed the precarity–distress link but had no competence to deliver.

South Korea, by contrast, has built both halves at once: a statutory rights floor (industrial-accident insurance, employment insurance, an anti–unfair-practice law with a complaints ombudsman) and a standing, state-backed psychological-counselling service for artists, running since 2014.

It is the natural experiment in what it looks like when a government tries to do both the floor and the room — and the first verified case in this project of a public artist mental-health service.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · The Floor

Statute, Then Insurance

South Korea's cultural-policy architecture rests on two statutes and one public foundation, all administered by the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism (문화체왙관광부, MCST). Unlike Japan — which reached the freelance creative workforce only recently and obliquely, through transaction law — South Korea built a dedicated artist-welfare statute more than a decade ago and has been layering social-insurance and rights protections onto it ever since.

01 The Artist Welfare Act (예술인복지법) enacted 2011-11-17 · effective 2012-11-18

South Korea enacted the 예술인복지법 (Act No. 11089) on 17 November 2011, taking effect 18 November 2012.1 Its stated purpose (Article 1) is to protect artists' professional status and rights and to support their welfare so as to advance creative activity. The Act mandates: development and dissemination of standard contracts (표준계약서) by the state (Article 5); protection against occupational injury (업무상 재해) by cross-reference to the Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act (Article 7); the establishment of the Korea Artists Welfare Foundation (한국예술인복지재단) (Article 8); and an artist-activity-certification (예술활동증명) system (Article 3-2) that operates as the gateway to nearly all welfare programmes. It did at the outset what Japan only began to approach in 2024: it named freelance artists as a protected category and stood up an institution to serve them.

02 Industrial-accident insurance reaches artists (예술인 산재보험) 2012 voluntary · mandatory 2021-07-01

Industrial-accident insurance first reached artists alongside the Welfare Act's enforcement in 2012 — initially as voluntary enrolment via the small-business-owner special case (중소기업사업주 특례), with the Korea Artists Welfare Foundation administering premium support under Article 7 of the Welfare Act.1 A 2021 reform — the labour-provider special case (노무제공자 특례, Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act arts. 91-15 to 91-19, in force 1 July 2021) — later moved artists onto a mandatory footing.2

03 Employment insurance for artists (예술인 고용보험) effective 2020-12-10

On 10 December 2020, artists' employment insurance took effect — created not by a separate artist law but by folding artists into the mainstream Employment Insurance Act (고용보험법, Act No. 17429, promulgated 9 June 2020) as a new Chapter 5-2 ("Application to insured persons who are artists," Article 77-2).3 It covers a person who concludes a cultural-arts service contract (문화예술용역 관련 계약) and directly provides their own labour, above a monthly-average-income threshold of ₩500,000 (short-term artists on contracts under one month are covered regardless of income). The benefits are job-seeking allowance (구직급여) — requiring ≥9 months of contributions in the preceding 24 months, paid 120–270 days — and maternity benefit (출산전후급여) — requiring ≥3 months of contributions, paid 90 days (120 for multiples). The Ministry of Employment and Labour framed the reform explicitly as "the first step toward universal employment insurance" (전국민고용보험의 첫거음). By 2 December 2021, one year after launch, about 95,000 artists had acquired insured status, with music workers the second-largest sector (16.4%) after broadcasting/entertainment (28.7%).4

A correction worth recording. The contribution structure is often mis-stated as a "0.8% rate." On the primary sources, 0.8 is the income-base factor (premiums are levied on contract value × 0.8, after a flat 20% expense deduction), not the rate.

The actual unemployment-benefit rate was 1.6% at launch and 1.4% from July 2021, split half-and-half between the artist and the commissioning business, with low-income artists able to receive an 80% premium subsidy via the 두루누리 (Durunuri) programme.5

Section 01 · Rights & the Room

A Remedy Body, and a Counselling Service

04 The Artist Rights Guarantee Act (예술인권리보장법) enacted 2021-09-24 · effective 2022-09-25

The 「예술인의 지위와 권리의 보장에 관한 법률」 (Act No. 18466), enacted 24 September 2021 and effective 25 September 2022, is South Korea's anti–unfair-practice and anti-harassment instrument for artists.6 Article 13 prohibits unfair practices (불공정행위) by state bodies, arts-support agencies, and arts businesses — including coercing unfair contract terms using a superior position, refusing or delaying fair revenue distribution, and obstructing artistic activity. Its Chapter 4 (Articles 16–19) establishes a gender-equitable arts environment, with statutory duties to prevent and respond to sexual harassment and sexual violence (성펄론·성폭력). Crucially — and unlike Japan's split JFTC/MHLW enforcement — the Act builds its own remedy machinery: a relief committee under the MCST Minister (Art. 20), an Artist Protection Officer / ombudsman (예술인보호관, Art. 27) with investigative powers (Art. 29), a reporting channel (예술인신문고, Art. 28), and escalating remedies — corrective recommendations and orders, suspension of state funding, mediation, and fines (Arts. 32–41). South Korea did not just declare rights; it built an office to enforce them.

KAWF — and, uniquely, a mental-health service

The Korea Artists Welfare Foundation (KAWF, 한국예술인복지재단), established under the Welfare Act on 19 November 2012 and supervised by MCST, runs the country's artist-welfare programmes: the flagship arts-activity preparation fund — a low-income grant of ₩3,000,000 per person for 2026, with 18,333 selectees budgeted — a living-stability loan scheme, an artist-dispatch programme, legal consulting, administration of the artists' insurance, and the artist-activity certification that gates them all.7 Certified artists passed 200,000 cumulatively by late October 2025, and the 2026 preparation fund is gated on that certification plus an income-recognition test.8

And then the item that sets South Korea apart in this entire project: a standing, state-backed psychological-counselling service for artists — 예술인 심리상댠 — run by KAWF since June 2014.9 It began as a pilot (8 centres in Seoul/Gyeonggi, ~80 artists) with the psychology profession — the Korean Psychological Association and its division the Korean Clinical Psychology Association (한국심리학회 · 한국임상심리학회); the Korean Association for Suicide Prevention (한국자삼예방협회) joined later, through a June 2024 memorandum of understanding. The service offers funded psychological testing; individual counselling of up to 12 sessions (work and career problems, depression, anxiety, relationships, life-crisis events, financial stress); group counselling for arts organisations; crisis intervention for suicide risk and sexual violence; and suicide-prevention "gatekeeper" training. Eligibility is open to any artist with valid activity certification, including emerging artists.

As in Japan and the EU, a targeted PubMed search located no pan-Korea probability-sample prevalence study of musicians' or performers' mental health; the indexed Korean evidence base is small-N, convenience-sample, and intervention-oriented. The nearest occupational study is Lim et al. (2019), a cross-sectional study of 139 Korean dance artists (performing artists, not musicians) that linked an identity imbalance on job value to higher depressive symptoms — suggestive, but a convenience sample reporting associations, not population prevalence.10

Part B — Appraisal

Section 02 · Reading the Model

Both Halves — But the Room Is Small

What South Korea gets right is that it built both halves

Every prior region in this project lands on one side of a recurring divide: the floor of rights (Japan's labor law) or the room of services (the U.S. charitable model) — rarely both, and never the care layer from the state. South Korea is the exception. On the floor side it is, if anything, more complete than Japan: a dedicated artist-welfare statute since 2012, industrial-accident insurance from the same year, employment insurance from 2020 (with the artist cohort deliberately used as the wedge for universal coverage), and a 2022 rights law that — unlike Japan's externally-enforced Freelance Act — carries its own ombudsman, reporting channel, and corrective-order powers. On the room side it does the thing this project has been looking for across five regions and not found: a government-backed psychological-counselling service for artists, with funded individual and group sessions, explicit suicide-risk crisis intervention, and clinical-association partners — running continuously since 2014.9 For an NPO whose thesis is that creative precarity damages mental health, South Korea is the proof-of-concept that a state can occupy both roles at once.

However, the room is small relative to the floor

The counselling service is real and reasonably well documented: KAWF's own decade review puts its 2024 scale at 1,732 artists served across 47 centres on a ₩1.45 billion budget (up from ₩195 million in 2015), with roughly 10,000 artists reached over ten years.9 But set against the floor — 200,000+ certified artists and ~95,000 insured in year one — those ~1,700 annual users are a fraction of one percent of the certified population: the architecture protects far more incomes than it touches minds.8 And the binding constraint is the same one that has limited every region: there is still no pan-Korea probability-sample prevalence study of musicians' mental health to right-size it against. The state has built a care layer essentially on faith in the precarity–distress link — the correct bet, on this project's reading, but one running ahead of its own domestic evidence. The certification gateway is also a double edge: it makes targeting clean, but the uncertified and most precarious sit outside both the floor and the room.

Read the structure with care

The legal and institutional facts here are firm and government-sourced: the two statutes, their dates and law numbers, the insurance routes, and the counselling programme were each read in Korean on law.go.kr, moel.go.kr, korea.kr, and kawf.kr.1 Two cautions. The current enrollment scale is only verifiable to the 95,000 cumulative as of December 2021; no later primary figure was confirmable, and a widely-circulated "29.2% coverage" figure appeared only in secondary text and is not asserted here.4 And the mental-health evidence anchor is a dance-artist study, not a musician study, with the rest of the Korean musician literature small-N convenience work.10

Verdict

South Korea is the first region in this project to build both the rights floor and the care room — the strongest single template for "what good looks like" — while showing that the room stays small (≈1,700 artists a year against 200,000+ certified) and built ahead of any domestic measure of the need it is meant to meet.

Part C — For This Project

Section 03 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 Point to South Korea as the existence proof that the state CAN run artist mental-health care

This is the headline transferable asset. Across Japan, the EU, and the rest of the original cycle, a Taiwan NPO could only argue that the care layer is a gap the state has left open. South Korea changes the argument: a government, through a public foundation (KAWF), has operated a funded artist counselling service — individual and group sessions, crisis intervention, suicide-prevention training, clinical-association partners — since 2014.9 When advocating to 文化部 or 衛福部, a Taiwan NPO can now cite a working, decade-old East Asian precedent for a state-backed service, not just a need.

3.2 The model worth borrowing is the foundation-as-delivery-vehicle

KAWF is the structural lesson: a single statutory public foundation that bundles income support, insurance administration, certification, legal consulting, and counselling under one roof, supervised by the culture ministry.7 Taiwan's nearest analogues (國藝會, TAICCA, and the 藝創工會 on the worker side) are fragmented across those functions. A Taiwan NPO can position itself either as the care specialist that a future Taiwanese KAWF-equivalent would contract, or as the advocate for consolidating artist welfare into one such body — with the counselling service designed in from the start, not bolted on.

3.3 Use the certification gateway carefully — it targets well but excludes the most precarious

예술활동증명 makes KAWF's programmes auditable and fraud-resistant, and it is why South Korea can report 200,000 certified artists.8 But it also means the uncertified — emerging, informal, or intermittently-working musicians, often the most distressed — fall outside the service. A Taiwan NPO's design lesson is the inverse of the state's: build a low-barrier or no-certification entry point for crisis and counselling support, precisely to reach the population a certification-gated state service structurally misses.

3.4 Generate the prevalence evidence South Korea never produced

South Korea built the care room on faith in the precarity–distress link, with no domestic probability-sample study of musicians' mental health to size it. The same evidence gap exists in Japan, the EU, and Taiwan.10 A Taiwan NPO's highest-leverage, lowest-cost early move is to be the evidence generator — a credible local prevalence survey of Taiwanese music workers' mental health would be a first-of-its-kind asset that no government in the region has produced, and it would right-size every service and advocacy argument that follows. South Korea shows the service can come first; Taiwan can do better by letting the evidence and the service grow together.

Limitations

  1. The mental-health service's scale is now sourced (KAWF 예술인뉴스 vol. 70): ₩1.45B budget, 1,732 artists, and 47 centres in 2024, ≈10,000 over the decade. What remains unpublished is a designated-centre service map and per-centre caseload; the exact 2014 launch day was also unconfirmable (corrected to "June 2014").
  2. Employment-insurance enrollment is verifiable only to ≈95,000 (December 2021); no later primary figure was confirmable, and the secondary "29.2% coverage" figure is excluded.
  3. The peer-reviewed anchor is a dance-artist depression study, not a musician study; the Korean musician-specific literature is a single small MPA RCT plus a sample-ambiguous mechanistic study, and Korean-language (KoreaMed/RISS) journals outside PubMed were not searched.
  4. The Industrial Accident Compensation Insurance Act host articles are resolved this pass via 법제처 easylaw: artists' mandatory coverage runs through the 노무제공자 특례 (arts. 91-15 to 91-19, in force 2021-07-01); the 2012 route via Welfare Act Art. 7 was voluntary (중소기업사업주 특례).
  5. The current contribution rate (1.4%) and the 0.8 income-base factor were verified on KAWF (a statutory foundation) rather than a ministry/statute page.
  6. Loan amounts/interest for the living-stability scheme live on artloan.kr and were not fetched.
  7. This is a policy scan, not an exhaustive review; prefecture/metropolitan-level arts support and the full 산재보험 statute were noted but not deep-read.
  8. No direct contact with MCST, KAWF, the Ministry of Employment and Labour, or 근로복지공단.
