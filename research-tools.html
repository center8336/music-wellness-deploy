Four layers — keep them distinct

(1) Theoretical models explain why work harms or protects health. (2) Psychosocial-exposure instruments measure the work environment (the cause). (3) Outcome measures measure the mental-health result (the effect). (4) Standards tell organizations how to manage psychosocial risk. A credible study pairs a layer-2 exposure instrument with a layer-3 outcome measure, anchored in a layer-1 model, and aligns advocacy to a layer-4 standard — because measuring the environment is not the same as diagnosing the person.

01 — Theoretical models

Why work affects health

The conceptual frameworks that explain how job conditions produce strain or wellbeing. JDC-S and ERI are the two the Norwegian musician study builds on; JD-R is the dominant modern umbrella.

Job Demand–Control (JDC)

Karasek · demand–control
Strain rises with high demands + low control ("high-strain" jobs carry the most risk).
✅ Karasek, R. A. (1979). Job demands, job decision latitude, and mental strain. Administrative Science Quarterly, 24(2), 285–308. doi.org/10.2307/2392498

Demand–Control–Support (JDCS)

iso-strain
Adds social support as a third axis; high demand + low control + low support is worst ("iso-strain").
✅ Johnson, J. V., & Hall, E. M. (1988). Job strain, work place social support, and cardiovascular disease. American Journal of Public Health, 78(10), 1336–1342. doi.org/10.2105/AJPH.78.10.1336

Effort–Reward Imbalance (ERI)

Siegrist
Strain from a mismatch between high effort and low reward (money, esteem, security); worsened by overcommitment.
✅ Siegrist, J. (1996). Adverse health effects of high-effort/low-reward conditions. Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 1(1), 27–41. doi.org/10.1037/1076-8998.1.1.27

Job Demands–Resources (JD-R)

the modern umbrella
Any demand drives a health-impairment path; any resource drives a motivational path. Broader than JDC-S.
✅ Demerouti, E., Bakker, A. B., Nachreiner, F., & Schaufeli, W. B. (2001). The job demands-resources model of burnout. Journal of Applied Psychology, 86(3), 499–512. doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.86.3.499 · review: Bakker & Demerouti (2007), JMP, 22(3), 309–328, doi.org/10.1108/02683940710733115

Conservation of Resources (COR)

Hobfoll
Stress as actual or threatened loss of valued resources; the backbone of burnout research.
✅ Hobfoll, S. E. (1989). Conservation of resources: A new attempt at conceptualizing stress. American Psychologist, 44(3), 513–524. doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.44.3.513

Effort–Recovery model

Meijman & Mulder
Load reactions from effort must be reversed by recovery; insufficient recovery accumulates strain. Apt for touring.
⚠️ Meijman, T. F., & Mulder, G. (1998). Psychological aspects of workload. In P. J. D. Drenth et al. (Eds.), Handbook of work and organizational psychology (2nd ed., Vol. 2, pp. 5–33). Psychology Press. (book chapter; no native 1998 DOI)

Challenge–Hindrance stressors

Cavanaugh et al.
Distinguishes growth-promoting "challenge" demands from draining "hindrance" demands.
✅ Cavanaugh, M. A., Boswell, W. R., Roehling, M. V., & Boudreau, J. W. (2000). An empirical examination of self-reported work stress among U.S. managers. Journal of Applied Psychology, 85(1), 65–74. doi.org/10.1037/0021-9010.85.1.65

Vitamin model

Warr
Job features affect mental health non-linearly — some plateau, some harm at very high levels.
✅ Warr, P. (1987). Work, unemployment, and mental health. Oxford University Press. · refined: Warr (1994), Work & Stress, 8(2), 84–97, doi.org/10.1080/02678379408259982

Recovery Experience

Sonnentag & Fritz
Off-job recovery via psychological detachment, relaxation, mastery, and control (with a validated questionnaire).
✅ Sonnentag, S., & Fritz, C. (2007). The Recovery Experience Questionnaire. Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 12(3), 204–221. doi.org/10.1037/1076-8998.12.3.204
02 — Psychosocial-exposure instruments

Measure the work environment

Validated questionnaires that quantify the cause — the psychosocial conditions of the job. COPSOQ III is the best ready-made comprehensive instrument; JCQ and QPSNordic are what the Détári musician study used.

Job Content Questionnaire

JCQ · operationalizes JDC-S
Demands, decision latitude, social support, job insecurity. Validated Chinese versions exist.
Licensed (JCQ Center)
✅ Karasek, R., Brisson, C., Kawakami, N., Houtman, I., Bongers, P., & Amick, B. (1998). The Job Content Questionnaire (JCQ). Journal of Occupational Health Psychology, 3(4), 322–355. doi.org/10.1037/1076-8998.3.4.322

Effort–Reward Imbalance Questionnaire

ERI-Q
Measures effort, reward, and overcommitment — the operational ERI scales.
Free for research (Düsseldorf group)
✅ Siegrist, J., Starke, D., Chandola, T., Godin, I., Marmot, M., Niedhammer, I., & Peter, R. (2004). The measurement of effort-reward imbalance at work: European comparisons. Social Science & Medicine, 58(8), 1483–1499. doi.org/10.1016/S0277-9536(03)00351-4

Copenhagen Psychosocial Questionnaire III

COPSOQ III
Comprehensive, internationally validated set of psychosocial factors. Top pick for a ready-made instrument.
Free / open · COPSOQ International Network
✅ Burr, H., Berthelsen, H., Moncada, S., Nübling, M., Dupret, E., Demiral, Y., … Pohrt, A. (2019). The Third Version of the Copenhagen Psychosocial Questionnaire. Safety and Health at Work, 10(4), 482–503. doi.org/10.1016/j.shaw.2019.10.002

HSE Management Standards Indicator Tool

UK HSE · 7 domains
35 items across demands, control, managerial & peer support, relationships, role, change. Action-oriented.
Free (UK HSE)
✅ Cousins, R., Mackay, C. J., Clarke, S. D., Kelly, C., Kelly, P. J., & McCaig, R. H. (2004). 'Management Standards' and work-related stress in the UK: Practical development. Work & Stress, 18(2), 113–136. doi.org/10.1080/02678370410001734322 · validation: Edwards et al. (2008), 22(2), 96–107, doi.org/10.1080/02678370802166599

QPSNordic

Nordic general questionnaire
~129 items / 26 scales of psychosocial work factors. Used (with JCQ) in the Détári musician study. No Traditional-Chinese version known.
Free for research · report (no DOI)
⚠️ Dallner, M., Elo, A.-L., Gamberale, F., Hottinen, V., Knardahl, S., Lindström, K., Skogstad, A., & Ørhede, E. (2000). Validation of the General Nordic Questionnaire (QPSNordic) for psychological and social factors at work (Nord 2000:12). Nordic Council of Ministers.

NIOSH Worker Well-Being Questionnaire

WellBQ · 5 domains
Holistic worker wellbeing — work experience, policies/culture, environment/safety, health status, home/community.
Free to reproduce (NIOSH)
✅ National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health. (2021). NIOSH Worker Well-Being Questionnaire (WellBQ) (DHHS [NIOSH] Publication No. 2021-110). cdc.gov/niosh/docs/2021-110 · validation: Chari et al. (2022), JOEM, 64(8), 707–717, doi.org/10.1097/JOM.0000000000002585

Burnout & engagement

Maslach Burnout Inventory

MBI
Emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, personal accomplishment — the field standard for burnout.
Licensed (Mind Garden)
✅ Maslach, C., & Jackson, S. E. (1981). The measurement of experienced burnout. Journal of Occupational Behaviour, 2(2), 99–113. doi.org/10.1002/job.4030020205

Utrecht Work Engagement Scale

UWES
Vigor, dedication, absorption — the positive pole (engagement, not just absence of burnout).
Free for research
✅ Schaufeli, W. B., Bakker, A. B., & Salanova, M. (2006). The measurement of work engagement with a short questionnaire. Educational and Psychological Measurement, 66(4), 701–716. doi.org/10.1177/0013164405282471

Copenhagen Burnout Inventory

CBI
Personal, work-related, and client-related burnout. Free / public domain.
Free / public domain
✅ Kristensen, T. S., Borritz, M., Villadsen, E., & Christensen, K. B. (2005). The Copenhagen Burnout Inventory. Work & Stress, 19(3), 192–207. doi.org/10.1080/02678370500297720

Oldenburg Burnout Inventory

OLBI
Exhaustion + disengagement, with positively and negatively framed items.
Free for research
✅ Demerouti, E., Bakker, A. B., Vardakou, I., & Kantas, A. (2003). The convergent validity of two burnout instruments. European Journal of Psychological Assessment, 19(1), 12–23. doi.org/10.1027/1015-5759.19.1.12
03 — Mental-health / wellbeing outcome measures

Measure the result

Short, validated self-report screens for the effect. Pair one or more of these with a layer-2 exposure instrument — exposure is not diagnosis.

PHQ-9

depression · 9 items
Depression severity mapped to diagnostic criteria. Ubiquitous, brief.
Free
✅ Kroenke, K., Spitzer, R. L., & Williams, J. B. W. (2001). The PHQ-9: Validity of a brief depression severity measure. Journal of General Internal Medicine, 16(9), 606–613. doi.org/10.1046/j.1525-1497.2001.016009606.x

GAD-7

anxiety · 7 items
Generalized anxiety severity. Pairs naturally with PHQ-9.
Free
✅ Spitzer, R. L., Kroenke, K., Williams, J. B. W., & Löwe, B. (2006). A brief measure for assessing generalized anxiety disorder: The GAD-7. Archives of Internal Medicine, 166(10), 1092–1097. doi.org/10.1001/archinte.166.10.1092

K6 / K10

distress · population monitoring
Non-specific psychological distress; designed for population prevalence/trend monitoring.
Free
✅ Kessler, R. C., Andrews, G., Colpe, L. J., Hiripi, E., Mroczek, D. K., Normand, S.-L. T., Walters, E. E., & Zaslavsky, A. M. (2002). Short screening scales to monitor population prevalences and trends in non-specific psychological distress. Psychological Medicine, 32(6), 959–976. doi.org/10.1017/S0033291702006074

WHO-5 Well-Being Index

positive wellbeing · 5 items
Brief positive-wellbeing measure (not just symptom absence). Widely cross-validated.
Free
✅ Topp, C. W., Østergaard, S. D., Søndergaard, S., & Bech, P. (2015). The WHO-5 Well-Being Index: A systematic review of the literature. Psychotherapy and Psychosomatics, 84(3), 167–176. doi.org/10.1159/000376585

WEMWBS

mental wellbeing · 14 items
Warwick–Edinburgh Mental Well-being Scale — positively framed, UK-validated.
License required (free most non-commercial)
✅ Tennant, R., Hiller, L., Fishwick, R., Platt, S., Joseph, S., Weich, S., Parkinson, J., Secker, J., & Stewart-Brown, S. (2007). The Warwick–Edinburgh Mental Well-being Scale (WEMWBS). Health and Quality of Life Outcomes, 5, Article 63. doi.org/10.1186/1477-7525-5-63

GHQ-12

general distress / caseness
General Health Questionnaire — psychological distress screening. (Origin: Goldberg 1972; scaled version Goldberg & Hillier 1979.)
Licensed (GL Assessment)
✅ Goldberg, D. P., Gater, R., Sartorius, N., Ustun, T. B., Piccinelli, M., Gureje, O., & Rutter, C. (1997). The validity of two versions of the GHQ in the WHO study of mental illness in general health care. Psychological Medicine, 27(1), 191–197. doi.org/10.1017/S0033291796004242

Perceived Stress Scale

PSS · appraised stress
Degree to which life is appraised as stressful (14-, 10-, and 4-item forms).
Free
✅ Cohen, S., Kamarck, T., & Mermelstein, R. (1983). A global measure of perceived stress. Journal of Health and Social Behavior, 24(4), 385–396. doi.org/10.2307/2136404
04 — Management / policy standards

How to manage psychosocial risk

The frameworks to cite when framing musicians' mental health as an occupational issue and making the case to government, employers, and funders.

ISO 45003:2021

international standard
Psychological health & safety at work — guidelines for managing psychosocial risk within an OH&S system. The one to cite for 勞動部/文化部.
✅ International Organization for Standardization. (2021). Occupational health and safety management — Psychological health and safety at work — Guidelines for managing psychosocial risks (ISO 45003:2021). iso.org/standard/64283.html

WHO Guidelines on mental health at work

WHO · 2022
Evidence-based global recommendations to prevent harm and support workers' mental health.
✅ World Health Organization. (2022). Guidelines on mental health at work. who.int/publications/i/item/9789240053052

WHO & ILO policy brief

mental health at work · 2022
Translates the WHO guidelines into actions for governments, employers, and worker organizations.
✅ World Health Organization & International Labour Organization. (2022). Mental health at work: Policy brief. iris.who.int/handle/10665/362983

PRIMA-EF

European framework
The European framework/model for assessing and managing workplace psychosocial risk.
✅ Leka, S., & Cox, T. (Eds.). (2008). PRIMA-EF: Guidance on the European framework for psychosocial risk management (Protecting Workers' Health Series No. 9). World Health Organization. iris.who.int/handle/10665/43966

CSA Z1003 (Canada)

national standard
Voluntary national standard for a psychological health & safety management system — a model others cite.
✅ CSA Group & Bureau de normalisation du Québec. (2013). Psychological health and safety in the workplace (CAN/CSA-Z1003-13/BNQ 9700-803/2013, reaffirmed 2022). CSA Group.
05 — Applying it

How to use this for a Taiwan music-worker study

A ready-to-build stack

Anchor the theory in JD-R (with JDC-S/ERI as named sub-models, for comparability with the Norwegian musician study); field COPSOQ III (or JCQ) as the psychosocial-exposure instrument; pair with PHQ-9 + GAD-7 + WHO-5 as outcome measures; and cite ISO 45003 in the policy/advocacy section. Exposure instruments and outcome measures sit in different layers — field both, because measuring the environment is not the same as diagnosing the person.

Used in our research. The Détári et al. (2020) critical reading measured seven psychosocial dimensions via JCQ and QPSNordic items — it notes JCQ has validated Chinese versions while QPSNordic does not, which is a direct input to any Taiwan replication. No other deploy report currently fields a named instrument from this catalog.
