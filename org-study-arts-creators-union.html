藝創工會

Org Study · Est. 2011

↓ Scroll

Why this case is different

Backline, MusiCares, and Music Minds Matter are all mental-health organizations. 藝創工會 is not — it is a trade union of self-employed arts creators, and it is the closest thing Taiwan has to organized infrastructure for the music workforce. It is read here as three things at once: a partner (the natural Allies-model anchor for a Taiwan NPO), an organizing model (how a precarious creative workforce builds dues, funds, contracts, and trust — and reaches financial self-sufficiency), and an incumbent benchmark (what it delivers on occupational health and harassment in 2025 — and where mental health is, and is not, present). The finding in one line: Taiwan is not an institutional void, but the music-specific mental-health layer is genuinely missing — and this union is the body to build it with.

01 — Profile

A union that made art creation an occupation1

藝創工會 (the Taipei City Arts Creators' Occupational Trade Union) is a labour union — not an NPO or foundation — for self-employed creative workers across the arts. It organizes the precise population the global research base flags as most exposed to occupational and psychological risk: freelance, project-based, irregular-income creators, including musicians and sound artists.

From recognition fight to self-funding union2

The union was founded on 19 February 2011, the product of a multi-year push to win recognition that "art creation is an occupation" — a 2011 中國時報 headline read 「藝術創作終於成為職業」. Its founding chair was the performance artist 湯皇珍; leadership has since rotated through nine terms to the current chair 蔡雅庭, with 8th-term chair 張文駿 (stage name 張吉米) leading the high-profile 2023 advocacy in §4. Its defining trait is who it organizes — self-employed, no-fixed-employer creative workers — and that determines everything about its model.

02 — The union model

Dues, funds, and earned self-sufficiency

Three structural features make 藝創工會 instructive for anyone building a Taiwan creative-sector organization.

PILLAR 01

It monetizes a statutory function

Self-employed workers enrol in 勞健保 and (since 2022) 職災保險 through an occupational union. Membership is therefore practically necessary, not just ideological — a durable, recurring base a pure advocacy NPO lacks.3

PILLAR 02

It reached self-sufficiency — and left grants

It held an NCAF organizational-operations grant 2020–2023, then in 2024 deliberately did not re-apply, stating dues now cover operating costs and that it preferred not to compete with member applicants.3

PILLAR 03

Purpose-built funds, rules public

Beyond dues it runs three internal funds (Rights, Research, reserve). The fund rules are published; the balances are not, and this study does not estimate them.4

The NT$2,500 counselling subsidy inside the Rights Fund is the union's single most direct mental-health benefit — and its ceiling (≤15% of annual fund spend; a posted cap of about 59 member slots this year) is the clearest quantitative signal of how small the mental-health line is next to the union's labour-rights core.5

03 — The 2025 lens

Occupational health, 性平, and the mental-health question

Of the three issues this project tracks — occupational health, sexual harassment, and mental health — the union addresses the first two directly and the third only at the periphery.

Occupational safety & health — a physical-hazard framing6

The 職業安全衛生指引 section is a curated directory linking to nine external government OSH PDFs, re-framed for arts work — exhibition install, film/TV crews, heat hazard, night work, aerial platforms, rope access, heritage conservation. The hazards named are almost entirely physical. Mental health appears only obliquely: the lone MH-adjacent phrase ("fatigue management") is borrowed from a government night-work guide. Psychological hazard is not a named hazard category. Late 2025 the union added a performing-arts OSH cheat-sheet (懶人包) and OSH forums.

Sexual harassment / 性平 — a referral programme, not an adjudicating body7

The 性平專區 is explicitly framed as a response to Taiwan's 2023 arts-sector #MeToo wave. The union provides prevention training (with 台灣性別平等教育協會), legal consultation via a named lawyer (open to harassment parties including non-members), psychological counselling through the 勵馨基金會 蒲公英 sexual-assault centre (union pays the fee), and signposting to government hotlines. What it does not have is an internal investigating committee or complaint window — it brokers external lawyers, counsellors, and trainers rather than adjudicating cases. It has argued that self-employed creative workers still fall through the gaps of even the amended 2024 性平三法, and pushed for a Korea-style dedicated "藝術人保護官" (arts protection officer).

The survey series — Taiwan's best labour data on arts workers8

The 調查報告 section is, for this project, the union's most valuable asset: a longitudinal labour-data series on a population almost no one else measures — a 2019 overwork survey, 2022 and 2023 instalments, and the flagship 2024《性別平等與勞動情況調查》(n=944, fielded June–July 2024, NCAF-sponsored, 67% female; n verified against the report PDF).

Mental health is treated qualitatively, not as a headline metric. The report lists harms of bullying and harassment as 焦慮 / 失眠 / 憂鬱 / PTSD and calls for accessible counselling, but publishes no clean measured MH-prevalence rate in its accessible materials; the widely-circulated "過半壓力超標 / 2成達建議就醫" lines are sourced to press summaries (secondary source: press summaries) rather than the union's own chart, so they are reported here as secondary-quoted figures, not chart-verified hard numbers. The honest read: the union can field rigorous workforce surveys and has gestured at psychological strain — but mental health has not yet been measured as its own outcome.

What the union already covers

  • Occupational safety & health guidance (physical hazards)
  • Sexual-harassment training, legal aid, and referred counselling
  • Longitudinal labour, income, and harassment data
  • Equal-footing model contracts (incl. 3 for music)
  • Social-insurance & occupational-injury enrolment

The mental-health layer that's missing

  • No music-specific mental-health service
  • Psychological hazard absent from the OSH framing
  • MH delivered only via a capped NT$2,500 subsidy + outsourced 勵馨 partnership + government signposting
  • No measured MH outcome in the survey series
  • No Mandarin helpline / case management / peer-support for music workers
04 — Contracts, insurance & advocacy

The labour-rights core — and a decade of policy wins

Two everyday services bear directly on wellbeing. The 合約範本 library offers lawyer-drafted, equal-footing contract templates across visual (~13), performing (11), music (3 — licensing, project outsourcing, lyrics/composition commission), and criticism, version-dated 2024–2025 — reducing the contractual precarity that drives financial and psychological stress. And the union is the enrolment vehicle by which self-employed arts workers access occupational-injury insurance under the 《勞工職業災害保險及保護法》 (effective 2022-05-01), complemented by the 《文化藝術獎助及促進條例》 §9 (premium subsidies) and §10 (insurance in arts procurement); from 2026-01-01 the union's Tier-1 insured monthly salary rises to NT$29,500.9

Advocacy record10

The union's influence is unusually concrete for its size: a 2018 administrative-litigation win against the Ministry of Labor; 2020–2021 campaigning for occupational-injury coverage; a 2022 expansion of the arts-work fee-rate schedule; a 2023 statutory copyright-royalty increase of 25%; and court support for harassment victims. Its highest-profile moment was the 2023-07-18 Legislative Yuan press conference — with legislator 王婉諭 and a coalition of arts bodies — issuing four demands: enforce gender-workplace-equality law for arts workers; fund victim counselling via 文化部 + 衛福部; create a Korea-style "藝術人保護官"; and reform public-grant allocation. A 2025 statement insisted cultural exchange be premised on "保障勞動權益與身心自主" — a rare explicit nod to the psychological dimension. The pattern: a small, lawyer-backed union punching above its weight by coalition-building — the political capital a new MH NPO would otherwise lack.

05 — Governance

Lean, volunteer-run, term-limited11

The board comprises 9 directors + 3 alternates and 3 supervisors + 1 alternate, all unpaid, with the chair limited to one re-election. A gender quota is codified in the election rules — any constituency electing three or more representatives must include at least one of any gender, a structural 性平 commitment beyond the public-facing programme. Chairs have rotated through nine terms (founding 湯皇珍 → … → 8th 張文駿 / 張吉米 → 9th 蔡雅庭), giving continuity without entrenching any single figure. The flip side: an all-volunteer board and small secretariat constrain how much new service — such as a dedicated mental-health programme — the union can build without partners or funding.

06 — Strengths

What works

It already organizes the exact workforce

Reach into the self-employed creative population — including musicians and sound artists — through a membership tied to a statutory need. No new NPO can manufacture that relationship quickly.

Earned financial self-sufficiency

Reaching dues-funded operating breakeven and choosing to stop applying for operational grants signals a sustainable model — and a partner that won't compete for the same grant pool.

Real labour data

The 2019 → 2024 (n=944) survey series is the best existing evidence base on Taiwan arts workers' conditions, harassment exposure, and income — a ready foundation for a music-MH needs assessment.

A working "broker, don't build" model

On both harassment support and OSH the union delivers by brokering external expertise rather than employing it — a capital-efficient template a lean NPO can copy directly.

Demonstrated political capital

A decade of litigation wins, fee-schedule and royalty gains, and the 2023 Legislative Yuan coalition show it can move policy — reach a new org would take years to build.

07 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch (as a partner / model)

Mental health is peripheral and outsourced

The only direct MH service is a capped NT$2,500 subsidy plus the 勵馨 partnership and signposting; OSH omits psychological hazard; surveys don't measure MH. No music-specific MH service exists.

Capacity ceiling

An all-volunteer board and small secretariat limit new programming; a mental-health initiative would need to bring its own staffing and funding, not load it onto the union.

Opaque finances

Fund balances, total revenue, and even membership count are not public — the union's scale and headroom can't be independently sized.

Taipei-scoped, arts-wide

It is a Taipei City union covering all arts creators, not a national music-specific body — music workers are a subset, and nationwide reach would need building.

Unverified current leadership

The current 9th-term chair is named only on the union's own site, not corroborated externally — a reminder that basic facts here rest partly on self-report.

08 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental-health NPO should take

  1. Partner with the union first — it is the Allies-model anchor

    Make 藝創工會 a referral-and-awareness partner (the Music Minds Matter "Allies" pattern, executed through a union rather than trade brands). It supplies workforce reach and political legitimacy; the NPO supplies the mental-health service it lacks — the most direct realization of the Taiwan Opportunity thesis.

  2. Build the layer the union is missing — don't duplicate what it has

    Don't re-create contract templates, OSH directories, or insurance enrolment. Build the music-specific mental-health service — Mandarin helpline/chat, vetted-therapist case management, peer support, psychosocial-environment education — on top of the union's existing labour-rights base.

  3. Copy the "broker, don't build" delivery model

    The harassment programme shows a lean path: partner with existing clinical/legal bodies (a 勵馨 / 臺灣音樂治療學會 / performing-arts-medicine analogue) for delivery rather than hiring a clinical workforce — faster, cheaper, and survivable on a startup budget.

  4. Use the survey series as a ready-made needs assessment — then add the MH measure

    The 2024 n=944 instrument already captures harassment, income, and precarity. Co-field or extend it with a validated mental-health outcome (e.g. the JDC-S/ERI psychosocial framework), turning the union's data engine into the evidence base for funding and policy.

  5. Aim for earned-income self-sufficiency, not perpetual grants

    The union reached operating breakeven on dues and chose to exit operational grant dependence. Design a membership/partnership or earned-income line from the start — Ministry of Culture grants to launch; sustainable income to survive.

  6. Borrow the union's political playbook

    Its 2023 Legislative Yuan coalition and "藝術人保護官" demand show how arts-labour issues reach lawmakers here. Join such coalitions and align with the NT$5.63B 全民心理健康韌性計畫 (2025–2030)12 rather than lobby alone — the union is an experienced co-belligerent.

  7. Frame mental health as occupational health

    The union's blind spot — psychosocial hazard absent from its OSH framing — is the opening. Positioning musicians' mental health as an occupational safety issue lets a new org speak the union's language, plug into the OSH and 性平 work already underway, and make the case to 勞動部 / 文化部.
