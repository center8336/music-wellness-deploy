Framing

Why the EU, Why Policy

A Coordinator of Twenty-Seven

Third of a six-region policy cycle. The aim is the same each time: read how a jurisdiction protects the mental health and welfare of music and performing-arts workers — through law, public funding, and worker bodies. The EU is the odd unit out, because it is not one jurisdiction but a coordinator of twenty-seven.

Where Japan reached for labour law, the U.S. for charity, and Thailand for industrial policy, the EU has reached for the only lever its treaties allow: supranational coordination. Culture and social protection are national competences; Brussels cannot legislate a musician's contract in Lisbon or Helsinki.

So it has built, instead, an unusually thick layer of problem-definition and soft law. The EU is the natural experiment in whether naming and coordinating a problem, without the power to mandate the fix, moves the needle.

Part A — The Landscape

Section 01 · The Machine

The Coordination Machine

No region studied so far has produced as much paper on artist welfare as the EU — and that is the point, not a criticism. Lacking the competence to regulate working conditions directly, the Union works through the Open Method of Coordination (OMC): member states compare, learn, and converge voluntarily.

01 The OMC report — the diagnosis 2023

The anchor document, The status and working conditions of artists and cultural and creative professionals (4 July 2023), is the work of an expert group from all 27 member states' culture and employment ministries. It names the problem plainly — artists face "precarious working conditions, low, irregular and unpredictable incomes, high levels of self-employment, project-based careers and a lack of legal structures" — and its headline recommendation is an EU framework of guidelines and minimum standards via a Council Recommendation or Conclusions: non-binding by design.2

02 The Parliament wanted teeth Nov 2023

On 21 November 2023 the European Parliament adopted, by 433 to 100 with 99 abstentions, a resolution on an EU framework for the social and professional situation of artists. It went further than the OMC group: it called for new legislation — a binding directive on decent working conditions and accurate employment-status determination — a European platform, and social conditionality on EU culture funding.3

03 The Commission declined to legislate 2024

The Commission's response offered 13 non-legislative initiatives — a high-level round table, yearly thematic workshops with member states, a promise to "consider reinforcing social conditionality in the next cycle of Union programmes," and analytical tasks for labour bodies. No directive.4 The most recent move continues the pattern: the Culture Compass for Europe (adopted 12 November 2025) announces a forthcoming EU Artists' Charter setting out "common principles of protection and equity, while leaving Member States free to define their own models," first tabled at a High-Level Round Table on Working Conditions on 8 December 2025, with a structured stakeholder dialogue through 2026 and the Charter itself slated for Q2 2027.5

The EU does spend real money on culture — but on output, not welfare. Creative Europe runs on a €2.44 billion budget for 2021–2027, funding projects, cooperation, mobility, and market access; none of it is a social-protection or mental-health line.6 The structural welfare instruments — unemployment insurance, sickness pay, the "artist status" the OMC report wants defined — sit in national budgets and national law, where the EU can recommend but not require.4

Section 02 · The Mental-Health Layer

Where the EU Is Ahead

On the specific question this project cares about, the EU has done something none of the prior regions had: it has formally connected precarious work to mental health at the policy level. On 9 October 2023 the Council adopted Conclusions on "Mental Health and Precarious Work" — the first-ever EU conclusions on the interconnection between mental health and employment — warning that people in precarious work are "particularly at risk of developing negative stress that can lead to anxiety and depression."1 That is the precise causal chain the musician-mental-health literature relies on, written into an EU policy instrument.

It sits inside a broader push: the Commission's 7 June 2023 Communication, A comprehensive approach to mental health, the EU's first comprehensive approach to mental health (a Communication, not a formal "strategy"), bundling 20 flagship initiatives and €1.23 billion in financing opportunities pooled across existing instruments, around prevention, access to care, and reintegration — and explicitly naming "good mental health at work" and recognising culture as a relevant policy domain.7 None of these is artist-specific, and none is binding — but the EU is the only region in the cycle whose apparatus has, on the record, joined the two halves of this project's thesis. On the workplace front specifically, EU-OSHA's 2026–2028 Healthy Workplaces Campaign — Together for Mental Health at Work, launching October 2026 — makes psychosocial-risk assessment its cornerstone, the nearest the EU comes to operational follow-through on "mental health at work."10

What the peer-reviewed evidence shows

According to PubMed, the EU-based study that most directly touches musicians and mental health is Kuusi, Tervo-Niemelä and Viertiö (2024) in BMC Public Health. Surveying four church professions — pastors, church musicians (n=92), diaconal and youth workers — it found severe distress more common in all groups than in the general population, and identified loneliness as the single strongest correlate (for church musicians an odds ratio of 24.36, though with a very wide 95% CI of 2.78–213.72), while good work satisfaction was strongly protective.8 The finding is suggestive precisely because these musicians are not precarious freelancers: they are salaried, and still distressed, with loneliness — a relational feature of music work — doing more than income insecurity. A targeted search returned no pan-EU probability study specifically of freelance musicians' mental health; the closest evidence is national — a near-complete survey of the Norwegian Musicians' Union (Vaag et al., 2016) and the Danish "When Music Speaks" project — not an EU-wide probability sample (absence ≠ non-existence — Rule 4).11

Part B — Appraisal

Section 03 · Reading the Model

Diagnosis Without Delivery

What the EU gets right is the problem-definition

No other jurisdiction in this cycle has assembled the diagnosis as thoroughly. The OMC report is a genuinely useful cross-national map of artist precarity; the Parliament resolution is unusually direct in demanding binding action; and — most relevant here — the Council Conclusions on Mental Health and Precarious Work put the precarity-to-distress mechanism into an official EU text, while the €1.23bn comprehensive mental-health approach gives that text a budget and a home.1 For a project whose premise is that creative precarity damages mental health, the EU is the one region that has said so at the policy level — meaning the framing this NPO would advance is not fringe in Europe, but Council-endorsed.

However, the EU coordinates a fix it cannot compel

The limit is constitutional, not merely political: culture is a "supporting competence" and social protection a national one, so the Union's strongest instruments are recommendations, conclusions, work plans, and now a charter — every one non-binding. The sequence tells the story: the Parliament asked for a directive; the Commission answered with 13 soft initiatives; the 2025 Culture Compass formalises the soft form by naming an Artists' Charter that leaves "Member States free to define their own models."5 The thing that would actually change a musician's social-security coverage happens, if it happens, in 27 national legislatures the EU can only nudge.4 The risk is a widening gap between an ever-richer coordination layer and an unchanged delivery layer.

Read the evidence with three cautions

(1) The mental-health policy link is real but rhetorical — the Council Conclusions name the connection but bind no one and fund nothing directly; the €1.23bn sits with the general mental-health approach, not with artists.7 (2) The Artists' Charter is announced, not delivered — as of June 2026 a 2025 commitment with dialogue beginning in 2026 and text expected ~2027.5 (3) The peer-reviewed anchor is narrow — Finnish, salaried church musicians, a 92-person subgroup, with a loneliness odds ratio whose confidence interval spans two orders of magnitude; a strong signal, not a measurement.8

Verdict

The EU is the clearest example of the coordination route — the region that has best diagnosed and best articulated the link between creative precarity and mental health, and the region whose treaties guarantee it cannot, by itself, fix either.

Part C — For This Project

Section 04 · Translation

Lessons for a Taiwan NPO

3.1 Borrow the framing — it has already won the argument

The most transferable asset here is not an instrument but a sentence: an official body has stated that precarious work drives anxiety and depression, and that creative workers are disproportionately precarious. A Taiwan NPO advocating to 文化部 or 衛福部 can cite the EU's Council Conclusions and comprehensive mental-health approach as precedent that this linkage is mainstream policy thinking, not advocacy spin.

3.2 Coordination is not delivery — be the delivery the state defers

The EU's apparatus — reports, charters, recommendations — does not by itself reach the individual musician; in the EU because of divided competence, in Taiwan because policy moves slowly and culture-ministry money flows to output (TAICCA, grants, 國藝會) rather than worker welfare. Either way the care layer — counselling, peer support, crisis referral built for music workers — is the open lane, as in Japan and Thailand.

3.3 Loneliness, not just income, is a lever

The one EU peer-reviewed finding that touches musicians points away from the pure-precarity story: among salaried church musicians, loneliness was the dominant correlate of distress and work satisfaction the strongest protection. Peer community and belonging — cheap to build, hard for a state programme to deliver — may be where a small NPO has the most leverage per dollar, and the EU evidence gives that design choice a citation.

3.4 Watch the "artist status" debate

Across the EU's decade of coordination, the recurring structural recommendation is a defined legal "artist status" carrying portable social protection — and it remains a member-state matter, largely unbuilt. Taiwan faces the same question (extending 勞保/職業災害保険 and a stable status to project-based performers). Track it as the policy prize; position the NPO as evidence-generator and bridge service while the status question is unresolved. The member-state models cluster into three incompatible logics — itself the reason the EU cannot mandate one "artist status": contributory insurance funds (Germany Künstlersozialkasse, Austria KSVF), unemployment-based intermittency (France intermittents, Belgium statut d'artiste), and income / pay-floor instruments (Ireland Basic Income for the Arts — now moving pilot → permanent — and the Netherlands Fair Practice Code). For Taiwan the question is not which European model to copy but which logic best fits its labour-insurance architecture.9

Limitations

  1. The mental-health/precarity link is established as policy framing (Council Conclusions) and strategy (comprehensive approach), both non-binding; the report does not claim the EU funds or delivers artist-specific mental-health services — it does not.
  2. The Artists' Charter and parts of the Culture Compass are forthcoming; the report reads an announced roadmap, not an operating instrument.
  3. The peer-reviewed anchor is a single Finnish study of salaried church musicians with a small subgroup and a very wide key confidence interval; it informs the loneliness argument but cannot stand in for pan-EU prevalence.
  4. The direct Consilium press-release fetch returned empty (JS-rendered); the 9 Oct 2023 conclusions are verified via three independent secondary sources rather than the primary page.
  5. Primary EU legal texts were read via official summaries and sector reporting except where the EC Culture page was fetched in full; no national-level implementation in any specific member state was examined.
  6. No direct contact with the Commission, the OMC group, or sector bodies.
