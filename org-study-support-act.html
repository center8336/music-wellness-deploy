SUPPORT ACT

Org Study · Est. 1997

↓ Scroll

Why this case matters

Support Act is the only full-scale national music-industry charity in Asia-Pacific — the regional exception that proves the gap the rest of this project's org studies document. It is also the oldest org in this series' comparator set (incorporated 1997, before MusiCares-style galas reached the region) and the only one whose complete audited accounts were retrievable as primary documents. Three things make it the most instructive case yet for a Taiwan NPO: it was founded by trade bodies (ARIA, PPCA, APRA AMCOS) rather than by a label or a benefactor; it survived a textbook government windfall-and-cliff (A$30M of COVID-era federal money, then zero) that the audited statements document year by year; and it has diversified past cash relief into a prevention/training product line that now defines its shape.1911

01 — Profile

Founded by the industry's plumbing, not its stars2

Support Act serves musicians, managers, crew and music workers across all genres who are unable to work due to ill health, injury, mental-health crisis or other crisis; the Wellbeing Helpline is open more broadly to creative-industries workers.1

The founding pattern differs from every other org in this series. MusiCares was created by the Recording Academy; Music Minds Matter was incubated inside a century-old benevolent fund; Unison was seeded by a trade body plus a benefactor family. Support Act was lobbied into existence over 17 years and founded with the backing of the three bodies that administer the Australian industry's money: ARIA (labels), PPCA (recorded-music licensing) and APRA AMCOS (songwriters' collecting society).2 The collecting-society route matters for Taiwan: it anchors the charity to recurring industry revenue streams and to institutions with member lists, rather than to any single company's goodwill.

02 — The distinctive model

Relief + helpline + a prevention product line

Support Act runs three service layers, and the third is what sets it apart.345

LAYER 01

Crisis relief, means-tested and specific

Crisis Relief Grants, Funeral Help, and Tax Help — the latter two being unusually concrete acknowledgments of how music-worker hardship actually presents (a death the family cannot fund; years of unfiled tax returns blocking access to benefits).3

LAYER 02

A 24/7 helpline plus social work

The Wellbeing Helpline (1800 959 500) provides free 24/7 phone counselling, extended beyond music to all creative-industries workers, backed by an in-house social work support service — a case-management layer between the phone line and the grant.4

LAYER 03

A named, branded prevention/training suite

REWIRE, LEAD WELL, Speak Up Safely, Hearing the Hard Stuff, De-escalation, Mental Health First Aid, Recognise and Respond (suicide-prevention training), Money Moves, MINIs, Creative Minds, Sound Minds, and Backstage Mental Health Support — plus a workplace layer: the Mentally Healthy Workplaces Program and published "Minimum Standards for a Mentally Healthy Music Industry." The audited accounts show why it matters financially: training programs attract program-tied state government grants (Creative Victoria, Sound NSW, South Australian Government, Creative Workplaces/Creative Australia — across the FY2024–FY2025 filings), giving the org a fundable product between "donate to charity" and "pay for crisis relief."57910

LAYER 04

A dedicated First Nations stream

Parallel First Nations crisis relief, Funeral Help and Tax Help, a First Nations Support Line, First Nations Mental Health First Aid, cultural programs (Voices Amplified, Yarning Strong) and a published First Nations strategic plan, with named program partners (JB Hi-Fi, Alberts). This is population-specific service design inside a music-specific charity — directly relevant to how a Taiwan org would need to treat, for example, Indigenous Taiwanese musicians.6

Three readings (ratios computed, not quoted):9

  • The COVID tail is visible line by line. FY2023's $2.35M in grants paid — against only $3.76M revenue — produced a $1.39M deficit as the org spent down the remainder of COVID-era funding; the Directors' Report attributes the deficit to exactly this wind-down, with the funding "fully expended" by FY2024. By FY2025 grants paid had normalized to $674,915 — a −71% fall from FY2023 — and the org returned a $799,561 surplus.
  • Government money is real but volatile and program-tied. The grants line swings between 26% and 37% of revenue across the three years, and the named funders across the FY2024–FY2025 filings are state cultural agencies funding specific programs (Creative Victoria and Sound NSW in FY2025; the South Australian Government grant appears in the FY2024 filing) — not recurrent federal core funding.910
  • Reserves ≈ 14 months of expenses, no endowment. Net assets of $4.66M against $3.94M annual expenses (≈1.18×, computed) is a working buffer, not an income-producing endowment: like Unison and unlike Help Musicians, Support Act must re-raise its base every year.

Key management personnel compensation was $225,025 (FY2023), $236,298 (FY2024), $233,530 (FY2025) — lean for a national charity.910

04 — The windfall-and-cliff

A$30M in, then zero111213

Support Act is the cleanest documented case in this series of what emergency government money does to a charity.

  • Before 2020: no recurring government funding; industry- and fundraising-dependent (⚠️ as-reported in appeal-period coverage).13
  • April 2020: the Australian Government announced A$10M for Support Act as part of its COVID-19 response.11
  • August 2021: a further A$20M (within a A$35M arts package), with eligibility temporarily widened beyond music to theatre, dance and circus workers. By that announcement, the org had processed 4,506 crisis relief grants and delivered 1,700+ hours of counselling cumulatively.12
  • January 2022: the funding exhausted, Support Act launched an emergency public appeal, CEO Clive Miller describing the org as under "enormous pressure" as demand persisted after the money ended.13

The audited accounts then show the landing (§3): two deficit years absorbing the tail of elevated demand, reserves drawn down from $5.6M (implied FY2022 level, computed from FY2023 opening) to $3.86M, then a rebuilt surplus in FY2025 on diversified revenue — record fundraising plus a return of state-level program grants. The org survived the cliff because it had reserves and a fundraising machine that predated the windfall.9

06 — Governance

Co-chaired, industry-embedded8

Leadership is industry-embedded and, notably, co-chaired: Lynne Small and David Albert share the chair, with CEO Clive Miller (in post since before the 2020 COVID response) and CFO Lisa Smith leading a listed staff of ~16 across crisis relief, social work, helpline, First Nations programs, prevention training and fundraising. The full board and staff roster is published with names and roles on the org's Our People page (snapshot 2026-07-06); a roster is a point-in-time fact.8

Two signals stand out. The co-chair structure spreads governance legitimacy across two industry constituencies rather than concentrating it. And the staff list shows dedicated First Nations roles — the population-specific stream is staffed, not just branded.8

07 — Strengths

What works

The only full-scale APAC precedent

Support Act proves a national, music-specific charity can reach maturity in Asia-Pacific — sustaining a 24/7 helpline, cash relief and a training arm for nearly three decades in a mid-sized market.12

Audited transparency

Complete KPMG-audited statutory accounts published on its own site — the strongest financial transparency in this series, and the reason this study could quote line items rather than org-stated totals.910

A fundable prevention product line

Branded training programs give state cultural agencies a concrete thing to fund (Creative Victoria, Sound NSW and the South Australian Government all appear as program funders across the FY2024–FY2025 filings), diversifying revenue beyond donations and crisis appeals.5910

Trade-body founding anchors recurring industry money

ARIA/PPCA/APRA AMCOS backing ties the charity to the industry's permanent institutions and their member rolls, not to any single company — and the donor base layered on top spans state governments, corporate partners (AusMusic T-Shirt Day with ARIA, Triple J, Live Performance Australia), a dedicated crew fund (CrewCare) and individual giving.214

Windfall discipline, demonstrated

A$30M of emergency federal money was absorbed, spent to purpose, and its withdrawal survived — with the deficits, reserve draw-down and recovery documented in audited statements rather than smoothed over.9111213

Population-specific design inside a music charity

The First Nations stream (own support line, own grants, own strategic plan, staffed roles) is the series' clearest example of equity-by-design.6

08 — Weaknesses & risks

What to watch

Government money is program-tied and volatile

The grants line swung 26%→37% of revenue in three years, and it funds programs, not core operations; a state agency's priorities can reshape the service mix.9

No endowment — perpetual re-raising

Reserves of ~14 months' expenses (computed) buffer shocks but generate no income; like Unison, the base must be rebuilt annually.9

Crisis-relief capacity has structurally shrunk

Grants paid fell from $2.35M (FY2023) to $0.67M (FY2025). The org-preferred reading is normalization after COVID; the risk reading is that relief capacity is now below post-COVID demand — the January 2022 appeal suggested demand did not fall with the funding.913

Helpline delivery model not fully visible

The accounts show a wellbeing-services line of ~$0.7M/yr but the delivery contract (in-house vs. outsourced clinical provider) is not itemized in what was retrieved; the Unison-style vendor-dependency question cannot be assessed this pass. ❓49

The ACNC register itself was not readable this pass

All financials here come from the org's own audited PDFs; the regulator's data view (AIS) is JS-gated to fetch tools. The audited accounts are the stronger source anyway, but the registry cross-check is pending.15

Single-country scope

Like every peer in this set, Support Act serves one market; its existence does not close the gap in the rest of Asia-Pacific.1

09 — Lessons for Taiwan

What a Taiwan music mental health NPO should take

  1. Found through the collecting societies and trade bodies

    Support Act's ARIA/PPCA/APRA AMCOS founding is the closest available template for Taiwan: seed the org through MÜST/RPAT-type collecting societies and industry associations, which hold both recurring revenue and member lists — rather than waiting for a single benefactor or label.2

  2. Build a prevention/training product line early — it is what governments will actually fund

    Taiwan's 文化部/文策院 fund programs and output, not welfare (a finding repeated across this project's policy studies). Support Act shows the workaround: package prevention as named, deliverable training products (MHFA, suicide-prevention training, workplace standards) that a cultural agency can grant against, while donations fund the cash relief that grants won't touch.59

  3. Treat any government windfall as a program, never as the base

    The A$30M COVID episode is the controlled experiment: the org that survived it was the one with a pre-existing fundraising machine and reserves. Plan the Taiwan org's core budget on industry + public fundraising, and absorb government surges as time-boxed programs with an explicit wind-down plan.9111213

  4. Publish audited accounts from year one

    Support Act's KPMG-audited statements are why this study could be precise where the Unison study had to flag ❓. In a low-trust founding environment (a new NPO in a market with no precedent), audited transparency is the cheapest credibility available.9

  5. Fund funerals and tax help, not just "counselling"

    The concreteness of Support Act's relief menu — Funeral Help, Tax Help — reflects how hardship actually presents and gives donors legible things to fund. A Taiwan org should map the equivalent concrete failure points (健保 arrears, 勞保 gaps, funeral costs) rather than offering only generic aid.3

  6. Design population-specific streams from the start

    The First Nations stream — own line, own grants, own plan, staffed — is the template for how a Taiwan org should structure service for Indigenous musicians and other distinct populations: parallel and staffed, not an afterthought paragraph.6

  7. Reserves target: ~12 months of expenses

    Support Act entered the COVID cliff with reserves and exited solvent; ~1.2× annual expenses (computed) is a defensible working target for a young org that cannot build an endowment.9
