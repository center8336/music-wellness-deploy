Summary

What the paper set out to do, and found

Rousseau and colleagues set out to map — for the first time, they argue — what is known about the physical and mental health of metal musicians, a population long crowded out of musicians'-health research by classical players.1

Two questions drive the review: the prevalence of physical and mental health disorders among these musicians, and the main risk factors. Expecting thin, scattered evidence, the authors chose a scoping rather than a systematic review (Arksey & O'Malley / JBI / PRISMA-ScR) — which carries one consequence worth stating up front: no methodological quality appraisal was performed, by design.2 They searched five databases plus grey literature in four languages, explicitly excluding studies of metal listeners, and included 47 studies across six domains: mental health (12), hearing (10), musculoskeletal/neurological (9), vocal (9), mortality (4) and "other" (3).2

The findings are a catalogue of genre-tinted risks rather than firm rates. Hearing loss and tinnitus are common and tend to exceed classical musicians', with one Norwegian survey putting hearing loss at 37.8%, singers worst affected.4 The review's signature physical hazard is headbanging, linked in case reports to everything from cervical strain to vertebral-artery aneurysm and traumatic brain injury, with a biomechanical model predicting injury once neck excursions pass ~75° at typical tempos.5 The most counter-intuitive result concerns the voice: extreme techniques (growling, screaming, distortion) were not shown to damage the larynx in cross-sectional work, and supraglottic phonation may even be protective — a "vocal resilience."6 On mental health the picture is heterogeneous: elevated alcohol use, and one study of touring professionals reporting depressive predisposition (50% vs 4%) and suicidality (40% vs 8%) far above the general population, alongside biographical case studies the authors are careful not to over-read.7 Four mortality studies report higher-than-population death rates among rock/pop musicians, elevated in women and from cancer.8 The authors conclude that metal musicians share most stressors with other musicians but face genuinely distinctive demands, and that the literature is dominated by case reports and small cross-sections, with a "clear lack of longitudinal and prevalence studies."1

Six buckets of risk

Mental health

Elevated alcohol use; touring professionals show 50% depressive predisposition (vs 4%) and 40% suicidality (vs 8%). Biographical cases handled with deliberate caution.7

Hearing

Loss and tinnitus common and exceeding classical players; 37.8% hearing loss in one survey, ~22% in others; tinnitus the most reported complaint.4

Musculoskeletal / neuro

9 studies

~68% lifetime in drummers, ~61% past-year in guitarists; headbanging → cervical strain to vertebral-artery aneurysm / TBI (case reports); injury modelled past ~75° neck movement.5

Vocal

9 studies

Vocal resilience: growl/scream not shown to damage the larynx cross-sectionally; supraglottic phonation may protect. 12.2% dysphonia, none in full-time or formally-trained singers.6

Mortality

4 studies

Higher-than-population death rates in rock/pop stars (SMR 1.35), elevated in women and from cancer — but built on fame-based cohorts (album lists, Wikipedia).8

Other

3 studies

Skin conditions ("drummer's digit", "guitar nipple"); metal drumming rates as vigorous exercise (VO₂ up to 90% of max). The thinnest, most scattered bucket.2

Comment

How to read it — and present it

This is a useful and likeably ambitious paper: the first to gather a genuinely scattered literature, it treats musicians as workers and athletes, picks the right tool for an immature evidence base, and is admirably careful not to pathologise the metal subculture. For arguing that musicians' health is a serious, under-served problem, it is a rich single entry point. Several things, though, should shape how you take it.

  1. The title says "metal"; the evidence is mostly rock

    The population was deliberately widened to metal, rock and punk because metal-only studies are so few, and much of what is cited — the hearing-loss cohorts, every mortality study — is rock/pop, with metal a subset at best.1 A defensible design choice that quietly inflates the apparent metal evidence base. When presenting, separate the genuinely metal-specific findings (headbanging injury, growl/scream resilience, the few metal-singer and metal-drummer studies) from those borrowed from rock/pop. The honest title is "metal and rock musicians."

  2. It cannot really answer its first question

    Research question 1 asks for prevalence, but the authors concede the corpus is "mostly case reports, case series, or small cross-sectional studies" with no longitudinal or true-prevalence designs — and, being a scoping review, they appraised none of it for quality.2 So the headline percentages (37.8%, 68%, 50%, 40%) are not prevalence estimates; they are single numbers from heterogeneous, mostly non-metal samples. Quote any one as "the rate" and you mislead. Lead with the range and the heterogeneity.

  3. The screening numbers don't reconcile

    The narrative reports 5,182 references identified and 3,550 screened after de-duplication; the PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 2) reports 5,484 identified, 1,934 duplicates and 3,320 excluded.3 The "47 included" is stable, but for a paper whose value rests on transparent, reproducible mapping, a funnel that disagrees with its own figure is a real blemish — and the authors admit the protocol "was not fully registered." Don't over-lean on the search counts.

  4. Mortality is the thinnest story — and the most quotable

    It rests on just four studies, built on an "All-Time Top 1,000 Albums" list and a Wikipedia-derived cohort.8 Those sample famous musicians, so fame, selection and survivorship bias are baked in; the people studied are rock/pop stars, not working metal musicians, and the designs cannot speak to causation. "Elevated mortality, especially in women and from cancer" is exactly the line an audience will remember and exactly the one to caveat hardest.

  5. The genuine novelty is "vocal resilience," not the risk list

    That extreme vocal techniques appear not to damage the larynx — and may protect it via supraglottic phonation — is counter-intuitive, metal-specific, and the most original thing here.6 Foreground it. By contrast the risk factors the review consolidates (loud volume → hearing loss, repetition → injury, touring → stress) have been known for years; the contribution there is a tidy map, not a discovery. Frame it that way and the paper is stronger, not weaker.

  6. Credit on stigma — but keep anecdote and data apart

    The authors explicitly warn against "simplistic associations between extreme music genres and psychopathology" and cleanly exclude fans from musicians, which is the right instinct.7 Yet the mental-health section still interleaves biographical case studies (Per "Dead" Ohlin's posthumous Cotard's syndrome; Kurt Cobain read through a "Werther effect" lens) with small empirical studies. For a reader those belong in separate buckets — colourful illustration on one side, measured evidence on the other — or the section sounds more conclusive than it is.

For this project

The review is a clean "problem-size" citation: it establishes musicians as an occupational population with real, under-managed physical and mental risk, and documents a genuine evidence gap (no longitudinal or prevalence studies; several domains barely touched). It pairs naturally with the Cerdá-Díaz musculoskeletal umbrella review already in our wiki — together they let us say the burden is large and the evidence is immature.

But it offers nothing Asia- or Taiwan-specific, nothing on the structure of the music economy or access to care, and despite "well-being" in the title it barely touches social or financial well-being — precisely where an NPO service-model angle would live. A breadth and framing citation, and a tidy model of scoping methodology; not evidence for what an intervention should do.

A careful and welcome first map whose real message is the shape of the evidence gap. Read it as an agenda-setter, foreground the metal-specific findings, and discount the rock-star mortality headline.
